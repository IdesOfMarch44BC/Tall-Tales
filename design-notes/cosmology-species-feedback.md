# Tall Tales — Cosmology, Boundary Realms, and Species: Feedback and the Connection Map

Third document in the review series. Part One covers the cosmology and boundary realms, Part Two the seventeen species, and Part Three is the piece this whole exercise has been building toward: the connection map — the specific welds between cosmology, species, regions, and factions that will turn four parallel documents into one setting. Part Four updates the master gap list.

Per your note, this ignores class references and all species stat blocks — though a few *prose* items in the species files are flagged because they're lore text, not mechanics.

---

## Part One: Cosmology and the Boundary Realms

### cosmology.md

**What's working.** The spinning top is a genuinely good structural metaphor, and its best property is the one stated up front: the structure is only stable *in motion*. That's not just imagery — it's a doomsday switch. "Stop the spin and everything collapses" is a campaign-ending threat sitting in the first paragraph, and something in the setting should eventually want to stop the spin. The Heavens/Shadow asymmetry is a classic executed with conviction: evil wins short-term because it will do anything, loses long-term because it can't trust itself, and the Heavens' knowledge of their own self-imposed limits "sits in them like a wound that will not close." That wound is the Lions' entire internal politics in cosmological miniature — the hard faction is what happens when mortals decide the Heavens' restraint is a luxury they can't afford. That resonance should be made explicit somewhere.

**The buried lede.** The Wyrd paragraph ends with this: *"the most learned of scholars may know that interaction with the Fey was the first source of magic, but none remember what the cost of the deal was."* That is a setting-defining secret delivered in one subordinate clause — and it collides productively with the single most important open question in the faction chapters: the Enclaves' inability to answer where arcane magic comes from. As written, the two documents don't know about each other. Decide the relationship deliberately, because every option is good: (a) the Fey deal *is* the answer, the Enclaves suspect it, and their silence is not ignorance but dread; (b) it's the leading theory among several, and Enclaves factionalize over it; (c) arcane magic is something else entirely and the Fey-origin story is itself Fey propaganda — they grow stronger on legends, after all, including legends about themselves. Whichever you pick, the unremembered *cost* is the campaign hook: a debt nobody remembers is a debt that can be called in.

**Issues to resolve.**
- **"The Machine" needs a decision.** The Material World is "the realm of the Machine," capitalized, and then personified: "The Machine, for its part, would prefer that both of them mind their own business." Is the Machine an entity, a genius loci of the Material World, a philosophical shorthand, or the Wyr's vanished Makers wearing a different name? Any of these is interesting; ambiguity-by-accident is not. If it's deliberate mystery, give it one more sentence acknowledging that people disagree about whether the Machine is a metaphor — that converts an editing question into lore.
- **"The one place where the Wyrd does not directly intrude" contradicts the regions doc**, where Wyrd-leakage zones (the Glass Plains, the Aurora Strait, the whole Frozen Coast) are established features of the Material World. The reconciliation is probably "the Wyrd cannot intrude directly — leakage happens only at boundary-realm thin points, which is precisely why those sites matter so much" — but that needs to be said, because as written the two documents flatly disagree.
- **The Wild gets the thinnest paragraph of the five planes** — four sentences for the setting's structural antagonist, versus full portraits for Heavens and Shadow. It also leaves the naming question open: is "the Wild" the plane, the material wilderness beyond the rails, or both? The frontier documents use it to mean the wilderness; the cosmology uses it to mean a plane. The likely doctrine — the material wilderness is where the Wild plane bleeds through, and Beast Lords are that bleed given sovereignty — would explain enormous amounts (why the Wild "fights back" coherently, why its rulers command elemental forces) and should be written down.
- **The iron question now has a possible answer.** The premise doc says cold iron is "an inherent weakness" for many Wild rulers; classical folklore assigns that weakness to fey; the cosmology says the Wild merges with the Wyrd to form "new and novel types of existence." If Beast Lords carry Wyrd heritage, the iron weakness has a cosmological mechanism — and the premise doc's stray "fey-aligned forces sabotage rails" line stops being a fossil and becomes a clue. Recommend making this braid deliberate.
- **The vim-origin claim is excellent and orphaned.** The Realm of Electricity passage — vim may be a "domesticated echo" of the Heavens' boundary, and "the Shadow disputes this claim bitterly, knowing what it would mean if it were true" — is a first-rate hook that ties the entire technology base to theology. No faction document reacts to it. The Lions *must* have positions on this (does the hard faction think Constructive Hearts are stolen divinity? does the moderate faction cite it to defend industry?), and Tinkerers should have opinions too.
- Copy edits: "the grow stronger of the legends and myths" → "they grow stronger on the legends and myths"; a few agreement slips in the Wyrd paragraph generally, which is rougher than the rest of the file.

### planes-boundary.md

**What's working.** The scheme is elegant — elements×elements, elements×(Heavens/Shadow), elements×(Dream/Nightmare), plus the two polar realms — and the prose is the best in the packet. The Realm of Tar ("has never lost anything. That is not entirely a comfort"), Fiddler's Green resisting corruption "with a stubbornness that seems almost personal," and the Unending Dark's closing move — the Shadow doesn't know whether its own nihilism is true, "it has simply learned that saying so is often enough" — are all superb. Each realm has a *personality*, not just a palette, and several are arguments made physical (Ash as the Shadow's quiet case that even fire ends). This file needs the least revision of anything submitted.

**Structural questions — decide whether these are gaps or geometry.**
- **Two elemental pairings are missing:** Air–Earth and Fire–Water. Every other scheme in the file is exhaustive, so the absence reads as an oversight — unless it's the top's geometry: if the four elements sit around the rim in adjacency order (Air–Fire–Earth–Water), then opposite elements never touch, and no boundary realm can form between them. That's a genuinely elegant explanation and it costs one sentence to state. Alternatively, the two missing realms exist and are *wrong* somehow — realms that shouldn't be able to form and did. Either way, don't leave it silent; completionist readers will count.
- **Two polar cross-pairings are missing:** Heavens×Nightmare and Shadow×Dream. This asymmetry is more interesting than the elemental one. What *is* the Heavens' nightmare? What does the Shadow dream of? If these realms deliberately don't exist, lampshade it ("no realm forms where the Heavens meet the Nightmare, and the scholars who ask why do not like any of their answers"). If they do exist, they are the two most dangerous ideas in the cosmology — the Heavens' fear given geography, the Shadow's longing given geography — and probably belong behind a mystery rather than a full entry. Strongly recommend addressing rather than omitting.
- **Anchor each realm to the Material World.** Several boundary realms already have established leakage sites in the regions doc — Ash → the Ash Quarry, the Brilliant Aurora → the Aurora Strait, Sea of Clouds and Endless Plummet → the High Cradle's deep valleys, Smoke → the Smoking Mother's territory. A one-line "where it touches the world" note per realm would weld this file to the regions file and immediately generate adventure geography for the realms that don't yet have a site (where does Tar leak? — that's a campaign).
- **Travel.** The cosmology says the Wyrd "is often used in any form of supernatural travel or communication," and the boundary realms are described as traversable ("armies have been lost in the Realm of Smoke"). Nothing says how anyone gets in or out. Even a paragraph of doctrine — thin points, ritual crossings, what the Blade-Bound's spatial cuts pass *through* — would make the realms usable rather than decorative.

## Part Two: The Species

**The pattern works.** Every entry follows the same quiet skeleton — distinct silhouette, cultural thesis, internal tension, and *why adventurers leave* — and that last element is exactly right for a game document; it means every species ships with built-in character motivations. The best entries have real emotional cores: the Korr are the standout (short lives spent deciding whether witness or experience honors limited time — "height is epistemology" is a phrase worth building a culture on, and "The elders watch them go. They remember every one" is the best closing line in the packet). The Dóm (grace as obligation, excellence as a trap), the Squar (a people afraid of surviving so well they're erased), and the Nol (the mask that was a prayer becoming a performance) are close behind. The Nol entry in particular does something sophisticated: it's a playable deconstruction of the "monstrous humanoid" trope with a genuine internal argument between generations.

**Cross-cutting species notes.**

- **SRD fossils in the prose.** Stat blocks aside, several *lore* passages retain find-and-replace escapees: the Brindel entry says "goblin territory," "goblins see intricate beauty," and "brindel-kind/goblins" interchangeably (three instances); the Dóm traits reference "a domovoi"; the Gorûn traits "an ogre"; the Tikka traits "a kobold"; and the Dóm/Wyr size lines say "Serekh." You're overhauling traits anyway, but the Brindel body-text instances are worldbuilding prose and need the pass regardless. (The Lemari being "mistaken for elves out of habit" is clearly deliberate and is a good wink — keep that one, and keep the discipline it implies: no legacy species, only echoes of them.)
- **The Human entry is the weakest by a wide margin.** Every other species gets a thesis and a tension; humans get generic endurance-runner anthropology that could be pasted into any setting. In *this* setting, humans deserve a specific angle, and the material is already lying around: the faction system replaces nationality — are humans the species most defined by institutional identity because they have the least biological one? The expansion the Wild resists is overwhelmingly human-driven rails and human-founded Companies — is the civilization/Wild war, from the Wild's perspective, substantially a *human* war? Are humans overrepresented among the unaffiliated majority, the Trade Companies, the Lions? Give humans the same treatment as everyone else: a thesis (restlessness as the engine of the frontier), a tension (the species that builds institutions fastest and outgrows them fastest), and a reason the Wild might name them specifically.
- **Mairin and Pelorim share the same taboo** — deep attachment as a threat to community — with different rationales (grief-ripples vs. route-forgetting). Read together they feel like one idea twice. Two options: differentiate harder, or make the convergence canon — two plains cultures independently arriving at attachment-wariness is the kind of pattern in-world scholars would argue about, and it quietly implies something about life on the plains (or about what the plains have lost) that could be a genuine mystery. The second option is cheaper and more interesting.
- **The Silvaen are sitting on the setting's central fault line and nobody has noticed.** They are plant-people grown by mimicry, rooted to homelands, who call themselves "wardens, not conquerors" — a playable species that is arguably *of the Wild* rather than of civilization. Do the Beast Lords consider Silvaen their own? Do settlements? Do the Frontier Wardens treat a Silvaen as a colleague, a contact, or a subject? A Silvaen PC walks around embodying the question the whole setting is about, and the entry doesn't engage with it. One paragraph fixes this and creates the most politically interesting species in the roster. (Also: the Silvaen appear in no region — the Verdant Belt lists Brindel and Lemari only. Anchor them.)
- **Biome-to-region mapping is clean and should be stated.** Arctic → Frozen Coast, desert → Salt Reach, forest → Verdant Belt, mountain → High Cradle, plains → Iron Reach. The species docs and the regions doc match almost perfectly (Serekh Cisterns, Gorûn routes, Chyrr Watchposts, Uln tunnel-cities, Dóm couriers, Brindel Warrens, Lemari Canopy, Squar Hidden Paths all appear in both). This is the best-executed weld in the entire packet — it just needs a sentence somewhere acknowledging the scheme, plus anchors for the unanchored (Silvaen, Nol, Barudrim, Mairin).
- **Small consistency gem worth keeping:** the Wyr think best in cold — and the Northwatch Enclave's director, Halvi Snow, is a Wyr running a research Enclave on the Frozen Coast. That's the kind of quiet cross-document coherence the rest of the setting should imitate.

## Part Three: The Connection Map

This is the braid — the specific welds, in rough priority order, that connect the four layers (cosmology / species / regions / factions). Most cost a sentence or a paragraph each. Collectively they're the difference between a stack of documents and a world.

### Cosmology ↔ Factions

1. **The Fey deal ↔ the Enclaves' unanswered question.** Covered above; the single most important weld in the setting. Whatever the truth, the Enclaves' critics ("they consider it a confession") and the Lions' hard faction gain enormous substance the moment the Fey-origin story is in circulation.
2. **"Architects of Faustian bargains" ↔ the Contractors.** The Shadow's description explicitly houses the bargain-makers — which means at least some Contractor counterparties are Shadow-native, and the Lions' theological objection to the Houses stops being abstract. But surely not all: the cosmology offers four other sources (Fey, celestials, elemental sovereigns of the Wild, and whatever inhabits the boundary realms). A taxonomy of contracting entities *by plane of origin* belongs in the Noble Houses rewrite, and it instantly stratifies the Houses politically — a House whose ancestral pacts are with elemental sovereigns is a different diplomatic animal than one bound into the Shadow, and the Lions will treat them differently.
3. **Vim's disputed origin ↔ the Lions' theology of technology.** If vim may be domesticated Heaven, the Lions need doctrine about industry — and the hard/moderate split gets a new front: is a Constructive Heart a small miracle or a theft? Tinkerers, meanwhile, are either engineers or unlicensed theologians depending on who's asked.
4. **The Heavens' self-imposed limits ↔ the hard Lions.** "Evil is willing to do what good is not" is the wound; the hard faction is the mortal argument that the wound should be cauterized. Making the hard faction's theology an explicit (heretical?) answer to this cosmological fact elevates them from "the strict ones" to a genuine position in the oldest argument in existence.
5. **The Blade-Bound's homeless power ↔ the plane that does not dream.** The Orders' power is canonically "not arcane, not divine, not psionic." The cosmology now offers a beautiful answer: the Material World is the *one* plane the Wyrd doesn't reach — and trained will could be the Material World's own indigenous supernatural tradition, the Machine's answer to magic. This would explain why nobody's frameworks fit, give the Orders a cosmological identity that is quietly enormous, and make the Lions' uncertainty about them *correct* in a way no one suspects. Even if you don't canonize it, it should exist as an in-world theory.
6. **The spinning top's fragility ↔ somebody's agenda.** Stability-through-motion implies a faction, cult, or entity that wants the spin stopped — the setting's eventual apocalypse plot is already licensed by paragraph one of the cosmology. Reserve it.

### Cosmology ↔ Species

7. **The Nol's "malign spirits" ↔ the Nightmare.** The masks were made against "predators of the unseen world that hunted by familiarity" — that is a Nightmare-entity description to the letter. Making the Nol mask tradition an ancient folk defense against Wyrd predation gives a playable species a working relationship with the cosmology and makes the Nol quietly indispensable anywhere the Nightmare leaks (the Lions on the Frozen Coast should be *recruiting* them).
8. **The Wyr's Makers ↔ the Machine ↔ the Frozen Fortress.** Three dangling threads of the same color: an artificial people with vanished creators, a personified Material World "of the Machine," and an abandoned humanoid-built installation that no living tradition claims. They may braid or they may not — but the *question* of whether they braid should be asked in-world (a Wyr scholar's heresy: the Makers didn't leave; they became the Machine — or built it, or fled it). The Buried Maker-Sites on the Frozen Coast plus the Frozen Fortress in the High Cradle give the question geography.
9. **The Korr ↔ the Witnesses of the Lion.** Two cultures independently organized around witness — the Korr watching from above, the Lions refusing to look away up close. This is either kinship or critique: Korr elders would say the Lions trade sight for proximity; the Lions would say witness without intervention is spectation. Canonize the relationship either way — Korr overrepresented among Circuit Riders, or a famous Korr Lion whose choice to descend scandalized the cliffs, or a standing philosophical argument between an Order of elders and a Lions chapter. This is the richest species-faction pairing available and it's currently coincidence.
10. **The Dóm ↔ the Circuit Riders.** Two courier traditions overlap on the Frozen Coast — one carries messages as aesthetic-moral obligation, the other carries witness as ministry. Friction, mutual respect, or a formal courier compact between Iceharbor's Lions chapter and the Dóm would texture the whole region.

### Species ↔ Factions (the alignment layer)

11. **Write the war-alignment table.** The civilization/Wild conflict is the setting's spine, and the species roster quietly distributes across it: rail-integrated (Human, Barudrim, Mairin, Tikka-as-engineers, Uln trade, Wyr), treaty-holding neutrals (Pelorim — whose Lions treaty *keeps Companies out*, Gorûn, Serekh, Korr, Chyrr, Dóm, Squar), Wild-embedded (Brindel, Lemari, Silvaen). That's not flavor — that's the political map of the setting, currently implicit. One table plus a paragraph per ambiguous case makes it explicit, and it reframes the "frontier": expansion isn't pushing into empty hostility, it's pushing through a lattice of *other peoples' treaties* — which is far more interesting and generates diplomatic adventures rather than just combat ones.
12. **Faction demographics.** Which species fill which factions? The notable-factions doc already models this well (a Tikka engineer running Vault & Coil's conversion works, a Nol commanding the Pale Riders, a Barudrim general, a Lemari and a Wyr among Enclave leadership). The faction rewrites should each carry a line or two: Chyrr strike-first doctrine grinding against Guild Enforcer restraint; Uln cold-forging as a Tinkerer specialty the Guild prizes; Squar as almost-never-Wardens-because-the-Wardens-would-love-them-and-they-know-it; Gorûn as the Mercenary Companies' most sought and most quietly resentful recruits.

### Species ↔ Cosmology's threats

13. **Homeland-bound species vs. the leakage map.** The Silvaen weaken away from home; the Lemari are bound to trees; the Mairin to settlements. Now overlay the leakage zones: what happens to a Silvaen whose homeland becomes a Shadow-leakage zone? To a Lemari clan whose great tree stands where the Wyrd thins? These species have *hostages* in the cosmological war, which makes them motivated parties, not bystanders — and makes "the region that shaped you is corrupting" one of the best personal-stakes hooks in the setting.

## Part Four: Updated Master Gap List

Resolved by this batch: the cosmology primer (was gap #2), the religion question is half-resolved (gods, demigods, and angels canonically exist and work — but none are named, and Leo's nature is still undefined; the pantheon is now the gap), and the species roster (which wasn't even on the list because previous documents only implied it).

**Still open, in priority order:**
1. **The Timeline.** Still the critical path; still referenced ("Second Boundary War") and still absent. Everything in Parts One–Three above wants dates.
2. **The pantheon and Leo.** The Heavens are populated and organized — by whom? Even three named gods and a decision about what Leo *is* (god, demigod, celestial, ascended mortal?) unblocks the Lions rewrite.
3. **Threats bestiary/roster.** Now easier: the cosmology provides the taxonomy for free (Shadow-native, Nightmare-native, Wild sovereigns, boundary-realm entities, Fey). Named antagonists per category is what's missing.
4. **The reconciliation pass:** the contradictions and fossils flagged across all three reviews (Wyrd-intrusion doctrine, "fey-aligned" terminology, the Machine decision, Champion-of-the-East, goblin/domovoi/ogre/kobold/Serekh strays, energy units).
5. **Faction chapter rewrites** to the six-part template — now with Parts One–Three of this document as the source of each chapter's cosmological and species material.
6. Currency/scrip, faction texture passes, the map — unchanged from the previous review.

**Revised work order suggestion:** Timeline → pantheon/Leo decision → reconciliation pass → one proof-of-concept faction chapter (the Lions remain the best candidate — they now touch the pantheon, the vim question, the Korr, the Dóm, the hard-faction theology, and the Nightmare all at once) → remaining factions → threats roster.

The striking thing about this batch is how little it *conflicts* with what came before — one real contradiction (Wyrd intrusion), a handful of fossils, and otherwise the layers already fit. The setting doesn't need more invention. It needs about forty deliberate sentences placed at the seams — and most of them are listed above.
