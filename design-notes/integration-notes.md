# Tall Tales — Integration Notes: Premise, Regions, and the Old Subfaction Material

A follow-up to the faction lore feedback, covering three things: how the new documents change the earlier critique, feedback on the three new documents themselves, and a concrete salvage-and-integration plan for the old notable-factions material.

The headline finding: **the concrete layer I said was missing mostly exists — it just lives in a different part of the book and never touches the faction chapters.** The faction chapters are all pattern and no proper noun; the regions and notable-factions documents are all proper noun and lean on the patterns. Neither references the other. Thirstgate hosts the Lions' mortal-Banneret operations, a major Enclave, a Guild headquarters, and a central Registry — and none of the four relevant faction chapters mentions Thirstgate once. The rewrite's core job is now clearer and smaller than my first review implied: not "invent the concrete layer" but **weld the two layers together and fill the remaining gaps.**

---

## Part One: The Earlier Feedback, Rescored

Going down the seven cross-cutting notes from the first review in light of the new material:

**1. Proper nouns — largely answered, wrongly shelved.** The setting has named cities, Companies, Enclaves, Houses, Mercenary Companies, Beast Lords, and NPCs, many of them good. The fix changes from "invent exemplars" to "distribute them": each faction chapter's new Named Exemplars section should be seeded from the regions/notable-factions material (updated to current lore — see Part Three), with the notable-factions chapter surviving as a cross-referenced compendium rather than the sole home of specificity. The faction chapters should *use* these names in their prose, not merely link to them — the Guilds chapter's Banking Concern threat should say "Argent Line," because that is who it is.

**2. History/timeline — confirmed to exist (or be planned), still not uploaded.** The regions doc references "the Second Boundary War (see Timeline)." If a Timeline chapter exists, it is now the single highest-leverage document in the book: the faction chapters' vague "generations of hard experience" can all be pinned to its events. If it doesn't exist yet, the Second Boundary War is the seed — a named war implies a First, implies parties, implies unresolved consequences (the Rust Belt is already one). Recommendation stands: 5–10 events, each with a living consequence.

**3. Everything works too well — partially answered.** The new material contains genuine live fault lines: the Lost House mystery (Talvecq), the Rust Belt reclamation question, the Vault & Coil / Chyrr tension "that has not yet escalated to action but easily could," Velgrim's expected retirement, the Fadrick-as-hardliner-successor threat hanging over Vairo, Crossbow Holdings' internal alignment pressure, the Glass Plains leakage. These are exactly the "Current Fault Lines" material the faction chapters need — most of them just need to be *claimed* by the relevant faction chapter and sharpened from "could escalate" to "is escalating as the campaign opens." The factions still need a few internal ruptures the new docs don't supply (the Lions succession is now half-built via Vairo/Fadrick; the Guild neutrality crisis is half-built via Argent Line; the Orders still have nothing).

**4. Street-level entry points — substantially answered at the region level.** Every region has a "Suggested starting situation" block, and they're good. The remaining gap is faction-side: the region hooks tell you where a party starts; the faction chapters still need to say what each institution *pays a party to do*. These should cross-reference (the Wardens chapter's mission list should include "confirm what's living in the Rust Belt," pointing at the Iron Reach section).

**5. Sensory texture — still open.** The new documents are stronger on texture than the faction chapters (Lastlight "visibly nervous in the specific way of communities that have heard things in the trees the night before" is exactly the register the faction chapters need), but iconography, ritual, dress, slang, and recognition signals remain absent everywhere. Unchanged recommendation.

**6. Missing subject matter — mostly answered, with survivors.**
- *The guns:* answered at the systems level, and well — the firearms/ammunition-logistics/imbued-melee triangle in the premise doc is a genuinely elegant piece of design (see Part Two). What remains is cultural: the faction chapters should now *show* this economy. Tinkerers presumably manufacture firearms and Constructive Hearts — say so in the Guilds chapter, and let the ammunition supply chain be a thing Companies fight over and Registries price writs around. The planned Duelist class still implies a dueling culture nobody has written.
- *Money/energy economy:* jolts/ebbs/orichalcum answers the energy side handsomely. Currency proper (Company scrip, what a laborer is paid in, what happens to scrip when a Company folds) is still open, and the insolvent Company behind the Rust Belt is the perfect historical case study to hang it on.
- *Antagonists:* the Beast Lords section is a major upgrade — four named sovereigns with distinct temperaments, and "What he/she/it wants" as a recurring header is exactly right. The Hollow King ambiguity (Shadow-aligned or not? Three Springs survives him and nobody can reconcile that with either theory) is the best mystery in the packet. Still missing: named Shadow-side antagonists (the Dust Lanes leak Shadow constantly and no demon, undead intelligence, or cult has a name), and any criminal/heretical humanoid opposition.
- *Religion beyond Leo:* still entirely open. Three Springs was "founded by a religious community" — founded around *what*? That one clause is currently the only non-Lions religious content in the setting.

**7. Prose tics — one carrier confirmed.** The notable-factions doc shares the "specific" habit ("the specific careful attention," "specific Gorûn elders," "specific decision points") and the regions doc has it mildly. Same pruning pass applies to all documents.

## Part Two: Feedback on the Three New Documents

### the-endless-frontier.tex (premise/player guide)

**What's working.** This is a strong pitch document. "Industry exists, but never wins," "you are invading a living system that fights back," and "nothing scales cleanly" are each one-line answers to why the frontier is *endless* — which is the question any Weird West that never closes has to answer, and most don't. The tech stack is the best part: Constructive Hearts explain trains-not-cars, ammunition logistics explain why melee still matters in a gun world, orichalcum scarcity explains why high-end gear is campaign-defining, and biollurgical chassis are a wonderfully strange solution to mobility that doubles as setting flavor (machines that behave like livestock — inherit, breed, mourn). Every piece of the physics generates gameplay. This is systems-as-worldbuilding done properly.

**Issues to fix.**
- **The energy units don't reconcile.** The opener says power is measured in *jolts*, "a discrete unit of usable energy," then the translation table maps jolts to *amps* (a rate, not a quantity) — and then every actual number in the document is denominated in *ebbs*, which appears in no table and is never defined relative to a jolt. As written, a careful reader cannot tell whether an ebb is a jolt, a quantity of jolts, or a separate unit. Pick one canonical energy-quantity unit, define it once, and make the volts/amps/ohms/watts table an explicit "translation for physics-minded readers" sidebar (it reads as flavor-renaming, which is fine, but it's currently presented as the measurement system).
- **Terminology drift: "Fey-aligned forces."** The rail-vulnerabilities section attributes sabotage to "fey-aligned forces" — a term appearing nowhere else in 190KB of lore, which otherwise consistently says Wild peoples, Beast Lords, sovereigns. If fey are a distinct category, they need introduction; if not, this is a fossil from an earlier draft and should read "Wild-aligned."
- The cold-iron weakness of the Wild's rulers is dropped in almost casually and is mechanically enormous (iron as both symbol *and* vulnerability). It deserves a sentence on how civilization exploits it — iron shot? iron fortification lines? — because players will immediately ask.
- Small structural note: the document is a player guide but front-loads energy-unit accounting before it tells players about living machines and named weapons. Consider reordering for seduction: world shape → the Wild → living machines → weapons → *then* the energy economy that underlies it.

### regions.tex

**What's working.** This is the best-constructed document in the entire packet and should be the model for the faction rewrites. Every region gets: a one-line identity, named locations with populations and owners, a conditions paragraph, and a starting-situations block — that's precisely the Origins/Texture/Fault-Lines/In-Play skeleton I proposed for factions, already executed. The regions also differentiate *kinds* of danger rather than degrees ("the Belt's danger has rules. The Marches' danger does not" is a perfect line), and several locations are self-contained campaign pitches: the Rust Belt, the Frozen Fortress, the Broken Track ("the workforce was lost over a single night, no incursion ever identified"), the Hollow, the Lost House. The Witch-Roads elegantly give the planned Witch class a home and give the unaffiliated the informal infrastructure I asked for. And the species-by-region distribution (Pelorim plains treaties, Brindel Warrens, Serekh Cisterns, Chyrr Watchposts, Dóm couriers, Uln tunnel-cities) does quiet, efficient work making the nonhumanoid peoples geographic rather than generic.

**Issues and suggestions.**
- **This document and the faction chapters don't know about each other.** The Lions chapter describes the mortal Banneret's operations with no location; the regions doc puts them in Thirstgate. The Guilds chapter fears an unnamed Banking Concern; the regions doc names Argent Line and its leverage. The Wardens chapter describes Beast Lord relationships in the abstract; the regions doc has four named sovereigns with distinct diplomatic statuses. Every one of these is a cross-reference waiting to be written, and the welding is most of the rewrite's work.
- The map problem: seven regions defined relationally (north of, southeast of) with no map will eventually produce contradictions. Even a crude schematic locks the geography before it drifts.
- Distances and travel times are absent, and in a setting where "travel is dangerous" is a core truth, they're load-bearing. How many days is Thirstgate to Lastlight by rail? Endline to the Glass Plains by Gorûn caravan? A small travel table per region would do more for play than another location.
- The Iron Reach mentions "two Noble Houses claim significant territory along its southern edge" — presumably Ardenne is one; name the other (or make the second House Vehk's northern claims and reconcile with its Salt Reach seat).
- The cosmological vocabulary (Wyrd, Dream/Nightmare, Brilliant Aurora, Realm of Ash, Realm of Smoke, Sea of Clouds, Endless Plummet, Shadow-leakage vs Wyrd-leakage) arrives here fully formed with no introduction in any uploaded document. If a cosmology chapter exists, regions should cite it the way it cites the Timeline; if not, it's now the second-most-needed missing document after the Timeline.

### notable-factions.tex (old subfactions document)

**What's working.** Far more of this survives than "old document for older versions" suggests — most entries are compatible with the current faction lore and several are better than compatible; they *demonstrate* the current lore. Marcellus Argent is the Trade Companies chapter's "political management" hierarchy wearing a face. The Vairo/Fadrick dynamic is the Lions' moderate/hard split turned into a succession thriller. The Burning Library is a whole thesis about Enclave institutional survival in three paragraphs. Severin Talvecq is a ready-made patron NPC attached to the setting's best cold case. The Beast Lords section, as noted, is the strongest antagonist material anywhere in the packet, and "What it wants" should become a mandatory header for every named power in the book. Brother Anselm — the junior Acolyte everyone trusts, whom the hierarchy has "so far chosen not to either formalize or disrupt" — is the kind of small, humane entry that makes a setting feel inhabited.

**Issues, mostly reconciliation with current lore.**
- **"The Champion of the East" collides with the rank system.** The current Lions chapter establishes Champion as a formal rank held only by celestial beings. Marcus Fadrick, a mortal Veteran Sergeant, carrying "Champion of the East" as a sobriquet muddies that — either rename the nickname (Warden of the East? the Sword of the East?) or make the collision deliberate: the hard faction calls him the Champion of the East *precisely because* the title is above his station, as a provocation. The second option is better; it turns a continuity error into politics.
- "Veteran Sergeant" — "Sergeant" isn't in the Lions rank ladder (Page, Squire, Knight, Veteran, Banneret...). Standardize to Veteran, or add Sergeant as an operational role distinct from rank.
- "General Audra Velgrim" — the current Mercenary chapter emphasizes that no two Companies share structure, so "General" is defensible as a Long March quirk, but say so in one clause, or the title reads as an oversight.
- Hesper Coil's "Maker-mark-equivalent expertise" — Maker-marks are established (in the regions doc) as a Wyr phenomenon; a Tikka having an "equivalent" is either a significant piece of lore (marks can be replicated?) or a loose phrase. Decide which.
- The Northwatch Enclave's formal research exchange with the Wardens is a great exception to the Enclaves' urban-core habit, but the Inter-Faction Relations chapter currently doesn't cover Enclave–Warden relations at all. When that pairing gets written, Northwatch is its anchor case.
- Tessa Crossbow as an *unaffiliated* former Mercenary commander running a major Company quietly illustrates the Unaffiliated chapter's "Chosen" pattern at the highest level of success — worth an explicit cross-reference; she's the best existing answer to "why would anyone refuse a faction?"
- The Wandering Witness entry is lovely and should be quoted or referenced in the Lions chapter's Circuit Rider section as the extreme case of the Rider-community bond the chapter describes abstractly.

## Part Four: Remaining Gaps and Suggested Work Order

**Still missing after all fifteen documents:**
1. The Timeline (referenced, not present) — write it next; everything cross-references it.
2. Cosmology primer (Wyrd, Shadow, the boundary realms, Leo's nature) — the regions doc uses this vocabulary constantly without a source.
3. A Threats chapter (Shadow-side named antagonists, Wild creature categories, criminal/cult opposition).
4. Religion for the unaffiliated majority (and what Three Springs was founded around).
5. Currency/scrip and the labor economy.
6. Faction texture passes (iconography, ritual, slang, recognition signals) — still absent everywhere.
7. A map, even schematic, plus travel times.

**Suggested order for the rewrite itself:**
1. **Timeline first** (even a one-page draft) — it deconflicts everything downstream and the Second Boundary War already anchors it.
2. **Reconciliation pass on notable-factions** (the title collisions and terminology fixes above) so it's safe to cite.
3. **Faction chapters one at a time** to the six-part template, welding in the regional/named material per the salvage map. Start with the Lions (most material, most fault lines already built: Vairo/Fadrick, Wandering Witness, Dayn, Dustline operations) or the Noble Houses (the Talvecq mystery gives the chapter an instant spine). Either makes a good proof-of-concept chapter.
4. **Inter-Faction Relations last among the faction docs**, since it inherits every fault line the chapters add — and give it the missing pairings (Enclaves–Wardens via Northwatch, Enclaves–Houses, Wardens–Houses).
5. Cosmology, Threats, and religion chapters as the following wave.

The pieces are all here. The premise doc supplies the physics, the regions doc supplies the geography and the model structure, the old subfactions doc supplies the cast, and the faction chapters supply the institutional intelligence. What the book needs now is less invention than plumbing — and one good proof-of-concept chapter to set the pattern.
