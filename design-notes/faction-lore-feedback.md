# Tall Tales — Faction Lore Feedback

Feedback on the twelve faction chapters, organized as cross-cutting notes first (issues that recur across nearly every file, where fixing them once fixes the whole book), then chapter-by-chapter feedback with specific suggestions for the rewrite.

The short version: the institutional sociology here is genuinely excellent — better than most published settings — but the book currently reads like a brilliant academic study of a world rather than an invitation into one. The rewrite's job is to keep the analytical intelligence and add the proper nouns, the live crises, the sensory texture, and the street-level entry points that turn analysis into a playable place.

---

## Part One: Cross-Cutting Notes

### 1. The proper noun problem (the single biggest upgrade available)

Across roughly 140KB of lore there is exactly one named individual (Leo) and zero named Enclaves, Houses, Companies, Orders, Registries, cities, wars, or historical events. Everything is described at the level of category and pattern: "a House," "the harder factions," "a Company whose rail line," "a famous neutrality breach" that is never actually recounted.

Players and GMs hook onto names. "The Noble Houses feud with each other" is scenery; "House Veranth and House Okello have not exchanged direct words since the Red Betrothal, and every Registry within five hundred miles knows not to accept contracts touching either" is a campaign. The abstraction is a deliberate stylistic choice and it does real work — it keeps the material portable — but taken this far it means the GM has to invent 100% of the concrete layer before anything can happen at the table.

Recommendation: keep the archetype framing, but give every faction 3–5 named exemplars (specific Enclaves, Houses, Companies, Orders, Registries) with one distinguishing trait, one current problem, and one relationship each. Explicitly label them as defaults the GM can rename or replace. This preserves portability while giving tables something to grab. The Trade Companies chapter even punts on this on purpose ("the specific Companies vary by region and the GM's judgment") — that's the right instinct for exhaustiveness but the wrong one for zero examples.

### 2. No history, no timeline

The setting's most interesting fact — there are no states — is asserted but never explained. Was there a collapse? Did nations ever exist here? How old is the rail network, the Enclave system, the Lions' formal structure? The Shadow, the Wild, and Leo are load-bearing concepts referenced in every chapter and defined in none of them (they may live in other chapters of the book, but the faction chapters constantly gesture at events — "generations of hard experience," "documented appearances," "the failures that reshape regional politics for a generation" — without ever recounting a single one).

Recommendation: a shared timeline of five to ten major named events that all faction chapters can cross-reference. Each event should be something with unresolved consequences: the incursion that birthed the proto-Lions, the containment catastrophe that shapes public fear of Arcanists, the Company collapse that stranded a dozen settlements, the House war whose peace terms are still being litigated. Unresolved history is the cheapest adventure-hook generator that exists, and right now the setting has an implied history and no actual one.

### 3. Everything works too well

This is the deepest structural issue for a game setting. Every faction in these chapters is functional, self-aware, and stable. Tensions exist, but they are almost universally described as "managed," "at equilibrium," "handled carefully and at distance," "navigated continuously and imperfectly." The Guild's neutrality holds. The Lions' internal split persists but never ruptures. The Houses' détente is "stable" and "uncomfortable." The prose repeatedly reaches for the mature, steady-state description — which is realistic and gives the world credibility, but adventures come from the moment the steady state breaks, and nothing in these chapters is currently breaking.

Recommendation: add a "Current Fault Lines" section to every faction chapter — three to five things going wrong right now, at campaign start. Not resolved, not managed: live. Examples of the kind of thing that belongs there: the mortal Banneret is dying and both Lion factions are maneuvering over the succession; a Banking Concern has frozen a regional Guild's dues in an open test of Guild neutrality; an Enclave has quietly lost containment on something and is hiding it; a Beast Lord who held a treaty for sixty years has gone silent; a Registry's senior adjudicator has been caught selling writ information. Each fault line is a campaign frame. The chapters currently describe pressure everywhere and rupture nowhere.

### 4. No street-level entry points

The chapters are written top-down — origins, structure, political position — which is the right skeleton, but almost nothing is written from the altitude at which a level-1 party actually lives. What does the faction want from adventurers? Who is the NPC that hands out the work? What are the entry-level jobs, and what do the jobs look like at mid and high tiers? Can a PC hold rank, and what does each rank actually get them?

Only the Wardens chapter has an "in play" section (and it's excellent — see below). Recommendation: make that section a template every chapter gets, with three components: (a) what membership looks like mechanically and socially at low/mid/high tiers, (b) five to eight concrete mission hooks the faction generates, (c) two or three patron NPC archetypes with names.

### 5. Sensory texture is almost entirely absent

There is no description anywhere of what any of these people look like. No iconography, uniforms, guild marks, oaths, rituals, slang, hand signs, songs, funerals, initiation ceremonies. How does a Circuit Rider dress? What does a Zealot's kit look like? What's on a Company banner? What does an Enclave smell like? How do two Wardens identify each other (the chapter says "practices" exist and describes none of them)? What does a bounty writ physically look like?

Recognition-at-a-glance is how players inhabit factions. Every chapter needs a short "texture" pass: visual signifiers, one ritual, one piece of in-world slang or a saying, one thing members do that outsiders notice. This is also where in-world voice can enter — a quoted proverb, an excerpt from a Rider's circuit log, a line from a Guild examination — which would break up the current single-register prose (see note 7).

### 6. Missing subject matter

Several things the chapters lean on don't exist yet anywhere in the packet, and a few genre-level expectations are unmet:

- **Antagonists.** The Shadow, demons, undead, and hostile Wild sovereigns are referenced constantly and developed never. The setting also has no criminal organizations, cults, heretical movements, or named villains of any kind. A companion "Threats" chapter (or at minimum, named antagonists inside each faction's fault lines) is needed for the factions' protective functions to mean anything concrete.
- **Religion beyond Leo.** The church network exists purely as a Lions funding mechanism. Is Leo the only venerated being? What do the unaffiliated majority believe, and what do their congregations do besides fund Zealots? This intersects the Unaffiliated chapter directly.
- **Money.** The Companies "functionally issue currencies" — a huge fact dropped in half a sentence. Company scrip, debt, banking, and what the poor actually get paid in is both worldbuilding and a rich adventure vein (company towns, debt peonage, scrip that dies when a Company does).
- **The guns.** The title is *Tall Tales* and there is nearly no firearms culture in 140KB of lore. Who manufactures guns — Tinkerers? Who regulates them? Is there a dueling culture (a Duelist class is planned)? What's the ammunition economy? Where does the western flavor live — presumably the Registries and the Companies, but neither chapter currently reads dusty. This is a branding-level gap: someone who picked the book up for the title would not find the title in these chapters.

### 7. Prose tics to vary in the rewrite

The voice is strong but has patterns that become visible across twelve chapters read together:

- **The negation opening.** "The Guilds did not begin as a unified institution. They began as a survival strategy." / "The Noble Houses did not begin as nobility. They began as necessity." / "The Orders did not begin with the Blade-Bound tradition." / "The Lions did not begin with Leo." / "The Wardens did not choose their role. The role chose them." Five of seven major factions open with the same rhetorical move. It's a good move — once, maybe twice.
- **The individual-relationships escape hatch.** "Individual X and individual Y often have working relationships that exist somewhat outside their institutions' official positions" appears in near-identical form at least four times (Enclaves–Lions, Orders–Lions, Wardens–Lions, and again in Inter-Faction Relations). True everywhere, but say it once in the relations chapter and cut the repeats.
- **The balanced-paradox flourish.** "...considered healthy by the leadership and corrosive by the people experiencing it, which is probably the correct assessment of both perspectives simultaneously." / "real and shallow simultaneously." / "seems simple and proves complex, or seems complex and proves simple." Delightful individually; a verbal habit in aggregate.
- **"Specific."** This word appears dozens of times, often several times per paragraph ("a specific quality of attention," "specific obligations," "the specific category of person"). A find-and-prune pass will noticeably tighten the whole book.

## Part Two: Chapter-by-Chapter

### factions-overview.tex

**What's working.** The "who are your people" framing is the best single idea in the packet — it's a clean, memorable answer to why factions replace nationality, and the Recognition / Obligations / Protections / Identity breakdown is genuinely useful design vocabulary that the rest of the book can (and should) reuse consistently. "What a Faction Does Not Provide" is exactly the right corrective — the faction as an institution with its own interests, not a discount card. The Frontier Question section is quietly one of the strongest pieces in the file: "the faction is still load-bearing for who they are, no longer load-bearing for what is available to them" is a great line and a great character-generation prompt.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- The statelessness claim needs at least a paragraph of history, even a deliberately mysterious one. "Nationality stopped being viable" is doing enormous work with no support. Two or three sentences about *how* — collapse, the Wild's pressure, the Companies outcompeting states — would ground everything downstream.
- Add a quick-reference table: faction, rough size, where you find them, what they want, what they offer a PC. This chapter is where a GM will orient, and right now orientation requires reading seven full chapters.
- The Frontier Question deserves expansion with two or three worked examples (the Arcanist whose nearest Enclave is six weeks away; the House scion in a region where their name means nothing).
- The dual-membership passage is good; consider adding one named canonical example (an actual person) rather than only the hypothetical Mentalist-Arcanist.
- The class table's "(planned)" entries are fine for a design document but should be flagged for cleanup before player-facing release, and the orphan-class list (Emanist, Aetherwright, Witch) hints that unaffiliated-leaning classes need coverage in the Unaffiliated chapter — currently they're not mentioned there.

### academic-enclaves.tex

**What's working.** The opening — "Arcane magic is not learned. It erupts." — followed by the child who leaves is the best origin sequence in the set: visceral, sad, specific, and generative (it immediately implies missions: finding children before their communities do something worse). The "arcane mass" concept — magic as a third state of being that accumulates and cannot be put down — is distinctive and mechanically suggestive. The refusal to answer where magic comes from, framed as open research question versus confession, is a superb mystery engine. The observation that the safest Enclaves are the *useful* ones, not the *feared* ones, is smart institutional logic.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **Rogue Arcanists are the chapter's missing adventure engine.** The origin section establishes that untrained Arcanists are catastrophically dangerous — and then the chapter never asks what happens when a trained one goes bad, refuses training, or flees the system. Who hunts them? The Enclaves themselves (with what enforcement arm)? The Lions (with what jurisdiction)? Registries (with what writ)? This is the faction's obvious mission generator and its obvious internal moral tension, and it's absent.
- Give the setting its Chernobyl: one named historical containment catastrophe that every community's fear traces back to. The chapter says communities "that have experienced an uncontrolled Arcanist firsthand don't forget it" — show the one nobody forgot.
- Name three or four Enclaves with distinct cultures: the hyper-theoretical old one, the pragmatic frontier one that trains fast and loose, the one with a militant reputation, the disgraced one still under sanction. Inter-Enclave rivalry is asserted ("bitter dispute") and never illustrated.
- The spellbook-as-shared-inheritance idea implies an economy the chapter doesn't explore: spellbook theft, forgery, the estate of a dead Arcanist, the black market in uncatalogued books. All adventure material.
- Open question worth answering (or pointedly not answering): what happens to the arcane mass when an Arcanist dies? If it "cannot be put down," death has implications — potentially the setting's best horror hook.
- Add ranks/titles for the student-to-senior progression so PC Arcanists have a ladder.

### landed-guilds.tex

**What's working.** The three parallel hierarchies (craft, administrative, political — the last officially nonexistent) is the most sophisticated institutional design in the packet and it's immediately gameable: knowing *which hierarchy your problem lives in* is a skill PCs can learn. Neutrality-as-procedure-not-principle is mature and believable. The Tinkerer strike-leverage point and the Banking Concern threat to Guild dues are both excellent pressure points. Examination by out-of-city Masters is a nice concrete detail — the chapter's best texture moment, and a mission hook hiding in plain sight (escort the examiner).

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **Justify the trenchcoat.** Why are Sawbones, Tinkerers, and Enforcers *one* institution rather than three separate guilds? The origin section explains why each craft needed collective protection, but not why they merged with each other rather than organizing separately — medicine, engineering, and violence-for-hire are strange bedfellows and players will ask. A specific historical crisis that forced amalgamation (and a faction of members who still resent it) would turn the oddity into content.
- The Enforcer section is noticeably thinner than the other two crafts and needs the most expansion — Enforcers are also the craft most likely to interact with a party.
- The Banking Concern leverage problem is mentioned twice (here and in Trade Companies) and dramatized nowhere. Make it a live fault line: a specific Concern is testing a specific Guild's neutrality *right now*.
- Recount the famous neutrality breach. The rules were "tested, found wanting, revised" — the story of the worst failure is the story that teaches readers what the rules mean.
- Texture pass: guild marks, hall architecture, what dues cost, what an apprentice's day looks like, the oath sworn at Journeyman elevation.
- Hooks: recovering a stolen case-library volume, a malpractice investigation the local hall wants handled quietly, a strike that a Company has hired a Mercenary Company to break, certification fraud on the frontier.

### mercenary-companies.tex

**What's working.** The registration line — a Company and a bandit confederacy differ "primarily by registration and demonstrated reliability," and the line is "thinner than either side officially acknowledges" — is the chapter's best idea and a whole campaign in one sentence. The three specializations are clean and honest about their tradeoffs (institutional softening in defense work is a nice touch). The trust-across-time framing of why Companies exist is convincing. The Tactician section does the best class-lore integration in the packet: "managing the flow of time within the engagement" makes the mechanics feel diegetic.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- This is currently the most generic chapter — it reads like a business-school case study of a mercenary industry rather than a portrait of one. It needs names most urgently: four or five Companies with personalities. The honorable old one trading on a century of reputation; the cut-rate one that undercuts everyone and loses people doing it; the ex-bandit outfit that registered and can't shake the past; the elite monster-hunting Company nobody can afford; the one that broke a contract once and has spent thirty years living it down.
- **Life as a grunt.** Companies are the most natural home for a classic adventuring party, and the chapter is written entirely from the commander's-eye view. Add a section on the member's experience: recruitment, the mess hall, pay day, what happens to the dead, pensions, the banner, the songs, what leaving costs.
- What happens when a Company goes rogue — breaks contract, turns on a client, drops off the registry? The bandit line implies this happens; make it a named historical case and a current fault line.
- The Tactician section is great but leaves the other native classes (Drifter, Berserker, Bounty Hunter, planned Duelist and Monster Hunter) with nothing. A paragraph each on where they fit in Company culture would balance it.
- The Registry rivalry is well covered here and in the Registry chapter — good cross-referencing, keep it — but consider consolidating the legal-precedence material in one place and pointing at it from the other.

### monastic-orders.tex

**What's working.** Grounding the tradition in a question ("what is the relationship between will and reality?") rather than a founder is elegant. The mind blade as diagnostic — the weapon reveals the practitioner and can't be faked — is the packet's loveliest single conceit. The "what the blade can cut" progression (flesh → materials → magic → barriers between spaces → things ordinary language can't describe) is a perfect advancement fiction. The unnegotiated, unspoken monastery-and-town relationship is quietly charming.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **The Orders are dramatically inert.** The entire final section is a list of things they don't do — no contracts, no sides, no wealth, no recruiting — and the chapter offers no internal conflict, no rival, no wound. A faction defined purely by serene abstention gives players nothing to push against and GMs nothing to break. The single highest-value addition: a schism. An Order (or a movement across Orders) that concluded the trained will should be used to *reshape* the world rather than merely perfect the self — an activist or interventionist heresy the mainstream Orders regard with alarm. That one addition gives the faction antagonists, missions, and a live philosophical argument instead of a settled one.
- What happens when a will *breaks*? A practitioner whose blade destabilizes, changes form unbidden, or cuts things the practitioner didn't intend is implied by the diagnostic framing and never followed up. Broken Blade-Bound are an NPC and monster generator, and the Orders' fear of producing them is character material.
- How does an adult join? The chapter only shows the settled monastery model. Wandering masters, trial periods, the person who shows up at the gate — the genre expects these and the chapter is silent.
- The Slash Space social-alarm point is good; dramatize one incident (the town that watched a practitioner step through a cut in the air, and what happened after).
- What do the Orders want from a party? Currently: nothing. Even abstention generates needs — a stolen technique manuscript, a broken former student loose in the hills, a Company pressuring the monastery's town.

### noble-houses.tex

**What's working.** The unsentimental origin logic — tolerance is revocable, so accumulate power until elimination costs too much, then extend protection for loyalty — is the strongest origins section in the packet, and the gap between that functional logic and the Houses' own lineage-and-covenant self-narrative is a built-in theme. The three traditions integrate well (the three-layer negotiation example is excellent). The Contracts-as-guarantor role is a clever answer to how territorially weak Houses stay relevant. The Mentalist ethics passage (practical capability management versus internalized prohibition) is sophisticated. The visible-Mutant sheltering paradox — protected into isolation — is handled with real care.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **This chapter suffers most from the no-names problem**, because House politics *is* the faction's content and there are zero Houses. The Inter-Faction Relations chapter even tells the GM to "track which Houses are allied, which are at odds" — with no Houses to track. Provide four to six named Houses: signature capability mix, territory, one feud, one alliance, one secret. This is the rewrite's single most important addition anywhere in the packet.
- Add current fault lines: a succession crisis, a pact coming due that a House can't pay, a runaway dependent (the Registries chapter references these — they deserve treatment *here*, from the House's and the runaway's side), a lineage whose mutations have started expressing wrong.
- The Contractor entities need at least a light taxonomy or a few named recurring patrons. Entities that multiple Houses have generational records on are prime NPCs, and "what the entity actually wants versus what it says it wants" begs for a worked example.
- Daily life is missing: what is it like to be a low-born House retainer, a member without capability, a daughter being evaluated for a pairing? The breeding-program unease is honest but abstract — one human-scale vignette would land it harder.
- Can outsiders marry in? Be adopted in? PCs will want to know their route into (or out of) a House.

### frontier-wardens.tex

**What's working.** The best chapter in the packet. "They describe what they feel at the Wild's edge as something closer to recognition than courage" is the best line in 140KB. The supplicant posture toward Beast Lords — acknowledge sovereignty, offer value, accept that the answer might be no — is a genuinely fresh take on ranger-fiction, and the resulting political problem (Wardens who *understand why* the Beast Lords destroy rail lines) gives the faction real ideological weight. And "The Warden Experience in Play" is the only street-level section in the whole packet and it's superb — the Shapeshifter "who moves with the specific quality of something that has been many things and isn't entirely sure what it currently is" is wonderful. **This section is the template every other chapter should copy.**

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- Name two or three Beast Lords and sovereigns, one standing treaty, and one treaty that failed (and what the failure cost). The Wild is the setting's structural counterparty and currently has no faces at all.
- The network's "practices" — ways of leaving information, identifying each other, establishing trust — are asserted and never shown. Two or three concrete examples (a trail-sign vocabulary, a cache protocol, a recognition exchange) become things players can actually use and forge.
- **The faction's missing boogeyman: the Warden who went over.** The chapter maps three ways Wardens make peace with their liminal position, but never names the fourth outcome — the one who stopped coming back, whose sympathy became allegiance. Is there a word for them? Do Wardens hunt their own? This is the tradition's internal fear and its richest drama, and it's one sentence away from existing.
- Recruitment: the origin section implies Wardens find boundary-crossers before the Wild kills them, mirroring the Enclaves' informant network. Make that explicit — it's a PC origin story and a mission type (reach the kid who keeps walking past the markers before something else does).

### witnesses-of-the-lion.tex

**What's working.** The richest chapter, deservedly the longest. Witness-as-founding-discipline is a genuinely fresh take on the holy order — the insight that the *quality of attention* determines the quality of the work, and that refusing abstraction is a practical combat discipline rather than a piety, elevates the whole faction. The emotional-honesty culture in Zealot formations (a formation where nobody admits fear is a formation whose prayer has gone performative) is the packet's most surprising and most humane idea. The Circuit Rider intelligence function is excellent. The mortal/celestial authority asymmetry is well drawn. And the hard/moderate split is the setting's best conflict engine — it correctly makes the Lions every other faction's weather system.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **Leo is underdefined.** God, celestial, ascended mortal, saint? Even deliberate mystery needs a frame — what do the Lions themselves believe he is, and do the hard and moderate factions disagree about *that* too? Related: the chapter says documented accounts of Leo's appearances exist and are studied. Show one. A half-page boxed excerpt from an actual account would be the single most powerful worldbuilding move available to this book — it delivers Leo, the witness tradition, and the in-world voice in one stroke.
- Name things: the three Commanders (they have types — Dragon Ascendant, Solar, Throne Archon — but no names), the mortal Banneret (structurally the most important human being in the setting and a guaranteed major NPC), and above all the hard faction. Movements have names — give the hardliners one (and a leading voice with a name and a plausible, sympathetic argument), and the moderates' internal label for themselves. Unnamed, the split stays abstract; named, it's a politics PCs can be recruited into.
- Missing operational detail: discipline and expulsion. The Unaffiliated chapter says a removed Lion is "the rarest case" — what does removal look like, and what happens to the removed? Also missing: how one *joins* (a Page has to come from somewhere).
- Fault lines: the Banneret succession, a church schism or funding revolt in a key region, a hard-faction cell that acted unilaterally against a House or Enclave and the fallout now landing on moderate leadership.
- Trim: "The Name" section restates the witness thesis already established in Origins; fold its best paragraph into Origins and cut the rest. At 24KB this chapter is over twice the length of some others; it earns much of that, but the witness point is made at full length at least three times.

### trade-companies.tex

**What's working.** "The closest thing this world possesses to a state" and "power that is real and shallow simultaneously" are exactly the right thesis. The sustained-fiction framing (everyone pretends the territory is merely "frontier" because nobody benefits from saying otherwise) is elegant. The employer-versus-faction distinction — "the Company is their employer... not the institution that knows them" — is a crucial clarification that keeps the whole system coherent. The faction-by-faction relationship rundown is efficient and the Warden entry ("the specific weariness of people who have repeatedly explained why a plan won't work and watched it be tried anyway") is a gem.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- Name three to five Companies as renameable defaults, one per archetype at least. The archetypes are good scaffolding; they need at least one occupant each.
- **Expand the money.** "Currencies they functionally issue" is a colossal fact in half a sentence. Company scrip, company stores, debt peonage, what happens to scrip when a Company folds — this is worldbuilding, adventure fuel, and the setting's most natural source of villainy-without-a-mustache all at once.
- **Labor.** Is there worker organizing on the rail lines? A strike collides three institutions at once — Guild neutrality, Mercenary law-enforcement contracts, Lions moral pressure — and would be one of the best flashpoint scenarios in the book. Its absence is conspicuous in a setting this economically literate.
- A Company collapse as historical event and current threat: what happened to the settlements when the line died? Ghost towns on dead rail are pure campaign geography.
- This chapter is also a natural home for firearms-economy material (who arms Company security, gun manufacture and Tinkerer patents, scrip-priced ammunition on the frontier) — see cross-cutting note 6.

### bounty-registries.tex

**What's working.** The niche is razor-sharp: the court of last resort filling gaps every other institution has structural reasons to refuse, with the refusals actually itemized — that's tight institutional design. The strict limits of a writ (named target only, posted jurisdictions only, void on error) create instant tactical and dramatic constraints. The House-bounty moral murk ("grievances that are not legally cognizable") is quietly dark in the best way. And "The Unsanctioned Hunt" is the most immediately adventure-ready material in the entire packet — the Registries' pragmatic hypocrisy about it is perfect.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **This is where the western should live, and it currently reads procedural.** Posting boards, the physical writ (paper? seal? something harder to forge?), dead-or-alive norms and price scales, the saloon where Hunters compare paper, the etiquette when two Hunters hold the same writ. The chapter's institutional logic is finished; its dust and leather haven't arrived. This is also the natural home for gun culture — the iron a Hunter carries, what a writ licenses them to do with it.
- Every bounty setting needs its legendary uncaught target. Name one — the writ that's been posted for twenty years, the price that keeps climbing, the Hunters who've died on it.
- Fault lines: corruption inside a Registry (an adjudicator selling target intelligence; escrow fraud), a reciprocity breakdown (a city's Registry blacklisted, its Hunters suddenly operating without cross-territory authority), a House bounty that turned out to be something much uglier than the paper said.
- The certification process is called "rigorous" — show it. What does the exam test? What voids a certification? A PC Hunter needs to know what they swore to.

### unaffiliated.tex

**What's working.** The closest to finished of the twelve. "Most people are unaffiliated" is a crucial grounding move that keeps the faction system from swallowing the world, and framing the majority as *not needing explanation* is exactly right. The Defected / Rejected / Chosen taxonomy is immediately usable at character creation, and the distinctions are drawn with care (the note that Rejected characters' complications "matter for roleplay in ways that pure defection generally does not" shows real design attention). The per-class social readings are excellent — especially the unaffiliated Contractor/Mentalist/Mutant as "the most dangerous social position" and the unaffiliated Zealot as "essentially impossible."

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **The unaffiliated need informal infrastructure.** Right now "no faction" reads as "no social structure," but freelancers everywhere develop fixers, reputation networks, taverns where work finds workers, and word-of-mouth vouching. A short section on the informal economy of the unaffiliated makes the condition playable rather than merely thematic — and gives unaffiliated PCs their own NPC ecosystem.
- Add faction poaching: how institutions recruit visible unaffiliated talent, and what pressure that recruitment can carry (the Orders' "not always welcome" interest in wild Blade-Bound is the model — generalize it).
- The overview chapter's orphan classes (Emanist, Witch, Aetherwright) lean unaffiliated by design and go unmentioned here; a paragraph on classes that are unaffiliated *by tradition* rather than by circumstance would close the loop.
- A short sidebar of five or six sample unaffiliated NPC concepts (one per pattern, plus a couple of ambiguous cases) would make the taxonomy concrete.

### inter-faction-relations.tex

**What's working.** "Pairwise rather than systemically" — the explicit refusal of a single alliance axis or good/evil axis — is exactly the right frame and worth stating as loudly as the chapter states it. The Enclaves–Lions treatment (with regional variation by which Lion faction is ascendant) is the model entry. "When Factions Collide in Play" is good GM guidance, and the observation that a party ignoring faction politics "is implicitly taking the position that personal relationships have superseded institutional ones, which is also a meaningful position" is a genuinely wise note.

**Suggestions for the rewrite.**
- **Coverage is uneven.** The Lions get three detailed pairings; the "Guilds and Everyone" / "Orders and Everyone" shortcuts flatten everything else, and several pairings with obvious content are missing entirely: Enclaves–Houses (two supernatural traditions with a shared Lions problem — allies or rivals?), Wardens–Houses (Contractor agreements "that intersect Wild sovereignty" get one tantalizing clause), Enclaves–Orders (two traditions studying inexplicable power from opposite directions), Mercenaries–Houses. Consider a full relationship matrix — 9×9 including Companies and Registries — with a one-word status per cell, keeping prose for the pairs that earn it. The matrix is also simply a great GM reference page.
- Add a **flashpoints section**: eight to ten concrete "this is happening somewhere right now" scenarios, each one sentence, each droppable into a campaign. (The Company strike, the dual-writ standoff, the hard-Lion cell moving on an Enclave, the House bounty a Registry shouldn't have accepted, the Beast Lord treaty going silent...) This chapter is the natural home for the payoff of every fault line the faction chapters add.
- Missing mechanism: how are inter-faction disputes actually *adjudicated*? There's no state court. The Houses' Contractor-guarantor role (from the Houses chapter) is the obvious answer and the two chapters never cross-reference — connecting them would be a satisfying click, and would make House Contractors the notaries of the whole diplomatic system.

## Part Three: A Suggested Chapter Template for the Rewrite

To make the additions systematic rather than piecemeal, consider rewriting each faction chapter to a shared skeleton (the current Origins → Structure → Politics core survives intact inside it):

1. **Origins** — current material, with at least one named historical event.
2. **Structure** — current material, plus ranks/titles a PC can hold.
3. **Culture & Texture** — new: iconography, dress, ritual, slang, one in-world quoted excerpt.
4. **Named Exemplars** — new: 3–5 named sub-institutions/people, each with a trait, a problem, a relationship.
5. **Current Fault Lines** — new: 3–5 live crises at campaign start.
6. **In Play** — modeled on the Wardens chapter: what membership feels like at low/mid/high tier, 5–8 mission hooks, 2–3 named patron archetypes.
