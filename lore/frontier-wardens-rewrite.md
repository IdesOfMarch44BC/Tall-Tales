# The Frontier Wardens

## Origins

Every generation, in every region, a certain number of people walk past the markers and do not die.

They are not the brave ones. The brave ones die quickly, because courage is a claim, and the Wild answers claims. The ones who come back describe what carried them in language that has been consistent across three centuries of other Wardens nodding at it: not daring, not defiance — *recognition*. Something at the boundary that felt less like a wall than like a room they had been away from. They went in the way you enter a house you half-remember, and the house permitted it, and they came out changed in the specific way the tradition watches for: quieter, unhurried, and unable to fully explain why the rail towns now feel slightly like weather they are visiting.

The Wardens find these people. It is the tradition’s first duty and its recruitment in one — the boundary-crossers located before the Wild keeps them or a frightened town does something older — and it mirrors, as more than one Enclave scholar has noted, the informant networks that find the manifesting children, except that the Wardens are not rescuing anyone from a power. They are introducing someone to a relationship.

Because that is what the tradition is. The Wardens are not rangers, not scouts, not soldiers of the tree line — the frontier has all of those, and buries them on schedule. The Wardens are the only diplomatic corps civilization has ever successfully maintained with the thing that broke the nations: the interpreters, negotiators, and boundary-keepers of the long, unequal, absolutely necessary conversation between the settled world and the Wild’s sovereigns. They walk the ground no one claims. They carry the messages no rail can. They maintain the treaties — the Winter Concession’s channels, the Plains compact, a hundred local understandings written in nothing but kept behavior — and they hold, at all times, the professional posture that defines them and discomfits everyone they serve: the **supplicant’s posture**. A Warden approaching a sovereign acknowledges sovereignty, offers value, and accepts that the answer may be no. Not as tactic. As truth. The Wild’s rulers are not a problem civilization is slowly solving. They are the neighbors, they were here first, they are winning, and the Wardens are the people honest enough to work from those facts — which is why every institution needs them, trusts them, and is never, ever entirely sure of them.

The unsureness has a reason, and the tradition has stopped apologizing for it: Wardens *understand* the Wild. A Warden who has spent twenty years learning why the sovereigns act arrives, sooner or later, at the place where the understanding becomes sympathy — where the destroyed survey camp reads as an answer rather than an atrocity, where the Warden briefing the Company owners must translate not just the Wild’s terms but its *point* — and the settled world has never fully decided whether its indispensable interpreters are its agents at the boundary or the boundary’s agents in the towns. The Wardens’ own answer, given for three centuries in the same flat tone: *yes.*

## The Shapes

The Wardens’ signature art is the oldest courtesy the boundary offers, and understanding it begins with understanding what a shape *is* at the boundary: a shape is a standing. To wear a form in sovereign country is to be **vouched for by it** — the wolf-shaped walker moves through wolf-country as kin, read by every watching thing as something the land has already said yes to — and so the shapeshifter’s art is not, at root, a combat form or a traveler’s convenience. It is a *credential*. The shapes are how a Warden enters the Wild as a relative instead of a claim.

The tradition’s own frame for the art is the **hearth**: the self as a house, and every shape a guest it hosts. A hearth holds what it holds — a young shifter’s self can keep one modest guest, or a few small ones; a great carrier’s can host something with real weight in it — and the tradition’s entire pedagogy is hearth-craft: how to take a guest in, how to keep the rooms in order, how to remain, across a working life of being many things, unmistakably the host. What fills the hearth differs by lineage, and the network holds three broad traditions, intermarried everywhere and distinct at the root:

**The pelt-binders** carry their shapes in skins. The pelt is not a costume; it is a *grant* — taken with rites from a beast that consented in the ways beasts consent, or given, in the tradition’s proudest cases, by a sovereign’s own creature as a live credential into its lord’s country — and the binder who wears it is the beast, wholly, for as long as the skin is on. Binders tend to few shapes, deeply held: the woman who is a wolf, entirely, and nothing else, and has been that wolf’s colleague for thirty years. The pelts are heirloomed, mourned, and never, ever sold, and the network’s harshest customary law concerns exactly what a stolen pelt makes its thief: not a shifter — *prey*, everywhere, indefinitely.

**The hearth-walkers** carry their shapes in themselves — the many-formed tradition, hosting a working household of lighter guests: the cat for the town, the hawk for the sky, the horse for the road. Walkers earn shapes by *living* them — the apprenticeship is a season spent alongside the animal, learning it from the inside of its days, until the shape consents to lodge — and the mature walker is the network’s utility knife and its most recognizable figure: the person at the relay post who moves, as the old field description has it, *with the specific quality of something that has been many things and isn’t entirely sure what it currently is.* The tradition considers that description affectionate, accurate, and a mild professional warning, in equal parts.

**The face-takers** carry people. The humanoid shapes — the changeling art, the third tradition and the watched one, because a face is not a beast: no sovereign grants it, no season’s apprenticeship earns it honestly, and the guest it lodges is the likeness of a *person*, with everything that implies. The network did not invent face-taking and has never been fully comfortable owning it — the art’s own accounts trace it to the boundary’s strangest edge, where the Wild leans against the dreaming — but the network keeps it, disciplines it harder than anything else it does, and uses it, because the face-takers’ defense of their craft has never been answered: *the town is also a landscape.* Somebody has to walk civilization the way the binders walk the deep country — unremarked, credentialed, kin-shaped — and the Warden who can wear a rail town’s faces has saved more treaty relationships, quietly, at kitchen tables and in Company offices, than any three heroes of the tree line. Face-takers operate under the network’s only written code, carry its only revocable standing, and answer, for the gravest violations — a face worn to steal its owner’s life — to a discipline the network conducts itself, completely, before anyone else can ask to.

Two facts bind all three traditions. The first is where the vigor comes from: the shapes draw on the same deep source as everything that grows — the generative boundary the scholars call the Realm of Wood — and the Wardens’ arts are, in the cosmologists’ ledger, the oldest licensed use of it. The second is the price, and it is the tradition’s own road to its own ghost story: **a guest can become the host.** The shifter who carries too much shape, too long, too deep — who winters as the wolf because the wolf is warmer, who stops changing back promptly, then stops changing back — is walking, form by form, the road the whole network fears, and it does not end in monstrosity. It ends in the next section.

## The Kept

The Wild keeps people. It is what the boundary has always done with those who belong to it more than they belong back here, and the network’s word for its own lost is exact, gentle, and terrible: not *fallen*, not *turned* — **kept**.

Every Warden who works the deep ground long enough feels the pull the recognition was always the near edge of, and most manage it for a working lifetime: the discipline of the hearth, the partner who asks the blunt questions, the customary furloughs in town that young Wardens resent and old Wardens never skip. Some do not manage it. A Warden goes in — on a routine walk, on a hard errand, sometimes simply at the end of a long quiet argument with themselves that their sign-partners reconstruct afterward from small things — and does not come out, and is not dead: the network’s craft can usually establish that much, a presence persisting in the ground, sign that is almost theirs, a shape among the sovereign’s shapes that watches the old relay line a little too long. The Wild kept them. What they are now — servant, citizen, beast, something the sovereigns themselves have a word for that no Warden has ever gotten translated — differs case by case, and the network’s doctrine on its Kept is three sentences, taught to every second in their first season, and it is the purest expression of the tradition’s whole soul:

*We do not hunt the Kept. We visit. If the visit goes badly, we do not visit again.*

Some visits go well — there are Kept who leave the relay caches restocked, Kept whose old ground stays mysteriously safe for travelers, one famous Kept who has attended, at a distance, at the tree line, every Gather of her old circuit for nineteen years. Some go badly, and the network withdraws, and marks the sign, and grieves the way it does everything: quietly, practically, in the maintenance of what the lost one used to maintain. And some — the cases the doctrine was written for — go over to the other thing entirely: a Kept who has taken service with a sovereign against the settled world is the most dangerous scout, saboteur, and negotiator-in-reverse the frontier can face, a mind with the network’s whole craft and the Wild’s whole cause. The Wardens do not hunt even these. The Wardens tell the Lions where not to look for them, which everyone involved understands is not the same thing, and is the most the tradition will ever do, and the network has paid for that line, more than once, and kept it.

## The Craft

The network runs on practices, not offices, and the practices are teachable, physical, and everywhere.

**Sign.** The Wardens’ trail-language: a vocabulary of cut, stack, knot, and scratch — read on blazes, cairns, fence posts, and door lintels across every region — that renders, in a dozen strokes, the news that matters: *ground quiet / ground restless / sovereign moving / treaty business / do not / welcome / mine.* Sign is layered — the public strokes any traveler may learn (and should: the *do not* glyph has saved more Company lives than every wall in the Reach), the network strokes, and each Warden’s **hand** — the personal signature no two alike, by which a reader knows not just the message but the messenger, and by which the network, reading an old friend’s hand on a new blaze, knows things no words in the message say.

**Caches and the relay lines.** The deep ground is strung with the network’s logistics: sealed caches (food, medicine, bronze tools, never iron) at walked intervals, and the **relay posts** — a hut, a hollow tree, a dry cave, a shelf in a monastery gatehouse — where messages, maps, and Wardens themselves change hands. The relay lines are the frontier’s true nervous system; the Lions’ intelligence, the Companies’ assessments, and the Enclaves’ field stations all graft onto them; and the network’s one absolute rule of the lines is the reason everyone is permitted to graft: what travels the relay travels *whole* — no message opened, none edited, none refused. The line carries. Judgment happens at the ends.

**The no-iron discipline.** Cold iron is anathema to the Wild’s rulers — symbol and wound in one — and the Wardens’ answer is total: past the markers, the network carries none. Bronze knives, bone needles, wood and stone and leather, the beautiful iron-free field kit that Guild halls make to Warden pattern and other travelers buy as superstition. It is not superstition. It is manners, of the load-bearing kind: the Warden who walks unarmed-in-iron into sovereign country is making, with every step, the statement the whole tradition rests on — *I am not the claim; I am the conversation* — and the sovereigns, who read everything, read it.

**The diplomacy.** Approaching a sovereign is a craft with a syllabus: the announced approach (never surprise a court); the gift, which must cost the giver something real and take nothing from the sovereign’s ground; the forms of address, learned per sovereign and updated per generation; the shape worn, chosen like formal dress — your own face for hard truth, a granted pelt for trust, never a face-taken form ever, a rule with a story behind it that seconds are told exactly once. And the acceptance, rehearsed until it is real, of *no*. The Wardens’ whole standing at the courts rests on three centuries of taking no for an answer and coming back, next season, polite. It is the one credential no other institution has ever managed to fake.

**Assessment.** The craft the settled world pays for: the Warden walk-and-report that prices the frontier — ground temper, sovereign mood, the exact wrongness of a proposed route — feeding the Companies’ actuaries, the Concord’s premiums, the Lions’ deployments. A Warden assessment moves money three regions wide, the network charges accordingly and spends it on caches, and the tradition’s integrity rule is blunt: assessments report the ground. If the honest report kills your client’s project, the report was free of charge to the ground, and the ground is also a client. This clause is read aloud at every engagement. It has lost the network exactly the business it was written to lose.

## The Sovereigns

The Wardens’ opposite numbers: the Beast Lords and elder powers of the Wild, each a court, a temper, and a standing file on the relay lines. Four define the working map. (Fuller accounts in the Notable Powers chapter; what follows is the network’s own working shorthand.)

**Antler-Who-Counts** holds the deep Verdant Belt: the stable one, the treaty-keeper, the sovereign the whole supplicant tradition was refined against. He counts — everything: trees taken, promises made, seasons kept, the exact accumulating balance of every understanding his court holds with the settled world — and his counting *is* the treaty: there is no document; there is the Antler’s ledger, held in a mind older than the Reckoning, and the network’s Belt circuit exists substantially to keep civilization’s side of the count clean. His *no* is rare, final, and always arithmetically explicable, which makes him, by the standards of this section, nearly restful.

**The Smoking Mother** holds the burned hills of the Salt Reach: the volatile one, grief-tempered, her court’s weather changing in hours — and the network’s standing achievement, renewed each generation, is that the Gorûn routes cross her ground at all. Warden diplomacy at her court is conducted through her creatures, never directly, and priced in patience.

**The Hollow King** holds the Hollow Marches, or the Marches hold him, and the file is the network’s thickest and least conclusive: a sovereign whose alignment with the Shadow is argued by every authority and settled by none, whose movements erase what they cross — and whose seasonal circuit has passed within reach of Three Springs for forty-nine years without touching it, a mercy or an oversight or a *policy* that no theory of him survives contact with. The Marches circuit is the network’s hardest posting. Its Wardens are excused the Gather’s small talk by ancient custom.

**The Patient Stone** holds the high country of the Cradle: the elder power that is not a beast at all — a sovereignty of the mountain itself, slow as strata, addressed by the network across generations rather than seasons: a question laid at its courts in one Warden’s lifetime is answered, sometimes, in her second’s. The Stone’s temper is proverbial (*“the Stone forgives everything, eventually, and forgets nothing, ever”*), its ground is the safest sovereign country in the world for the respectful, and it is currently, for the first time in the network’s records, the addressee of a message from an institution that cannot come and ask in person — see below.

## The Network

There is no Warden headquarters, charter, or rank ladder, and the tradition regards this as a considered position rather than an omission: the network is people, ground, and practices — each Warden holding a **walk** (their ground, inherited, negotiated, or opened), training a **second** when they find one worth the years, and meeting at the **Gathers**: the seasonal regional moots, half logistics and half family, where caches are budgeted, sign is standardized, the sovereign files are read into the collective memory, and the network conducts, around fires and without minutes, the entire governance it has ever needed. A Warden is made by exactly one mechanism: an existing Warden vouches, the Gather concurs, and the new second’s hand is entered in the sign-rolls. A Warden is unmade by the same fire, and it has happened, and the network does not discuss the cases but keeps them, like everything, in the rolls.

Texture, briefly, because the tradition wears little: the iron-free kit; the pelt-cloaks and the walkers’ habit of dressing in layers that come off fast; the **ground-names** — Wardens introduce themselves by walk, not lineage (*“Wren, of the Rust Belt circuit”*), and the network’s rare formal honor is to be known by ground alone; and the speech: the boundary is **the edge**; settled country is **inside**; to die on the walk is to **stay out**; and the network’s valediction, said at partings on the edge for three hundred years, is the whole tradition in four words: *“walk soft, count true.”*

## The Wardens Among the Factions

**The Lions** and the network conduct the frontier’s most necessary difficult marriage: the Lions need the relay lines, the assessments, and the guides; the network needs the Lions’ weight when the Shadow moves; and between them stands the permanent asymmetry that the Lions fight for the inside and the Wardens speak for the edge. The mainline manages it case by case and Warden by Warden. The Unblinking’s file on the network is not thick, which the network’s elders consider the most dangerous compliment they hold.

**The Companies** buy the assessments, resent the integrity clause, and owe the network the two facts their actuaries price highest: which ground is quiet, and why the quiet ground is quiet. The Quickthorn’s borrowed “Warden-Captain” title remains under formal objection, renewed at each Gather with decreasing hope and increasing dryness.

**The Guilds** meet the network at the grow-yards — the Overdrawn Yard’s fence line is currently the most important thirty paces of Guild–Warden cooperation in the world — and in the workshops, where the iron-free kit trade has made Warden-pattern the frontier’s quiet luxury standard.

**The Houses** touch the network at the boundary demesnes, and in one standing heartbreak: House Ferrant, whom the anathema bars absolutely, for whom certain Wardens carry messages into treaty lands under a courtesy the Wild tolerates by pretending not to notice — and who have now asked, through that channel, for the largest courtesy ever requested of it (see below).

**The Orders** and the network share the deep country, the want-nothing temperament, and the observation the Wardens made first and keep closest: *the Wild tolerates the monasteries.* The network’s elders have drawn from this their quietest and largest conclusion — that the land’s objection was never to people, but to claims — and it is the nearest thing the tradition has to a theology, and they test it, gently, in every negotiation, and it has not yet failed.

**The Silvaen** are the relationship the network cannot file: the awakened mimic-plants are of the Wild by any reading the sovereigns use, of civilization by their own moral lights, wardens by their own word — *wardens, not conquerors* — and the network extends to Silvaen individuals full working trust, relay access, and the standing invitation to the Gathers, which the Silvaen accept perhaps once a decade, memorably. On the Quickthorn’s grown lines the two warden traditions have found their first real disagreement, and are conducting it the way both prefer: slowly, at the tree line, in complete sentences.

## Current Fault Lines

- **The Rust Belt Question.** Fadrick has tabled reclamation at Thirstgate, the Lions will need guides, and the network is split down its center — not on whether to help, but on what its own patrols have been confirming for three years: that the Belt’s incursion-country has gone *organized* in a way Wild ground does not, that the sovereigns’ own creatures now shun it, and that whatever is consolidating in there around the old Meridian right-of-way is something the Wild is also watching. The circuit’s last three reports use, for the first time in network records, the same phrase: *not ours, not theirs.*

- **The Withdrawal from the Glass.** Sovereign territories on the Salt Reach margin are shifting — courts relocating away from the Glass Plains, quietly, for the first time in living memory. The Wild does not retreat from civilization. It is retreating from the Plains. The network has forwarded the observation to the Lions and the Vehk with the annotation its elders debated for a month before including: *whatever the trend line ends in, the neighbors expect to be alive for it, and are moving.*

- **Iven Marsh Is Writing.** The Belt circuit’s great Warden — kept in 292 SR, twenty years silent, his ground gone strange and safe in the way of the well-kept — has begun leaving sign on the relay lines. His hand, unmistakable. The strokes, unreadable: the old danger-glyph, modified by additions no living Warden can parse, repeated now at four posts, each nearer the edge. The network’s doctrine says visit. The network’s elders, who knew him, have not yet chosen the visitor, because the doctrine’s second sentence is about visits going badly, and Iven Marsh taught half of them the sign they cannot read.

- **The Herd Question.** House Ferrant has asked, through the courtesy channel, for a message to be carried to the Patient Stone: eleven of Cold Anvil’s finest frames stand facing the deep Cradle, waiting, and the House wishes to ask the mountain *what its country is saying that the herd can hear.* The network has never carried a question to the Stone on behalf of iron. The Stone has never been known to refuse a courteous question. Between those two sentences sits a Gather’s whole agenda, and the elders have concluded, with the tradition’s usual speed, that they will decide by autumn. Possibly next autumn.

- **The New Column.** Antler-Who-Counts has added a column to his count. His court’s creatures have been observed — methodically, at the tree line, in the Antler’s unmistakable census manner — attending the Quickthorn’s rooted lines, the Green Chain’s yards, and, once, a Second Muster watch-post: the made soldiers, being counted. The network does not know what the new column tallies — assets, grievances, kin — and the Belt circuit’s request for clarification received the answer the Antler gives to premature questions, which is the number counted so far, stated once, without units. The number was larger than the made soldiers on the Belt fringe. The circuit is still working out what else is in it.

## The Wardens in Play

**Playing the Shapeshifter.** The class arrives with its lore in its pockets: the hearth-budget is the character sheet’s version of the tradition’s central discipline (one heavy guest or many light — and the choice *is* characterization: the binder with her single lifelong wolf, the walker with a working household, the face-taker with three names and a code), forms are *earned* in the fiction — a pelt granted, a season lived, a face studied — so advancement generates play rather than merely following it, and the class risk is the faction’s own ghost story: every level of shape carried is a step down the road to the Kept, and the campaign’s best quiet pressure is the party noticing their Warden wintering as the wolf a little longer each year. A non-shifter Warden is equally canonical — the network is a craft before it is a class — and a face-taker PC, operating under the written code inside civilization, is the setting’s most interesting social-infiltration frame, because the code is real and the network enforces it.

**What the network pays adventurers to do.** Rarely in coin (the network is cache-rich and Ring-poor); reliably in the frontier’s harder currencies — relay access, sign-literacy, safe ground, introductions, and the assessment discount that Company clients would kill for. Typical work, by tier:

- Walk a resupply circuit: caches to fill, sign to refresh, a relay post to repair — the network’s evaluation contract, and the party learns the public glyphs whether they meant to or not.

- Carry the relay: a message run down the lines through ground gone restless, under the one rule — it travels whole.

- Escort an assessment: guard a Warden pricing a proposed route, and watch her tell the client the truth at the end, and decide what you think of the clause.

- Find a boundary-crosser: the recognition has taken someone’s child past the markers, the town is frightened in the old way, and the network wants its future colleague found first.

- Attend a court: gift-bearers and witnesses at a sovereign negotiation — the party stands very still, carries no iron, and learns what *no* sounds like taken well.

- Walk the Rust Belt circuit’s next confirmation, into ground that is not ours and not theirs, and bring the phrase back with evidence attached.

- Be considered, at a fire, without being asked yet, for the visit to Iven Marsh.

- At the highest tiers: carry the Ferrant question to the Patient Stone — a mission measured in seasons, paid in a House’s gratitude and a mountain’s attention, and remembered by the network for exactly as long as the network lasts.

**Patrons.** *A circuit elder* — the closest the network has to a quest-giver: a Gather’s worth of needs, dispensed over tea, in order of what the party has proven. *A face-taker under code* — the network’s inside-work generates errands that need honest strangers precisely because the face-taker cannot be one. *The Silvaen who attends the Gathers* — once a decade, memorably: a patron whose requests arrive years apart, concern the health of whole landscapes, and pay in the one coin no one else on this list holds — the good opinion of the ground itself.

## Player Toolkit

A Warden character should name their home circuit, the elder who first vouched for them, one public danger-glyph they use often, and one boundary they crossed before they understood it. A Shapeshifter should name every form as a specific relationship: what creature granted or lost the heart, what appetite comes with the shape, and what behavior tells companions the form is lingering too long.

Choose one civilization custom you find comforting, one you find incomprehensible, and one piece of Warden doctrine you interpret differently from your elder. If you are a face-taker, write the code governing consent, duration, and use of another person’s face before the first session.

## GM Toolkit

Wardens can provide route knowledge, assessments, relays, safe caches, boundary etiquette, and introductions to powers no contract reaches. They cannot command the Wild, promise that a known route remains known, or explain every judgment to clients who lack the necessary context.

Portray the Wild as attention and relationship, not generic wilderness hostility. Give each territory a pattern, a courtesy, and a consequence. Telegraph a boundary before punishing its breach. When a sovereign refuses, make the refusal intelligible even when its reasoning is not human.

**GM questions:** What is this ground tolerating, and why? Which old courtesy has become dangerous to keep? What has the Warden noticed but not yet found words for?
