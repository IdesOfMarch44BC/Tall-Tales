# The Peoples of the Frontier

Seventeen peoples share the settled world and its edges, and no two of them agree on what the world is for. This is not a flaw in the setting; it is the setting. The factions organize people by what they do. Species organize them by what they *are* — by the shape of the body, the length of the life, the country that made them — and the two systems cut across each other at every angle, so that a Korr and a Barudrim may both wear the Lion, and mean entirely different things by it.

A word on where these peoples live, because geography is not incidental here. Each biome has its peoples, and the regional map of the world (detailed in its own chapter) is also a map of who you will meet: the **Frozen Coast** is Dóm, Uln, and Wyr country; the **Salt Reach** deserts belong to the Gorûn, Serekh, and Tikka; the **Verdant Belt** forests to the Brindel, Lemari, and Silvaen; the **High Cradle** mountains to the Chyrr, Korr, and Squar; and the **Iron Reach** plains to the Barudrim, Mairin, Nol, Pelorim, and the humans who are found, restlessly, everywhere. Peoples travel, of course, and the rails have scattered every species into every station town. But a people is shaped by its homeland long after it leaves, and the deepest thing you can know about most of them is the country that is no longer under their feet.

What follows is written for the table: who these peoples are, why they leave home to become the sort of person a campaign is about, and how the wider world receives them when they do.

## Peoples of the Frozen Coast

### Dóm

The Dóm are lithe folk of the high ice, born to motion where others freeze. Compact and flexible, with a low center of gravity and wide, resilient feet, they skim across hard snow and bare ice as though skating upon it — carving slopes, leaping ravines, riding shifting drifts with a precision that looks, to outsiders, reckless to the point of suicide. To the Dóm it is discipline refined past thought. The cold is not an enemy to be endured but a partner to be led, and a Dóm who has learned to lead it moves through the killing country of the north the way a dancer moves through a room everyone else must cross on their hands and knees.

Among the Dóm, grace is survival and survival is beauty. Elegance is not ornament but proof: clean lines, controlled momentum, the absence of a single wasted motion in a landscape that charges for waste in lives. Over generations this reverence hardened into doctrine. Those who moved most beautifully were praised, then elevated, then expected to lead — and so skill became authority, and beauty became an obligation, and a gift became a debt payable for a lifetime.

The debt found its heaviest expression in the Dóm’s oldest calling: they are the messengers of the frozen world. Long before roads or borders, the Dóm carried words, warnings, and promises across ice fields no one else could cross, and a message delivered cleanly — on time, without falter or excess — was held as beautiful as a flawless descent. The best couriers were not merely fast but precise, reliable, and invisible, leaving no trace behind them but a fulfilled obligation. To be entrusted with a message is the profoundest honor Dóm culture offers and one of its heaviest burdens: a courier is expected to succeed regardless of weather, injury, or cost, because a delayed message can end lives and a lost one can end alliances. Many of the most graceful Dóm grow up under a pressure they never chose, knowing their gift may bind them to a lifetime of carrying other people’s urgencies across the ice.

Dóm society is deeply communal — warmth, labor, and knowledge are shared without question — but admiration is not, and status is fragile. Injury, age, or a single deviation from accepted form can quietly strip a Dóm of standing, and those who shine too brightly are pressed into leadership and courier-work they never sought, with refusal read as immaturity or betrayal. So the Dóm leave for opposite reasons: some are shunned, deemed broken or inelegant when they can no longer embody the ideal; others are idealized so relentlessly that departure is the only way to reclaim their own lives; and a few simply carry one message too many and refuse, at last, to let grace be measured only by how much weight it can bear.

Other cultures idolize Dóm beauty and hire it eagerly — as couriers, scouts, and guides — praising a “natural grace” while ignoring the discipline and cost that produced it. On the Frozen Coast this reduction has hardened into something like a profession: the Witnesses of the Lion’s Iceharbor chapter maintains a standing courier compact with the Dóm of the Long Slope, and a Dóm bearing the chapter’s iron-lantern token is owed shelter at any funded church in the north — an obligation the Dóm have famously never had to invoke twice. Whether admired or feared, the Dóm are too often made into spectacle. Among themselves they know the truth: grace keeps them alive, but obligation decides who must move, and who may finally rest.

> **Perfection at All Costs.** When a Dóm makes a skill check, they may “double down.” If the first check was a failure, a successful second check makes it a success, but a failed second check makes something very disadvantageous happen (GM’s discretion). If the first check was a success, a failed second check makes it a failure, but a successful second check makes something very advantageous happen (GM’s discretion).

### Uln

The Uln are small, stocky folk who dwell inside the glaciers, carving vast tunnel-cities through ice that would crush or freeze any other people. Their pale-blue skin and compact frames are adapted to a narrow band of cold, a few degrees on either side of freezing, held year-round; and to the Uln the glacier is not hostile terrain but a structure — stable, predictable, and honest, provided it is treated with the respect an honest thing deserves.

Uln society is founded on precision, because the alternative is drowning. Every tunnel, chamber, and living space is measured, reinforced, and monitored, and heat is both life and death: warmth sustains the city, and excess warmth brings the ceiling down. From this the Uln built an entire culture of thermal discipline, treating heat as a finite resource to be accounted for, shared, and conserved, and teaching children before anything else that recklessness with warmth endangers not one life but all of them. The discipline extends into their craft. The Uln do not forge with open flame; they **cold-forge**, shaping metal and stone through pressure, vibration, controlled entropy, and geothermal or magical heat regulated never to cross a safe threshold — aligning materials at scales the lowland smiths cannot match, coaxing form rather than melting toward it. Uln work is famed for its resilience in deep cold and infamous for its fragility in heat, a faithful reflection of the principles that made it. The Landed Guilds classify the art as a branch of harmonics and have quietly conceded, in the standing arrangement by which the lowland halls license Uln resonance-work rather than attempt it, that on this one thing the glacier smiths are simply better.

Fire is mistrusted to the point of taboo. Open flame is rare in an Uln city and tightly regulated where it exists, and to wield it carelessly is a moral failing, not a mere mistake — a willingness to gamble with the lives of others. The mistrust runs into the language itself, which is dense with metaphors of heat and balance: ideas “run hot,” plans are left to “cool,” and waste of any kind is condemned as “burning the city.” Yet Uln life is not joyless. Their communities prize cleverness, foresight, and shared responsibility; every Uln knows the basics of structural maintenance and emergency cooling; and when disaster comes it is met not with panic but with practiced, methodical coordination, because survival, to the Uln, is not heroism. It is a procedure done correctly.

Uln adventurers are uncommon and faintly unsettling to their kin. Some leave to study foreign technologies and heat-based magic, hoping to understand a threat before it reaches the ice; others are exiles who broke a taboo, or who committed the quieter offense of asking whether the glacier should define an entire life. To live outside controlled cold is uncomfortable for an Uln, sometimes frightening — but for some, the knowledge is worth the discomfort.

> **Icecunning.** Ulns automatically succeed on Perceive checks for unusual conditions in ice or snow, including ice or snow traps and natural or supernatural hazards such as thin ice or blood-snow. An Uln who merely comes within 10 feet of unusual ice construction or a hazard gains information as though actively searching.

### Wyr

The Wyr are an artificial people, fashioned of carved wood and bound magic by hands long vanished from history. No two tales agree on who made them, or why. What is known is that the Wyr were not made as a *people* but as *solutions* — answers to problems that no longer exist, or perhaps were never solved at all.

Each Wyr bears a **Maker-mark**, a sigil worked into the frame at the moment of creation. The marks vary in form and function, and scholars believe they encode different makers, traditions, or philosophies of design. Some are inert, their purpose lost. Others remain empowered, granting quiet enhancements to perception, memory, or capability. Among the Wyr the marks are studied, debated, and endlessly argued over — not as proof of destiny but as fragments of intent left behind by absent parents. A Wyr thinks constantly; the mind is precise, recursive, and tireless, and that cognition produces heat, a flaw or a safeguard built into the design. In the cold — above all in the Arctic — the Wyr are at their sharpest, thought moving with effortless clarity. In warm lands they slow, each idea forced through layers of resistance, and many Wyr read this not as a curse but as a reminder that they were not built to be everywhere or to do everything.

Wyr society is quiet, patient, and introspective. They do not breed; they endure. New Wyr are rare, and the loss of one is felt keenly, so Wyr communities place enormous value on memory, record, and shared understanding. They argue often — never loudly, always persistently — over purpose, obligation, and whether a made thing owes anything to a maker who is gone. Some search obsessively for their makers. Others reject the question outright and define themselves by action rather than origin. For all their artifice the Wyr feel deeply, and express it sparingly: affection shown through maintenance and repair, grief recorded rather than wailed, joy arriving as the sudden clarity of a problem resolved. Many carry a quiet, unprovable belief that someone once cared enough to make them — and that belief, true or not, is what keeps them from feeling wholly alone.

To outsiders the Wyr are unsettling: wooden figures who speak softly, move deliberately, and watch without blinking. Some see tools awaiting reclamation; others, proof that life can be manufactured. The Wyr reject both. They are neither property nor miracle. They are people who began as an intention and must now decide what to do with it — and they sit, whether they wish to or not, at the center of three of the setting’s oldest questions. The Academic Enclaves note, with a discomfort that has not eased in three centuries, that *no Wyr has ever erupted* — that the one people made rather than born is also the one people arcane magic has never touched, a fact each school of thought claims as proof of its own theory and none can explain. The scholars who chase the Makers note the resemblance between the Wyr’s vanished creators and the personified Machine of the Material World, and the buried Maker-Sites where the old arts still hum, and House Ferrant’s whispered origin, and decline, so far, to say aloud what the resemblance might mean. The Wyr, asked their opinion on any of it, tend to reply that they find it restful to be the one people whose relationship with magic is entirely voluntary — an answer that is not data, and is remembered longer than most data.

> **Maker-Mark.** A Wyr chooses two alignments at character creation, one dominant and one recessive. The Wyr may make saving throws of the recessive alignment using their dominant alignment instead. (Example: a Wyr whose dominant alignment is Heavens and recessive alignment is Wild may make Wild saving throws with their Heavens alignment.)

## Peoples of the Salt Reach

### Gorûn

The Gorûn are massive desert nomads whose size is not excess but survival. Beneath their broad frames and thick limbs lie vast reserves of water, and every carried pound is a buffer against drought, distance, and failure. To the Gorûn, bulk is not indulgence; it is preparation, and a people whose homeland offers no margin for error learns early to carry its margin in its own body.

Gorûn society is built on movement and stewardship. They are herders and shepherds of hardy desert beasts, guiding great caravans along ancient routes between oases, seasonal pasture, and sheltered valleys — routes that are not merely paths but living traditions, learned by repetition and kept by ritual, so that a Gorûn who forgets a way or leads a herd astray carries the shame long after the desert has swallowed the tracks. Their strength makes them invaluable as laborers, guards, and mercenaries, and many take that work from necessity rather than love, because few other livelihoods can feed a people so large in a land so poor. This has bred an uneasy reputation: the Gorûn are seen as imposing, even threatening, yet are rarely aggressive, holding violence as a last resort for when endurance and negotiation have both failed. The Mercenary Companies prize them accordingly and, the Gorûn note dryly, understand them not at all — a Gorûn signs on to feed a herd three regions away, and is read by the Rolls as hired muscle who happens to be enormous.

The Gorûn write their lives on their bodies. Paint and removable dye shield the skin from sun and sand and shift with season and mood, favored by the young and the traveling; **tattoos** are permanent, and permanence is reserved for what will not change — rites of passage, completed migrations, droughts survived, vows made and kept. To deface a tattoo is a profound taboo, a kind of murder of memory, and to add one lightly is reckless. An elder’s markings are a life legible at a glance: where they have gone, what they have endured, what they have sworn never to forget. (The Noble Houses, whose Dynasts wear their own histories in plate and scar and shed skin, would recognize the grammar exactly, though neither people has ever remarked on the resemblance to the other.)

Beneath the stewardship runs a quiet resentment. The Gorûn are leaned on — as muscle, as living cisterns, as guides — and rarely offered belonging in return, and many feel trapped between the role of provider and a world that sees only the endurance. Those who leave often go seeking a life where their strength is chosen rather than assumed. Wherever they go they carry the shepherd’s habits: watching the horizon, counting the supplies, measuring time by survival rather than comfort — and, on the Salt Reach, the rarest Gorûn skill of all, the patience that lets a caravan cross the burned hills of the Smoking Mother’s country at all, negotiated season by season through her creatures, in a diplomacy the Frontier Wardens themselves regard as a standing miracle.

> **Large Size.** When a Gorûn makes a skill check involving grappling, lifting, or raw strength, they may add an additional die.

### Serekh

The Serekh are cactus-bodied people who treat water the way other peoples treat crops. To the Serekh, moisture is not merely survival — it is a thing to be cultivated, guarded, and shared, and a people who think this way build their whole society around the careful management of every drop. Serekh settlements farm dew and rainfall, tend hidden cisterns, and shape the terrain itself to gather water, and a well-kept Serekh community can outlast droughts that would erase its neighbors.

Because water is life and life is stored at home, a wandering Serekh is not read as a drifter but as a **scout**. One who leaves carries only a small reserve within the body and ventures out in search of new sources — an undiscovered spring, a buried aquifer, a region where life might take hold again — on journeys dangerous and uncertain and, when they succeed, deeply honored, because a water-seeker who finds a new source may save an entire people. Rarer still are the **moisture-oracles**, seers who enter a fragile, wilted trance through ritual desiccation and wake with senses unnaturally attuned to humidity, wind, and the water hidden beneath the earth; guided by them, Serekh caravans have crossed deserts held to be impassable.

The Serekh are slow, and mistaken for helpless, and both impressions are corrections waiting to happen. The desert skies hold predatory avians that stoop on the Serekh to drink the water in their bodies, and when the threat comes from above the Serekh answer with instinct older than strategy: the elders, spine-hardened and broad, cluster into a living barricade around the soft and the young and hold until the sky gives up. To outsiders the Serekh seem deliberate past the point of stubbornness — slow to move, careful to speak, unwilling to waste a single effort — but once a Serekh has chosen where to stand, they are as difficult to uproot as the desert itself.

> **Spines.** Thousands of tough spines cover the Serekh body. They allow a Serekh to deal lethal damage even while unarmed, and grant +1 Defend against melee attacks.

### Tikka

The Tikka are small, quick desert folk whose survival rests on vigilance, ingenuity, and an intimate grasp of what larger peoples overlook. Mouse-like in form — big ears, nimble hands, alert and constant eyes — they live in burrows beneath the sand, in stone outcrops, and in the ruins of the deep desert, and where a traveler sees empty waste, a Tikka sees a landscape crowded with opportunity, danger, and resources waiting to be turned to use.

Tikka culture is defined by preparation, and its first lesson is that size is a disadvantage only for those who fail to plan. Their settlements are labyrinths — pressure plates, collapsing tunnels, false chambers, concealed escapes — and their craft turns bone, chitin, leather, and scavenged metal into mechanisms that favor cleverness over force: traps, tools, devices. A Tikka warren is never taken by force alone; it defeats intruders by patience, misdirection, and exhaustion, wearing an enemy down until the desert finishes what the traps began. Socially the Tikka are cautious but not cruel, preferring avoidance to confrontation and misdirection to violence, and when they must fight they fight on their own terms — from concealment, and gone before the retaliation lands. Larger peoples sometimes read this as treachery. The Tikka read it as survival without waste, which is the only kind the desert permits.

Tikka leave the warrens for many reasons: engineers eager to test their designs against a wider world, scouts mapping new routes, exiles who broke a trust and forfeited the shared caches that sustain the rest. And a few leave for a subtler ache — weariness of living beneath the world, forever alert — wanting, just once, to know what it feels like to be seen without immediately being hunted. The Trade Companies and the Landed Guilds both court Tikka engineers hard; the finest works-engineer on the register, Hesper Coil of Vault & Coil, is Tikka, and every Tikka apprentice in a Guild hall knows it.

> **Slight Build.** When a Tikka makes a skill check involving squeezing through tight spaces, evading notice, or nimbleness, they may add an additional die.

## Peoples of the Verdant Belt

### Brindel

The Brindel are small, sharp-minded folk who thrive where the land grows thick and tangled — briar thickets, scrub forest, overgrown ruin, dense hedgerow — carving hidden warrens beneath root and brush and turning the landscape itself into a maze of narrow paths, concealed pits, and disguised traps. They have a practical genius for contraption and ambush: snares of thorn-vine, collapsing tunnels, swinging deadfalls, hidden slits for arrows. What reads to an outsider as chaotic wilderness is, more often, a defensive network arranged with intimate care, and in tangled country where larger folk stumble and bleed, the Brindel move with confidence and control.

Their society grows fast. Brindel mature quickly and raise large broods, and their warrens crowd and spill, so that younger Brindel are expected to push outward into unclaimed thicket and abandoned land — an expansion that has scattered Brindel-kind across many regions, appearing suddenly in places others judged too dense or too poor to settle. And for all their reputation for cunning traps and unruly warrens, the Brindel hold a refined aesthetic that few outsiders share: where others see rot and damp and infestation, a Brindel sees intricate beauty — the symmetry of a web, the patterns of moss, the iridescence of a beetle’s shell, the strange architecture of fungi rising through shadowed soil. Their art imitates these small wonders, in web-etched armor and beetle-shell jewelry and cloth dyed the deep greens and browns of the forest floor, and the same sensibility makes them the finest readers of undergrowth alive, able to judge the health of a thicket from its insects and fungi and creeping vines, to know which mushrooms glow, which vines hold a snare, which hollow roots hide burrowing prey.

Many Brindel leave as part of that restless expansion; others simply grow curious about the world beyond the briars. Away from the protection of thorn and tunnel, a Brindel adventurer leans on the skills that sustain the whole people: ingenuity, improvisation, and a gift for finding opportunity precisely where others see none.

> **Brushcunning.** Brindels automatically succeed on Perceive checks for unusual conditions in undergrowth, briars, or similar dense vegetation, including thicket or thorn traps and natural or supernatural hazards such as bleeding vines. A Brindel who merely comes within 10 feet of unusual brush gains information as though actively searching.

### Lemari

The Lemari are arboreal folk whose lives unfold high in the branches rather than on the forest floor. Light-limbed and long-tailed, keen of balance and strong of grip, they move through the canopy along routes invisible from the ground, leaping and climbing where others could not walk. To an outsider the forest is a dense tangle; to the Lemari it is a layered world of paths, platforms, and hidden clearings suspended in living wood.

Unlike many forest peoples the Lemari do not prize stillness or solitary wisdom. Their society is communal and intensely vocal, conversation flowing constantly through whistles, layered calls, rhythmic knocks against the branches, and complex spoken tones that carry tree to tree, meaning often emerging from several voices at once in patterns outsiders cannot follow. Silence unsettles them; a quiet forest is a forest with something wrong in it. Their communities center on immense ancient trees, one to a clan, shaped over generations into dwellings, ropeways, and living bridges so that a whole settlement can exist without ever clearing the floor below — and these great trees are more than homes. When a Lemari dies, the body is laid in the soil around the roots of the clan-tree, in the belief that the dead nourish the tree that sheltered them living, so that ancestors sustain descendants; and over centuries the great trees grow, quite literally, from the bodies of those who lived beneath them. The fruit of such a tree is treated with reverence, a living link between the past and the present.

Because their lives are so bound to place, most Lemari rarely leave the home canopy, and those who do are unusual figures — scouts, wanderers, the displaced survivors of clans whose trees have died or burned. The ground-world feels exposed and wrong to them, but some accept that discomfort to protect the forests that sustain their people. Other folk often mistake the Lemari for the elves of old story and call them so out of habit; the Lemari neither embrace nor much resist the name, but do not use it among themselves — to them, identity is not a broad species but the specific living tree that anchors a clan and the voices that share its branches.

> **Prehensile Tail.** A Lemari’s tail acts as a third grasping limb, able to hold (but not wield) a small object. A Lemari may brachiate — swing from vines — at normal movement speed and without an Athletics check.

### Silvaen

The Silvaen are living plant-beings shaped by imitation. Rooted in forests, glades, and deep valleys, they grow by mimicking the creatures around them. Some take the forms of beasts — stags of bark and leaf, serpents of vine and thorn — but these are little more than instinct-driven growths. Only those Silvaen whose forms echo thinking peoples awaken as persons: a Silvaen that grows with the posture, symmetry, and expressive features of sentient creatures develops self-awareness, memory, language, and moral understanding. This is not taught. It emerges. Among the Silvaen, sentience is not a birthright but a rare flowering, and the non-sentient Silvaen are treated with care and respect but are not kin in the same way — and no Silvaen claims ownership of the mindless forest-shapes.

Silvaen society is therefore built on attentiveness and restraint. They do not cultivate growth indiscriminately; forests under their care are managed with subtle pruning, careful imitation, and quiet guidance of which forms are permitted to emerge, and a Silvaen who encourages excess bestial growth is reckless, risking the spread of powerful, mindless things that unbalance the land. Identity, too, is bound to place: each Silvaen is tied to the region that shaped its form — the animals, peoples, and rhythms it first mirrored — and straying too far weakens it, body and spirit, as the sustaining patterns fade. This makes the Silvaen slow to travel and loath to interfere beyond their borders. They call themselves wardens, not conquerors, and they mean it as a description of the whole species.

That self-description places the Silvaen on the exact fault line the whole setting turns on, and they know it. A people grown by the forest, bound to the forest, calling themselves its wardens — are they *of* the Wild, in the way the Beast Lords are of the Wild? The question has no settled answer, and the Silvaen live inside it. The Frontier Wardens extend Silvaen individuals full working trust, relay access, and a standing invitation to the Gathers that the Silvaen accept perhaps once a decade, memorably; and on the matter of the Quickthorn’s grown soldiers — thorn-warriors cultivated by the thousand in Guild vat-yards, the industrial mass-manufacture of almost-minds — the two warden traditions have discovered their first true disagreement, and are conducting it the way both prefer: slowly, at the tree line, in complete sentences. The distinction between sentient and non-sentient Silvaen runs as a quiet grief through the whole people. Some feel guilt for an awareness others did not receive; some fear that awareness is not a gift but a fall, a divergence from the forest’s pure and thoughtless harmony; and some believe, most hopefully, that their sentience is simply the forest learning, at last, to care. To outsiders the Silvaen are unnerving — people of bark and leaf who stand too still, whose faces seem half-remembered, whose eyes give back the forest to anyone who looks. Those who earn their trust find them patient, thoughtful, and deeply moral: beings who understand, better than most, that growth without thought is dangerous, and that thought without roots is worse.

> **Master Mimics.** Whenever a Silvaen observes another creature using a skill, the Silvaen may also use that skill at two dots less effectiveness — unless the Silvaen already has more dots in that skill.

## Peoples of the High Cradle

### Chyrr

The Chyrr are a six-limbed people of the deep earth, born beneath mountain and desert where stone remembers longer than flesh. Their bodies declare their nature: two powerful legs rooted for leverage, an upper pair of strong arms for labor and decisive action, and a lower pair of smaller arms for balance, precision, and readiness — always readiness. They are built not for comfort but for alertness.

To the Chyrr, predation is not cruelty but responsibility. Their minds do not sort the world into safe and dangerous, peaceful and violent; danger is constant, and peace is merely the name for the moment before the necessary response. To relax vigilance is indistinguishable, to a Chyrr, from negligence, and complacency is not a flaw of temperament but a moral failure — an indulgence that invites collapse and endangers everyone. This belief shapes every stone of their lives. Their great cities lie deep, layered with redundancy, watchways, and sealed passages, each built to survive loss without unraveling; knowledge is distributed rather than centralized, and no single chamber or person is indispensable, because survival is collective, continuous, and never assumed. They do not seek conflict, but they prepare for it without apology, and they strike first — not from aggression but because hesitation multiplies risk. A Chyrr battle is an ambush by design: sudden, overwhelming, brief. Once resistance breaks, they withdraw, for prolonged violence is waste and excess destruction is danger, and an enemy routed is no longer a threat and therefore no longer their concern.

Their physiology enables labor and war that unsettle surface-dwellers — upper arms bearing heavy tools and weapons, lower arms bracing and stabilizing and acting on their own — and what others see as monstrous excess is, to the Chyrr, mere efficiency. Their warrior caste, the **Khet’kha**, bear the heaviest share of that readiness; to be Khet’kha is no lightly sought honor but a duty accepted with grim clarity: to live always prepared to kill, so that others may sleep. Among themselves the Chyrr are restrained and quiet, valuing precise speech, economical motion, and foresight over passion, and discouraging individual glory — success belongs to the system that made it inevitable, and those who chase recognition over preparedness are seen as dangerous, not rebellious but unstable. Yet the culture carries its own tension: some Chyrr fear that endless vigilance erodes memory, art, and joy, that a people who live only to prevent disaster may forget why survival was worth wanting; others answer that such thoughts are luxuries of the safe, and that the world punishes those who forget how fast safety ends.

This is precisely the friction the settled world feels when it meets them. The Chyrr strike-first doctrine grinds against the Landed Guilds’ hard-won restraint — the Guilds’ whole neutrality is built on *not* escalating, and a Chyrr reads the pause before a necessary blow as the negligence that gets people killed — and the Chyrr Watchposts along the Cradle’s edges are famed as much for their prickly diplomacy as for their impregnability. Chyrr adventurers are rare. Those who leave do so because they believe the surface world hides threats their people cannot afford to ignore, or because they can no longer endure a life in which readiness never ends. Wherever they go, they watch first, act second, and rest only when every shadow has been measured.

> **Four Arms.** A Chyrr has two upper arms and two smaller lower arms. The upper pair functions as ordinary arms. The lower pair can hold (but not wield as weapons) Light objects, items, or shields. A Chyrr may use the lower arms to counterbalance weapons, allowing them to wield two two-handed weapons simultaneously.

### Korr

The Korr are a short-lived people, and they know it.

Avian in form, hollow-boned, broad of wing, with a metabolism that burns like a forge from birth to death, the Korr live at a pace other peoples find exhausting to witness. Their hearts race, their eyes race faster; they eat constantly, sleep briefly, and are rarely still — not from anxiety, but because stillness carries, for a Korr, the specific weight of time deliberately not used. A Korr lives perhaps forty years, and in that span will see more sunrises than most peoples see in twice the time, and forget almost none of them. Their home is the high places — sheer faces, wind-carved spires, the narrow ledges where the air thins and the horizon runs out. Korr settlements are vertical things, carved into rock and linked by routes impassable to anything without wings or a lifetime’s practice, and from those heights the Korr **watch** — not passively, not peacefully, but with the fierce attention of people who know their time is short and have decided that witness is how they will spend it.

This is not abstract wisdom handed down by elders. It was earned, generation by generation, through the accumulation of a specific grief: the Korr have watched things happen that they could not, or did not, stop. Their oral histories — passed with urgency from the dying to the young — are full of such moments. The storm that took the lowland village. The army that crossed the pass. The rail line that cut the valley before anyone descended to object. The Korr saw all of it, and remember all of it, and what to do with that memory is the central and unresolved argument of their culture. Elders teach that restlessness is the enemy of clear sight: a Korr who cannot be still cannot truly watch, and a Korr who cannot watch has wasted the one advantage height affords. To the elders, height is not habitat but *epistemology* — to be above the struggle is to see it whole, and to descend is to trade understanding for mere proximity. It is not a comfortable philosophy to live inside when you are young, burning with fuel, and already aware that your life is a fraction spent.

The young feel the restlessness as physical pressure, and their metabolism does not distinguish the urge to cross three valleys in an afternoon from the urge to leave the cliffs entirely and see what lies beyond the range. To the elders these are different things — discipline redirected, discipline abandoned. To the young they feel identical. Some channel the pressure into obsessive watchfulness and become the most rigorous observers their settlements have known; others cannot, and find the horizon’s questions louder than the duty to stay and answer them. Korr flight itself is a sprint, not a soar: powerful, fast, and expensive, burning reserves that take hours to rebuild, so that a long journey becomes an alternation of sharp flight and hard hiking, and a Korr who has pushed too far will drop, land, and eat anything to hand with cheerful single-mindedness until the fire is fed. This makes them formidable in short engagements and impractical over long ones — which the elders cite as proof that patience remains a virtue, and the young cite as proof that the brief and intense is still worth doing.

Korr who leave are not exiles, but they are watched go with a specific sorrow. They are not wrong to leave, most elders will quietly admit; they are only choosing breadth over depth, many things seen against one place known completely, and whether that trade is wise is a judgment only the dying can render, and the dying rarely have time left to speak it. There is one road the elders find hardest to name, and it is the one that runs to the Witnesses of the Lion. Two peoples in all the world have organized themselves around witness — the Korr from the heights, the Lions in the thick of it — and they have argued the meaning of the word for a century, the Korr holding that true witness requires the whole seen from above and undeceived, the Lions holding that witness which will not close the distance is only spectation wearing a serious face. The argument is conducted with complete mutual respect and no progress whatsoever, and meanwhile the young Korr who leave the cliffs are drawn to the Circuit Rider’s road beyond any other calling, finding in it a way to spend a short life seeing *closely* that their own elders cannot quite bring themselves to condemn. The most famous of them served thirty-one years — most of a Korr life — and her home cliff entered her name in its watch-records as *one who descended, and saw*. The elders watch them go. They remember every one.

> **Flight.** Korr have a fly speed of 3 tiles with average maneuverability. Flight is metabolically expensive. Out of combat, a Korr may fly for a number of minutes equal to their dots in Athletics (minimum 1) before landing to rest an equal time. In combat, a Korr may fly for a number of rounds equal to their dots in Athletics (minimum 1), then must land and cannot move for an equal number of rounds.

### Squar

The Squar are immense, powerfully built nomads of the northern mountains — towering figures of fur and muscle who move with a silence that defies belief. To glimpse a Squar on a ridge at dusk, or slipping between the trees, is to feel certain you were not meant to see them at all; and by the time anyone comes to search, there is nothing left but uncertainty and the fading sense of having been watched.

For all their size and strength the Squar do not dominate their land. They endure it. Their culture is built around absence, on the conviction that anything fully known will in time be hunted, claimed, or destroyed. They travel ancient routes layered with hidden burrows, caches, and shelters — some known only to one, others shared sparingly among kin — and even within a band, knowledge is distributed rather than centralized, not from mistrust but from resilience: no single loss should doom the whole. Strength itself is restrained. Feats of power are performed in private, boastfulness is treated as dangerous folly, and a Squar noticed too often brings risk on everyone. Children learn early that their size is as much liability as gift, and that survival lies in patience, misdirection, and the discipline of choosing when *not* to act. To outsiders this reads as myth-making. To the Squar it is the plain difference between being feared and being found.

So the Squar exist half as people and half as rumor, and they cultivate the uncertainty on purpose, leaving just enough sign to inspire doubt and caution and never enough to constitute proof. Legends of towering watchers and vanished camps guard them better than any wall. But the invisibility has a cost. Whole routes are forgotten. Names fade. And some Squar carry a private dread that one day they will survive so completely that they are erased — a people who vanished not by dying but by succeeding at disappearance. Squar adventurers are rare and quietly controversial, for to walk openly is to choose visibility, and visibility invites the danger the whole culture is built to avoid. Some leave believing secrecy has gone too far, that strength should sometimes be used in the open; others are exiled for revealing themselves, or for refusing to stay quiet about what they have done. Every Squar who walks among other peoples carries the same unanswered question: how much of myself can be seen before the myth breaks?

> **Living Myth.** Once per scene, as a free action, a Squar may become invisible to anyone not watching them directly. Hostile creatures who could not see the Squar at the moment of activation lose track of them entirely until the Squar reveals themselves, and Track checks made against the Squar take a $`-2`$ penalty for the next 24 hours.

## Peoples of the Iron Reach

### Barudrim

The Barudrim are towering plainsfolk of immense strength and endurance, broad-framed to carry weight, weather storms, and stand fast against beast and war alike. From childhood they are taught that strength is neither blessing nor birthright but **responsibility**: those who can bear the most must, not for glory alone but because others depend on it.

Barudrim culture takes danger as a proving ground rather than a curse. Life is to be lived loudly and tested against hardship, monsters, and the unknown, and hunts, long journeys, and feats of endurance are not recklessness but rites through which a Barudrim earns story, scars, and standing. A life without deeds worth retelling is, to them, a life unfinished; fear is not denied but laughed at, sung over, and met spear in hand. Yet the hunger for glory is bound tight to duty. The Barudrim spend little worry on their own safety and enormous care on their people — risking oneself is expected, risking others for pride unforgivable — and their strength exists so that the young may grow, the old may rest, and the community may endure. A Barudrim who courts danger without purpose is not celebrated but pitied. Their settlements are communal and boisterous, full of smoke and song and the trophies of hard wins; armor is patched and reforged rather than replaced, weapons carry the marks of many fights, and every piece of gear tells a story, so that children grow among half-finished projects and half-exaggerated tales, learning early that scars are lessons and laughter keeps despair at bay. Among the Barudrim, silence is more dangerous than noise.

Most Barudrim now live alongside agricultural allies who feed them, and in return become professional protectors — not for love of violence but because their culture leaves few other ways to honor strength, and to stand idle while others suffer is the deepest shame. They are, unsurprisingly, among the most sought soldiers on the Mercenary Rolls, and the finest of them command; the General of the Long March, the largest muster in the trade, is Barudrim, and holds her title with the granite patience her people are known for. Barudrim leave their own for many reasons: some hunt greater threats and grander sagas than the plains can offer; some weary of a culture that never slows, where joy and danger are inseparable and rest itself feels like failure; and a few leave for the quietest fear of all — that their love for one person might, one day, outweigh their duty to the many.

> **Defiant Heart.** Once per scene, when reduced to half hit points or below, a Barudrim may take a free action to gain a $`+1`$ morale bonus on attack rolls, damage, and saving throws until the end of their next turn.

### Human

Humans are among the most widely dispersed peoples in the world, found from deep forest to windswept plain to the harshest desert. Unlike most peoples they are specialized for no single habitat; they are defined instead by a combination of endurance, resilience, and adaptability that lets them survive where others falter. The human body is built for persistence, able to cross great distances on foot and to endure heat, fatigue, and injury with remarkable tenacity — among the animals, humans are the great long-distance runners, able to pursue prey or cross open country long after faster creatures have spent themselves. That refusal to stop has shaped everything else about them.

Human societies are rarely still. They expand, migrate, rebuild, and reinvent at a speed that startles longer-lived peoples — cities rising and falling inside a few generations, traditions turning over as fast as the land beneath them — and where others see instability, humans tend to see opportunity. This restless adaptability lets them pursue nearly any path: scholar, warrior, explorer, craftsman, or mystic with equal ease. Other peoples master a specific environment or discipline more completely, but humans hold the singular knack of enduring hardship long enough to learn, and adapt, and endure a little longer.

It is worth saying plainly what that restlessness has meant for this particular world, because the setting is quietly built on it. The expansion the Wild resists — the rails driven into sovereign country, the Companies founded and the frontier fictions maintained, the endless pressure of settlement against a wilderness that pushes back — is overwhelmingly a *human* enterprise. Humans are the species most likely to be found unaffiliated and the species most likely to have built the institution doing the affiliating, the ones who staff the Companies and the Lions and the boards in disproportionate number, and the ones whose talent for outgrowing their own arrangements is precisely why the arrangements keep multiplying. Longer-lived peoples watch human institutions rise and fall and rise again with a weariness humans rarely notice; the Wild, if the Wardens are right about anything, does not distinguish among the peoples of the settled world so much as it distinguishes the *claim* — and the claim, more often than not, is human-shaped. Human adventurers are rarely the strongest or the most graceful in a company. They are, reliably, the last to give up, and the frontier is the monument to what that stubbornness builds and refuses to stop building.

> **Built for Endurance.** A human may travel 2 hours longer per day before risking exhaustion checks, and lasts an additional round at or below 0 hit points before dying.

### Mairin

The Mairin are small-statured plainsfolk whose lives are shaped by cultivation, patience, and continuity. Where others see open land, the Mairin see soil, seasons, and fragile potential. Their settlements are modest but meticulously planned, sustained by generations of accumulated knowledge, preserved seed, and careful record — the quiet heart of plains survival, feeding not only themselves but everyone who passes through.

Mairin society rests on a hard belief: that survival depends on stability, and stability depends on restraint. Deep bonds beyond their own people — romantic ones above all — are seen as a threat to that balance, because the Mairin fear grief more than hunger. A single loss can ripple outward and unravel years of careful labor and shared trust, and a love that would pull a Mairin away from home, or bind them too tightly to a wandering life, is treated as a danger not only to the individual but to the fields and families left behind. Mairin who form such attachments are rarely cast out, but they are gently and persistently encouraged to leave “for everyone’s sake,” and many become adventurers not from wanderlust but because staying would mean asking others to carry the cost of their own choices. Even far from home, most Mairin keep the habits of care and preparation that once sustained whole communities — the full pack, the counted supply, the small handicraft made against a need not yet arrived.

There is a puzzle here that the Mairin share, unknowing, with the Pelorim, and that in-world scholars have begun to notice: two plains peoples, independently, have arrived at the same wariness of deep attachment — the Mairin fearing the ripple of grief, the Pelorim fearing the pull toward stillness — as though something in the long memory of the plains taught both the same lesson in different words. Neither people claims kinship with the other. The convergence sits there anyway, unexplained, the kind of quiet thing a curious party might one day find the bottom of.

> **Always Prepared.** Once per day, a Mairin may produce from their pack one mundane item — preserved food, a simple tool, or a small handicraft — worth no more than 1 Ring. This represents the Mairin habit of always carrying preparation.

### Nol

The Nol are plains-dwellers whose reputation runs far ahead of the truth. To outsiders they are bogeymen — laughing shapes in the dusk, masked figures whose armor bristles with teeth and bone, whose silhouettes loom larger than any body should. The stories paint them cruel, capricious, barely leashed. The Nol know the stories well. They did not make them — but they learned to use them.

Long before fear defined their dealings with other peoples, the Nol practiced ancient spiritual traditions built around masks, and the masks were never meant for war. They were made to frighten off malign spirits — predators of the unseen world that hunted by familiarity and weakness — and their exaggerated jaws, distorted eyes, and inhuman proportions were a deliberate declaration to the spirit-world: *these are not prey*. Ritual laughter, mimicry, and noise went with them, meant to confuse and repel whatever stalked the edges of the night. When outsiders first met masked Nol patrols at liminal hours — fog, dusk, eclipse — the result was panic, and then violence, and the masks that had been protective charms became, in the stories others told, symbols of terror. Faced with mounting hostility, the Nol made a grim and pragmatic choice. To abandon the masks would invite both spiritual danger and human reprisal; to keep them would mean being feared — and fear, at least, could be managed. So the Nol leaned into it. Over generations their war-gear grew deliberately monstrous — exaggerated, symbolic, unmistakable — carrying a single message: *do not cross us, but know that we are not unreasonable.* Fear, carefully cultivated, became a shield; a reputation, once imposed, became a tool.

Beneath the masks the Nol remain a deeply communal people. Among their own the masks come off, and the laughter is frequent and sharp — humor savage in the way of those who have survived long odds, joking about their own myths, exaggerating them to absurdity, taking a grim delight in the fact that the greatest practical joke ever played may be their own reputation. Like the hyena they resemble, their laughter is misread as madness or cruelty; it is neither, but bonding, release, and defiance in the face of danger. The duality strains the culture. Elders remember when the masks were sacred tools rather than instruments of deterrence, and younger Nol sometimes know only the monster-role they are expected to perform; the spiritual leaders fear that fear itself, once a means, is becoming an identity, while others answer that as long as it keeps the people safe it is a price worth paying.

The frontier has lately handed that old argument a new and unwelcome vindication. The Nol masks work — demonstrably, measurably — against exactly the kind of thing the settled world is only now learning to name: the hunters of the Nightmare, the predators of the unseen world that stalk by familiarity, which leak into the waking world wherever the boundary thins and are worst on the Frozen Coast in the long dark. The Witnesses of the Lion’s northern chapters have recruited Nol quietly for two generations, and the mortal-colors Mercenary company known as the Pale Riders — who hunt what the Shadow leaves behind and field nothing that can be turned against them — rides under a Nol commander. Nowhere is a Nol more valued than in the places where the old masks are suddenly, urgently, no longer superstition; and nowhere does the argument between the Nol who remember the masks as prayer and the Nol who wear them as threat cut deeper. To meet a Nol in armor is to meet a warning. To meet a Nol unmasked is to meet a person who understands fear intimately, and refuses to let it rule them.

> **Cultivated Reputation.** Nols gain a free dot in Intimidate and may hold one more dot than normally allowed in Intimidate.

### Pelorim

The Pelorim are quadrupedal nomads of the open plains, defined not by territory but by **routes** — ancient paths carried in memory and story, learned from those who came before and owed to those who will follow. A Pelorim’s identity is measured by the ways they know, the dangers they remember, and the knowledge they pass on before moving forward again. To forget a path is a moral failure. Their culture teaches that devotion to any single place or person narrows the vision and abandons the wider web of obligation that keeps the world connected, and so deep attachment across peoples is met not with disdain but with fear — fear that love might make a Pelorim linger too long, choose stillness over motion, and let a way fade from living memory.

Pelorim who break this expectation are not hated, but they are no longer trusted with the most vital routes. Some leave willingly, believing they have chosen something worth the loss; others depart in quiet sorrow, carrying paths they may never be permitted to walk again. As adventurers they make superb guides, scouts, and messengers — still moving forward, even as they wrestle with what the moving cost them.

What the wider world owes the Pelorim it has largely forgotten, though the Pelorim have not. When the Second Boundary War ground toward its end and no faction of the settled world could speak to the Wild, it was Pelorim route-keepers and a handful of Wardens who carried the terms of the Winter Concession between parties that shared no other channel — and among that peace’s provisions is the standing treaty, held jointly by the Pelorim and the Witnesses of the Lion to this day, that keeps the Trade Companies’ rails out of the Plains-of-the-Long-Walk. The Pelorim are, in a real and under-acknowledged sense, one of the two functioning diplomatic instruments civilization possesses for the thing that broke the nations, and they keep that role the way they keep everything: in memory, in motion, and without writing any of it down.

> **Quadruped.** Pelorim are quadrupedal. They cannot wear armor or wield weapons designed for bipeds without modification (typically doubling the cost), but they gain a 2-dot bonus to Athletics checks involving running, have a base land speed of 2 tiles, and may carry a party member of less than Large size on their back.

## Making a Character of Any People

Species establishes a body, inheritance, and set of cultural expectations; it does not dictate profession, morality, accent, or allegiance. For any character, answer five questions:

1. Which part of the species entry describes your home accurately, and which part would your family argue with?
2. Who taught you the trait represented by your species rule?
3. What accommodation does your body require from clothing, tools, travel, food, medicine, or architecture?
4. Which custom do you keep when nobody from home is watching?
5. What stereotype does the wider world apply to you, and how do you respond when it becomes inconveniently useful?

Choose a personal name, a home-name, and the form of address strangers use. They may be identical. Players should feel free to establish naming patterns with the GM; no species has one universal language or naming convention across all regions.

## Mixed Communities and Upbringings

Most large stations, works-towns, ports, and faction centers are mixed. A character raised among another people may share local food, manners, religion, and speech while retaining bodily traits and some family customs. Adoption, fostering, apprenticeship, migration, and multi-species households are ordinary. “Raised elsewhere” should add relationships rather than erase ancestry.

When two species traits appear difficult to reconcile with equipment or a class feature, preserve the identity of both: modify the tool, describe a culturally specific technique, or agree on an equivalent expression. Do not charge a character repeatedly for ordinary access to the game because their body differs from the assumed bipedal baseline; listed equipment costs matter when scarcity is the point, not as a standing participation tax.

## GM Toolkit: Portraying Peoples

Avoid presenting one NPC as the spokesperson for an entire species. Give communities local interests that cut across ancestry: water rights, rail access, a marriage dispute, bad harvest, doctrinal disagreement, or an upcoming hearing. Use species culture to shape how an NPC understands the problem, not to replace the problem.

For a community, define one majority custom, one minority variation, and one practice shared across peoples because everyone lives in the same place. For an individual, choose one inherited value they embrace, one they reinterpret, and one concern unrelated to ancestry.

Species traits are player capabilities, not invitations to negate characters. Let Icecunning find useful information, let route memory matter, and let unusual senses change how scenes are described. A challenge can complicate a trait without making the trait routinely useless.

**GM questions:** What does this settlement assume a body can do? Who has already adapted it? Which cultural disagreement is internal rather than a conflict between species?
