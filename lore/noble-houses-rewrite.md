# The Noble Houses

## Origins

The Noble Houses did not begin as nobility. They began as the families that could not hide.

In the desperate generations — the Silent Years, the Boundary Wars, and the longer desperation before either — many lineages acquired alterations. The origins were various: a bargain struck in extremity with something that was not a person; a deliberate forging by old-nation arts nobody now admits to understanding; an exposure survived and passed down; a covenant honored in the flesh. Most such lineages, history suggests, carried their changes quietly — gifts that did not show, oddities that could pass — and they dissolved into the general population, and nothing in this chapter is about them.

The Houses descend from the others. The lineages whose alterations were **written on the body** — who came out of their desperate bargain or their terrible exposure visibly, unmistakably, permanently *other* — had no quiet option. A family of people with scaled skin, or metal in their flesh, or a thirst that must be fed, cannot blend into a village. It can only be what it is, in front of everyone, forever. And a village tolerates the strange neighbor until a bad winter, a dead cow, a sick child — tolerance is revocable in an afternoon, and for the visible, there is nowhere to be afterward.

The lineages that survived their first centuries were the ones that understood this early and answered it the only way available to people who cannot hide: they accumulated power until eliminating them cost more than enduring them, and then they extended protection outward — to retainers, tenants, whole districts — until enough people depended on the lineage that its destruction became unthinkable to the very communities that might once have attempted it. Visibility, which had been the death sentence, became the asset: a House cannot be infiltrated by imposters, cannot be accused of hiding among you, cannot be anything other than exactly what it appears to be. The Houses made *legibility* into the foundation of feudal trust. You know what an Ostrogar is the moment one enters the valley. That, the Houses learned, is worth more than being loved.

That is the functional account, and the Houses do not teach it. What the Houses teach is lineage and covenant: the blood as trust, the gift as obligation, the demesne as a duty inherited alongside the alteration. The gap between the two accounts is not hypocrisy, exactly — nine generations of actually keeping the obligation will turn a survival strategy into a sincere ethic in any family. But the older account never fully leaves the room. It is there in the marriage evaluations, in the quiet ruthlessness about succession, in the institutional memory — never spoken to children, never absent from decisions — that all of this was built because the alternative was the mob.

One further fact shaped the Houses, and it is the fact that shaped everything else in the world: **the Wild answers claims in proportion to their scale.** The Houses hold land — openly, the only faction that does — and they have survived holding it because they hold it *small*. A demesne is a valley, a river delta, a fortified pass and its villages: never a province, never a border drawn wide. House doctrine on the threshold is old, unwritten, and absolute, and a House that begins assembling something a dead kingdom would recognize is a House the other Houses will move against before the Wild does — as a courtesy, the saying goes, to the neighbors.

## The Blood

Every capability the Houses field runs in the body, and it runs *visibly*. This is the meaning of the word **Dynast**, and the Concord of Seats holds it as definitional: a Dynast is a member of the blood in whom the lineage’s alteration has expressed — in the flesh, on the surface, beyond concealment or dispute. A House cousin with quick reflexes and ordinary skin is not a Dynast, whatever the reflexes owe to the lineage. The plate, the scales, the pallor, the metal: *the gift is the mark, and the mark is the gift.* The Houses did not choose this standard out of vanity. They chose it because their entire social position rests on being exactly what they appear to be, and a hidden gift is, to House thinking, indistinguishable from a lie the body tells.

Expression is the central uncertainty of House life. The blood is not arithmetic: two strongly expressed parents may produce an unexpressed child; a gift may skip a generation and return doubled; a lineage’s expression may drift across centuries into forms the House’s own archives struggle to recognize. Every House maintains its genealogies — the **blood-book** — with a rigor that borders on the devotional, and every House knows the genealogies predict less than they appear to.

From this uncertainty flows the practice outsiders find hardest to sit with: the **pairings**. Marriages within and between Houses are evaluated — formally, professionally, archives consulted and elders convened — for what the union is likely to produce. The Houses discuss this in the language of duty and continuity; the discomfort underneath the language is real, is felt most sharply by the young people being evaluated, and is not resolved by anyone in this chapter, because the Houses have not resolved it either. What can be said is that the practice has limits the Houses genuinely enforce: consent is required by the Concord of Seats, coercion is grounds for intervention by the other Houses, and the enforcement is not hollow — not because the Houses are gentle, but because every House contains elders who were once the young person in the evaluation.

**Outsiders marry in.** The blood decides what comes of it, and the blood is unsentimental: some of the strongest expressions on record have arrived two generations after an outsider consort, and some proud unbroken lines have thinned to nothing. Consorts hold full protected status and variable actual standing, and the gap between the two is one of the reliable sorrows of House life.

**The unexpressed** are the House’s largest population and its permanent question — perhaps ten of the recognized blood for every Dynast. They are the House’s administrators, stewards, envoys, and memory; they are also, by definition, the members who *could* walk away and pass as anyone, which the Dynasts never can, and both populations think about that fact more than either admits. An unexpressed heir is a succession crisis. An expressed servant’s child is a different kind of crisis. Both happen constantly, and the management of both is most of what House politics *is*.

**Origins are doctrine — other people’s doctrine.** The Houses are studiedly incurious in public about where their alterations came from. Everyone else is not, and the scholars’ taxonomy has hardened into the frame the wider world uses:

- **Covenant lines**, whose alteration descends from a compact with the Heavens or their servants — witnessed, recorded, and (this is the point) *conditional*.

- **Bargain lines**, whose alteration descends from a dealing with something that was not a person — the Fey, the Shadow’s architects of bargains, the sovereign things of the Wild. The beings that offer such terms still exist; the Lions classify them, correctly, among the monsters; and the polite fiction of the Bargain lines is that the ancestor who paid the price paid *all* of it. No Bargain House confirms its origin. Several conspicuously decline to deny one.

- **Forged lines**, whose alteration was *made* — old-nation arts, wartime desperation, flesh-work at scales no Guild hall would now license. The Forged lines carry the particular stigma of the deliberate, and the particular pride of it.

- **Marked lines**, whose alteration came from exposure — a boundary thinning, a leakage survived, a place that changed the family that refused to leave it.

- **Contested lines**, whose origin is argued — by scholars, by the Lions, sometimes bitterly by the House itself.

The taxonomy matters because the Lions have made it matter. Hard doctrine reads a bloodline by its source: a Covenant line is grace inherited; a Bargain line is a debt walking around on legs; a Forged line is presumption; a Marked line is a wound that breeds. The mainline officially judges Houses by conduct rather than origin — and unofficially, every House envoy knows which chapters to visit with which genealogists’ letters in hand.

## Structure

A House is four concentric obligations.

At the center, **the head** — Lord or Lady by convention, though several Houses keep older titles — who holds the demesne, speaks at the Concord of Seats, and carries final authority over pairings and succession. Heads are made by each House’s own custom: primogeniture in some, election among expressed elders in others, and in House Ostrogar by a mechanism outsiders never believe until they see the keep.

Around the head, **the recognized blood** — everyone in the blood-book, expressed or not. Recognition is formal, recorded, and revocable only by the head; it carries the House’s full protection and the House’s full claim, and the second half of that sentence is the one that produces runaways.

Around the blood, **the sworn** — retainers, stewards, guards, and specialists bound by oath rather than birth, generational in the old demesnes: there are families that have armed House Ostrogar longer than most Companies have existed.

Outermost, **the demesne** — the land and its people, who owe the House rents and duties and are owed protection, justice, and (the obligation the Houses advertise least and honor most) *intercession*: the House stands between its people and every other power in the world. A Company cannot press a demesne laborer; a Registry writ stops at the demesne stone until the House endorses it; the Lions themselves negotiate entry in the hard-doctrine regions. The demesne is small, as covered above. It is not soft.

**The Concord of Seats** is the mechanism by which lineages become Houses and remain them: mutual recognition. A House is a House because the other Houses accept its blood-book, its guarantees, and its children in marriage. Recognition has been extended eleven times since Standard Reckoning began and withdrawn twice, and the withdrawn do not appear in this chapter, because that is what withdrawal means.

## Signs and Rites

The faction’s deep grammar is this: **the body is the record.** An Ostrogar’s plate carries every engagement; an Ardenne notary’s skin carries every sealed term; an Ophara keeps the molts of a lifetime; a Corvara’s first thirst is met in public ceremony. Where other factions keep their histories in books, the Houses keep books as *indexes* — the blood-book points at bodies, living and Seated and shed, and the House’s truth is read off skin and bone and metal by those who know the script. This is not incidental to the visibility rule. It is the visibility rule, matured into a civilization: a people who cannot conceal what they are became a people who record *everything* in the medium that cannot lie.

Expression is marked, and the customs vary — but what they share is publicity within the House. Expression is never allowed to be a secret, because a gift discovered in secret is a gift that has already begun to be feared.

Blood-books do not leave the keep. The theft of one is the sole crime for which the Concord authorizes another House’s direct intervention, which tells you what the Houses fear more than armies.

House colors, demesne stones at the borders, the exchange of **name-cups** at betrothals, the formal grammar of guarantee and grievance — the texture is deep and regionally various, and the single custom an adventurer must know is this: *never comment on a Dynast’s alteration before they do.* The gift is the House’s to speak of first. The Dynast has heard every remark, every euphemism, and every carefully averted glance a thousand times, and the courtesy of letting them open the subject is the whole difference between a welcome and a wall.

## The Houses

There are perhaps forty recognized Seats across the known regions. These seven are the ones a campaign is most likely to meet, and between them they span the faction’s range.

### House Corvara — the Thirst

*Bargain line (unconfirmed, undenied). Demesne: the Corvenwood valleys, eastern Verdant Belt fringe. Head: Lady Ivress Corvara.*

The Corvara are unmistakable at a glance, which is the point of a glance in this faction: pale past pallor, lithe past slender, with the elongated grace of something assembled for speed — and the teeth, which the House neither files nor hides. A Corvara Dynast moves the way the eye moves: arrival without visible transit. They are the finest duelists, climbers, and night-fighters among the Houses; they see in full dark; they take little ordinary food; and they drink blood, which the House states the way it states its acreage, because two centuries have taught them the fact will be discussed, and the only question is whether the Corvara are in the room when it is.

The demesne meets the requirement through the **tithe**: a measured, recorded, compensated donation owed by the sworn and the demesne families, entered in the Tithe Book with the formality of any rent and repaid in the coin the Corvara mint better than anyone — *medicine*. Corvara blood-medics are the finest trauma physicians in the known regions; the House pioneered transfusion generations before the Sawbones case libraries caught up, and a demesne family’s tithe purchases care no Company town can buy. Outsiders expect the Corvenwood to feel haunted. It is instead the healthiest river country in the east, and its families are, on the whole, rather smug about their landlords.

A Corvara’s palate is a diagnostic instrument. From a taste — taken with consent, under witness, by the House’s exacting protocol — a trained Corvara reads what the blood physically carries: health and hidden disease, poison, pregnancy, ancestry, and, most valuably to the Concord, **expression** — whether a lineage’s gift is present, thinning, or waiting in a candidate. Nothing of thought or memory; the House is emphatic on the boundary, and the Monastic Orders, who tend the disciplines of the mind, have formally examined and endorsed the emphasis, an endorsement the Corvara paid for in patience and display and consider among their best investments. On that palate rests the Houses’ pairing-verification trade: half the match-writs of the last two generations carry a Corvara reading in their annexes, and the House’s chief reader, **Solenne Corvara-Mar**, has tasted more of the Concord’s futures than any living person. Her client annexes would reorganize the faction if they were ever seen, which is precisely why they never will be.

Two facts complete the House. First: the Corvara are *not undead*. They are alive in every testable sense; the Lions have tested it, formally, twice, at the House’s own insistence; and the distinction is existential, because the Shadow’s restless dead are the thing the Corvara are mistaken for by every frightened village and every hard-doctrine pamphlet. Second, and consequently: **the Corvara hunt vampires** — the true ones, the Shadow-made ones, the things that wear the House’s reputation as camouflage. No one in the world is better equipped to find them, no one has better reasons, and the House’s standing bounty-partnership with the Registries on undead predators is the oldest continuous account either institution holds.

*Current trouble:* something is killing along the Corvenwood tithe-routes — drained bodies, arranged to be found, arranged to look Corvara to anyone who has never seen the House’s actual practice. The pattern is fluent in House custom: it knows the routes, the schedules, the courtesies. Lady Ivress has closed the borders, recalled Solenne from the circuits, and posted through the Registry at Lastlight the largest private bounty in the region’s history. The hard-Lions pamphleteers have not waited for findings. They rarely do.

### House Ostrogar — the Grown Wall

*Forged line (wartime; the House says “proven” where others say “made”). Demesne: the Varn Gap and its villages, southern Iron Reach — the second of the two Houses on the Reach’s southern edge. Head: Lord Hadrik Ostrogar.*

The Ostrogar grow their inheritance. An expressed Ostrogar is massive — the smallest adult Dynast of the line stands a head over a big Barudrim — and the body produces **living armament**: bone plate across the forearms, shoulders, and spine; crowns of horn; blade-ridges at the forearm’s edge around which the House’s fencing tradition is built. The growth answers use — plate thickens where the Dynast has been struck — and a life of holding the Gap is written on an Ostrogar as legibly as a Gorûn elder’s tattoos. The House reads its members’ histories at a glance, at funerals, and in the evaluation of heirs.

The growth does not stop. This is the fact at the center of the House, and the House has made of it something outsiders come away from changed. An Ostrogar who lives long enough thickens past armor into architecture: movement slows across the seventh and eighth decades, the plate closes, and the elder takes the **Seat** — a place in the fabric of the keep itself, prepared since their fiftieth year. The Seated live long past sitting. They are fed, warmed, honored, and consulted; they hold the House’s memory, hear every dispute of consequence, and watch the Gap through embrasures built to their eyes. The keep at Varn is four centuries old, and its foundation courses are the Seated of eleven generations — ancestors, in the walls, *alive*, in a slow progression from counselor to fixture to, eventually and gently, stone that still dreams. Enemies call the keep a tomb that talks. The Ostrogar call it the only honest fortress in the world: a wall that remembers why it stands.

The House’s outward life follows from its body. The Ostrogar are the Houses’ soldiers — holders of the Gap since the First Boundary War, in which the line’s forging is generally dated; guarantors of physical security the way the Ardenne are guarantors of terms; blunt, loyal, terrible in the field, and possessed of a dry, patient humor that surprises everyone who expected only the shoulders.

*Current trouble:* Lord Hadrik is two winters from the Seat, and he will not sit. He is the finest field commander the House has produced in a century, and he has told the Seated to their embrasures that he intends to die standing, in the field, in use — which the House’s whole doctrine of the body holds to be a private man’s answer to a public duty. The Seated are divided. The heirs are unnamed. And the House that guarantees everyone else’s security is, for the first time in living memory, uncertain of its own succession — a fact its rivals have noticed with the delicacy of crows.

### House Ferrant — the Living Iron

*Forged line by the House’s proud claim; Marked by the scholars’ whisper. Demesne: the Ferrant works-valley at Cold Anvil, northern High Cradle. Head: Lord Aldous Ferrant.*

The Ferrant are flesh and bio-metal in one body — the same living alloys from which spyagric frames are grown — and no one who has seen a Ferrant Dynast forgets the first sight: the dark metallic traceries running the forearms and throat like veins that learned geometry, the knuckles and brow shading into true metal, the eyes with a faint furnace-light in dim rooms. A Ferrant’s bones ring when struck. Their blood runs dark with suspended metal. They are heavy, tireless, indifferent to burns and bitter cold, slow to heal from cuts and unsettlingly fast to heal from breaks — and they can *hold charge*: a Ferrant Dynast can take vim into the body and carry it, ebbs banked in living metal, discharged through the hands at need. The House’s duelists do not carry pistols. They are, in the sense that matters, already loaded.

The gift made the House’s fortune twice over. First in the works: Ferrant hands can shape hot bio-metal barehanded, feel a flaw in an alloy by touch, and endure the conversion-floor conditions that kill or maim ordinary workers, and the Cold Anvil works-valley produces frame components that even Vault & Coil’s own floors cannot match — a dependence the Company resents with precision and renews without fail. Second, and stranger, in the **herds**: spyagric frames, which behave like livestock in every way that matters — inheriting, imprinting, mourning — accept the Ferrant as *kin*. A frame that has thrown its handlers will gentle under a Ferrant hand; the great chassis lines are bred, broken, and blessed at Cold Anvil; and the House’s stud-books for frame lineages are consulted across three regions with the same gravity as any blood-book. The Ferrant are, in the exact old sense of the word, the frames’ *husbandmen*, and they speak of the herds with an affection outsiders find either touching or deeply strange, usually both.

The House claims a Forged origin — old-nation flesh-smiths, a deliberate wartime making, pride taken straight — and the claim is plausible and undocumented. The whisper, which the House has spent money to discourage and thereby confirmed as worth whispering, is that the line began at a **Maker-Site**: that some ancestor family lived too long beside one of the buried places where the Wyr’s vanished creators worked, and came away carrying the Makers’ materials in their blood. The Wyr themselves visit Cold Anvil — rarely, formally, and with an attention the Ferrant return in kind — and what either people concludes from the other’s existence, neither has published.

One relationship defines the House’s limits: **the Wild will not have them.** Cold iron is anathema to the Wild’s rulers, and the Ferrant are iron that walks — treaty lands are closed to them absolutely, Warden guides refuse them (with apology; the refusal is load-bearing, not personal), and at least one Beast Lord is reliably reported to kill Ferrant on scent. The House holds the deepest civilized valley in the High Cradle and travels by rail or not at all, and there is a bitterness under its pride on this subject that its members do not perform for strangers.

*Current trouble:* a frame herd of the House’s finest line — eleven great chassis, bred over forty years — has begun refusing handlers, all handlers, including Ferrant. The herd is not sick by any measure the House knows. It has simply turned, as one animal, to face northwest, toward the deep Cradle and the buried Sites, and it is waiting for something, and House Ferrant — who know the herds better than anyone alive — have quietly begun hiring outsiders because they do not want their own people to be the ones who find out what.

### House Talvecq — the Lost House

*Contested line — catastrophically. Demesne: forfeit; the Talvecq seep-country in the central Dust Lanes stands under Concord trusteeship. Surviving senior: Severin Talvecq.*

The Talvecq gift was **the Answer**: flesh that met the world by changing. A Talvecq Dynast’s body responded — to injury, to poison, to Shadow-leakage, to need — with adaptation, visible and rapid: skin that closed like armor where struck, lungs that mastered the seep-vapors of their demesne within a season, forms that shifted to the shape of the problem. They were the hardiest line in the Concord’s records, stewards of the worst seep-country in the Dust Lanes precisely because nothing there could kill what would not stay killable, and for six generations the Answer answered and stopped, answered and stopped, the way a well-bred gift should.

In 259 SR it stopped stopping.

The Cascade took the House in a single season. The change outran its causes — Dynasts adapting to threats that were not there, forms accumulating without need or end — and the minds went with the forms: not all at once, the fragmentary accounts agree, which is the part no one who has read them discusses twice. By winter the demesne had fallen, not to an enemy but to its lords; the sworn who survived fled or were released by masters who, in their last coherent acts, unbarred the gates and ordered them out. What remained of House Talvecq walked, crawled, and unfolded into the seep-country, wearing scraps of heraldry into shapes that heraldry was never cut for, and it is there still. The trusteeship lands are not empty. Warden patrols confirm movement decades after any Talvecq body should have died, Registry bounties on the “Dust Lane revenants” stand uncollected — and once a year, roughly, something leaves a token at the demesne stone: a name-cup, a signet, once an entire blood-book page, weathered and folded and placed with what the finders invariably, unwillingly, describe as *care*.

The Concord and the Lions agreed on the cover within the year: a Shadow-leakage event, the defenses overwhelmed, the House destroyed. The cover holds among the public and convinces no institution that matters, because every blood-book in the Concord now carries the question the Cascade asked: *is this the far end of every gift?* Every House is a controlled burn — expression husbanded, pairings pruned, the fire kept banked across centuries — and Talvecq is the fire escaped. It is the argument beneath the conservative pairings, beneath the united refusal of Enclave study, beneath the Concord’s institutional caution entire. The Houses do not speak of the Lost House often. They govern by it daily.

Leo came to the ruin. This is documented. What he found there — whether anything of the people remained in the changed, whether he spoke with something, what he did or declined to do — is sealed by his own instruction, the only seal in the Lions’ archive, unbroken for fifty-three years. **Severin Talvecq** — expressed, but barely; a faint armoring of the skin along the spine, a verdict he has carried like the rest of his inheritance — has spent twenty years assembling the petition now lodged at Thirstgate under Ardenne guarantee. He does not want the demesne back. He wants the trigger: whether the Cascade was the Answer’s inevitable end, or whether something *asked his family a question* — poison, provocation, experiment, enemy — that their flesh answered until it answered itself away. On the first reading, he is the last ember of a fire that will someday take every House in this chapter. On the second, someone did this, and is still alive, and has had fifty-three years to consider doing it again.

### House Ophara — the Drowned Court

*Marked line (the House agrees, serenely; the delta marked them, and the delta is theirs). Demesne: the Greywater delta, where the Verdant Belt’s rivers reach the southern coast. Head: Lady Neszira Ophara.*

The Ophara are the amphibious House: scaled from crown to heel in patterns that record their birth-channel the way a fingerprint records a hand, clawed, membrane-eyed, gilled at the throat’s sides beneath the jaw — fully at home in air and better than at home in water, where an Ophara Dynast moves the way the Corvara move on land. Their blood is acid: a wounded Ophara is a hazard to the wounder, their duelists fight with a confidence that unnerves specialists, and their dead are handled by House custom alone, with ceremony and with gloves. They regrow — a lost finger in a season, a lost limb in a year or two, imperfectly and painfully and *reliably*, and an elder Ophara’s mismatched hands are read like an Ostrogar’s plate. And they are cold-blooded in the plain sense: winter slows them, the House’s great bath-halls are heated by an engineering the Guilds openly covet, and the Ophara year — its courts, its campaigns, its collections — is built frankly around the thermometer.

The delta made them and the delta is their argument. The Greywater is the drowned country: a hundred channels, brack and silt and shifting bars, the graveyard of every attempt to run rail or road across it and the only route between the eastern Belt’s produce and the southern sea. The Ophara hold it the way the Ostrogar hold the Gap — as the House whose body *is* the qualification. Their ferry monopolies, pilotage, flood-wardening, and above all their **salvage right** — by Concord recognition and older custom, what the Greywater takes, the Ophara raise, and what the Ophara raise, the Ophara own a third of — have made the Drowned Court rich in the unhurried, unanswerable way of people who own a chokepoint and have excellent manners about it. Ophara divers do the underwater work of three regions: bridge footings, wreck clearance, the recovery of what Companies lose and insurers pray about. Their molt-sheds hold the shed skins of every Dynast back nine generations, kept, labeled, and consulted — the **molt-books**, the House’s parallel archive, in which a life is read season by season off its own outgrown surfaces.

*Current trouble:* eight months ago a Company freight barge went down in the Serpent’s Channel carrying, the manifest eventually and reluctantly admitted, refined orichalcum — a cargo worth more than the delta’s annual trade. Salvage right says a third of it is Ophara the moment it is raised. The Company’s lawyers say the manifest’s admiralty clauses say otherwise; the Registry at the delta mouth has been asked to arbitrate a question older than admiralty clauses; and all of this has become theoretical, because the Ophara divers sent to survey the wreck report that the orichalcum is being *collected* — carefully, selectively, by something in the deep channel that was not there last year — and the House has stopped sending divers, and started, for the first time in its history, hiring them.

### House Vehk — the Glass Watch

*Marked line. Demesne: the Vehk terraces, western Salt Reach, within sight of the Glass Plains. Head: Lord Karim Vehk.*

The Vehk wear the boundary on their skin: vitreous traceries across the hands, throat, and temples — true glass, growing in the flesh, spreading slowly with age until the eldest Vehk glitter faintly by lamplight — and eyes with a depth and refraction no one meets twice without remembering. What the alteration does is the House’s purpose and its debt in one: **the Vehk perceive the boundary.** Where the world thins toward the Wyrd, a Vehk feels it as plainly as weather — direction, degree, and drift — and the House has kept continuous observational records of the Glass Plains for longer than Standard Reckoning has existed. When the leakage became measurable in 283 SR, the instruments were vindicating three centuries of Vehk journals, and the House’s archive became overnight the most strategically significant private collection in the south. The Lions’ embargo runs on Vehk data. So does every theory anyone has about where the trend line ends.

The Vehk are small, insular, scrupulous, and tired in a way that has become hereditary. Their origin is the Plains themselves — the family that would not leave the terraces when the thinning began, and was changed by staying — and their doctrine is that the gift is a *tenancy*: they see the boundary because someone must, and the seeing is the rent. They carry, more purely than any other House, the burden the whole faction shares: protected by their name into an isolation the name cannot fix. Vehk children glitter. Vehk children notice that visitors notice. The House’s inner courts are gentle in a way its public face is not, and the gentleness is a wall like any other.

*Current trouble:* the Thirstgate Tower Enclave has formally petitioned to study the Vehk expression — respectfully, with terms, and with genuine cause, since a heritable boundary-sense is instrumentation no Enclave can build. Lord Karim has refused, as his mother refused, as the House has always refused; after Talvecq, the Concord’s doctrine on the study of gifts is not curiosity but dread. But the Companies want the embargo’s edges surveyed, the Lions want the trend line explained, the petition gives everyone’s wanting a respectable channel — and Karim has responded by doing something no Vehk head has done: hiring outsiders, quietly, through the Registry, to learn who is *coordinating* the pressure.

### House Ardenne — the Written

*Covenant line. Demesne: river country on the Iron Reach’s southern edge. Head: Lady Ilse Ardenne.*

The Ardenne gift takes the faction’s grammar — the body is the record — and makes it literal: **oaths write themselves into Ardenne skin.** When an Ardenne Dynast seals an agreement — a drop of their blood, formally witnessed, worked into the instrument’s wax — the terms scar into their flesh in fine silver script, legible to any who know the notary hand, and the scar *lives*: it aches when its term is strained, burns when its term is broken, and pales to nothing only when the agreement is discharged or formally renounced by all parties. A young Ardenne notary bears a few lines at the wrist. Lady Ilse Ardenne, seventy and exact, is written to the shoulders and across the back — a standing archive of forty years of the region’s most consequential promises, wearing a high collar not from modesty but because strangers read her in queues.

On this gift stands the most valuable service in a stateless world: enforceable agreement between parties who share no law. An Ardenne seal on a treaty between two Companies, between a Guild and a Concern, between a settlement and a Mercenary Company — twice in the last century, on terms carried under Warden escort to a Beast Lord — is the closest thing that exists to inter-faction law, because breach is not arguable: the notary *shows the scar*, and the scar has never once been wrong in four generations of extraordinary incentive for it to be. The House charges accordingly, maintains a permanent college of notary-envoys, and holds to a neutrality in the sealed matters themselves that has been tested by everything the world contains and has not yet cracked in any way the world knows about.

The origin is documented as no other House’s is: the covenant of the first Ardenne, sworn before a servant of the Ledger in the Silent Years, its instrument preserved in the House archive — and sealed, of course, in the first Ardenne’s blood, which means somewhere on the founding document is the scar-script’s first line, and the House lets it be known that the covenant is still in force, and does not let it be known what it obliges them to, or what happens to the gift if they fail.

*Current trouble:* the House’s guarantee backs Severin Talvecq’s petition — a use of the seal in a matter the seal cannot resolve, which the notary college opposed and Ilse ordered anyway, and the college has noticed what Ilse has not explained: that when the petition is discussed in her presence, her hand moves, without her seeming to know it, to a line of script high on her left forearm that does not appear in the college’s register of her seals.

## The Houses Among the Factions

**The Lions** read the Houses through origin doctrine, as covered above, and the Houses have learned to be read: Covenant genealogies travel with every envoy, and the hard-doctrine regions receive Ardenne cousins where the mainline regions might receive a Corvara medic. The mainline’s working position — conduct over origin — has held for a generation and is one Banneret’s health away from renegotiation, a fact every blood-book in the Concord has quietly noted.

**The Guilds** and the Houses meet in the body. The Sawbones hold the world’s medical knowledge; the Houses *are* a medical phenomenon; and the resulting relationship — Corvara medics training at Guild halls, Guild examiners pointedly not asking certain questions in demesne infirmaries, the standing Guild position that blood-books are private property and the standing Sawbones suspicion that they are case libraries being withheld — is the most sophisticated managed friction in the faction system. The Tinkerers, meanwhile, have a relationship all their own with House Ferrant, equal parts reverence for the Cold Anvil works and professional indignity that the finest bio-metal shaping in the world is done by *hand*.

**The Monastic Orders** and the Houses barely intersect, with one formal exception: the Orders’ examination and endorsement of the Corvara reading protocols — the mind is the Orders’ province, and the Corvara were at pains to demonstrate their palate never crosses into it. The endorsement is renewed each decade in a ceremony both parties conduct with perfect gravity and, observers report, something adjacent to friendship.

**The Registries** intersect the Houses at the demesne stone and the runaway. A writ stops at the stone until the House endorses it; the Houses endorse readily against outsiders and reluctantly against their own; and the *runaway dependent* — recognized blood fleeing the claim that comes with the protection — is the classic hard case. The Registries will not carry a writ on a runaway who has broken no law; the Houses rarely forgive the refusal; and the unsanctioned hunts that result are conducted by exactly the sort of people the Registries exist to make unnecessary. A party will meet this situation. Note that for the Houses it divides sharply by expression: an unexpressed runaway can vanish into any city, and a Dynast runaway *cannot* — a fleeing Ophara or Ferrant is visible to every informer on the road, and the hunts that find them first are rarely the sanctioned kind. Some of the fiercest quiet work the Houses ever commission is not retrieval but *protection* of a Dynast runaway the House has privately decided to let go — watched over at a distance by hired strangers, because the one thing the House cannot give a child who fled it is its own name.

**The Companies** deal with the Houses as fixed terrain: demesnes cannot be bought, routed around only expensively, and negotiated with through forms the Companies find archaic and observe to the letter, because an Ardenne seal is the one instrument that makes a Company’s own promises credible to third parties. The Ophara salvage arbitration and the alternate-route survey south of the Varn Gap are, this year, the two places that resentment is closest to the surface.

**The Enclaves** want to understand the Houses more than any institution wants anything in this chapter. Heritable, expressed capability is the standing refutation of half a dozen Enclave theories and the confirmation of none; the Vehk petition is only the visible edge of a research appetite every House has felt; and the Houses’ united refusal — the one position the entire Concord holds without dissent — is not principle but memory. The Concord watched a gift catch fire once. It does not intend to hand anyone a bellows.

**The Wardens** and the Houses barely touch, with two live exceptions: the boundary demesnes (Vehk terraces, the Talvecq trusteeship, the Greywater’s wild channels), where two trades watch different edges of the same night with wary professional regard — and House Ferrant, whom the Wardens must refuse and hate refusing, and for whom certain Wardens quietly carry messages into treaty lands the House itself can never enter, a courtesy the Wild tolerates, so far, by pretending not to notice it.

## Current Fault Lines

- **The Talvecq Petition.** Lodged, guaranteed by Ardenne, and sitting before a failing Banneret whose successor may be Marcus Fadrick. If the seal opens, the Cascade’s trigger — inevitability or enemy — becomes knowable, and either answer remakes the Concord: the first indicts every blood-book, the second names a perpetrator with fifty-three years of lead. If Fadrick is elevated first, the petition dies, and Severin Talvecq, a patient man, stops being one.

- **The Corvenwood Killings.** Drained bodies on the tithe-routes, staged to indict the Corvara by someone fluent in the House’s own customs. The bounty at Lastlight stands. The bodies, in the last month, have begun appearing closer to the keep.

- **The Varn Succession.** Hadrik Ostrogar will not take the Seat, will not name an heir, and has begun — the Seated report it through the embrasures — riding the Gap alone at night, as if inviting the death he prefers. The Concord cannot compel him; the Gap cannot be unheld; and at least one Company has commissioned, very quietly, a survey of alternate southern routes, which the House would regard, if it learns of it, as a bet placed on its funeral.

- **The Waiting Herd.** Eleven of Cold Anvil’s finest chassis stand facing the deep Cradle, refusing all handlers, waiting. House Ferrant is hiring outsiders to follow the herd’s gaze to the buried Sites, because the House has begun to suspect the answer concerns its own origin, and there are questions a House prefers to have asked by people it can pay to be discreet — or to be wrong.

- **The Serpent’s Channel.** Sunken orichalcum, a three-cornered legal war between salvage right, admiralty clause, and Registry arbitration — rendered moot by the fact that something in the deep channel is collecting the cargo first, selectively, as if working from the manifest.

- **The Transfusion Question.** If expression can be carried in blood — moved, banked, bought — then the blood is currency, the pairings are obsolete, and the Concord’s whole architecture stands on a floor that has just been discovered to have a door in it. The Corvara, whose medicine moves blood between bodies every week and whose palate would know the answer if anyone’s could, have issued exactly one statement on the subject in forty years: *that the House does not discuss its research.* The Concord has parsed those seven words more closely than any treaty in its history.

## The Houses in Play

**Playing House blood.** A Dynast character wears the whole chapter: a gift that cannot be hidden, a genealogy that claims them, and a face that every stranger reads before a word is spoken. The campaign questions come built in — what the House wants of them, what they fled or serve, and what it costs to be permanently, visibly *of* something in a world where everyone else can choose what to appear to be. An *unexpressed* House character is equally playable and inversely loaded: all of the claim, none of the mark, the ability to pass that no Dynast has — and a lifetime of being the blood-book’s disappointment, or its quiet indispensable hand, or its runaway who actually got away. Which of the two the House values more is a question each House answers differently and no House answers honestly.

**What the Houses pay adventurers to do.** The Houses prefer their own sworn for anything touching the blood — which is exactly why the work they contract out is the work this game is about: matters the House cannot be seen to touch, cannot spare its own for, or cannot trust to anyone whose loyalty is hereditary. Typical work, by tier:

- Escort a tithe-caravan, a match-writ, or a notary-envoy through country where what they carry is worth more than they are.

- Find a runaway dependent before the unsanctioned hunters do — and decide, on meeting them, whose contract you are actually keeping.

- Investigate the Corvenwood killings from outside the House’s compromised vantage; Lady Ivress pays a premium for findings over corpses.

- Dive the Serpent’s Channel under Ophara pilotage, survey what is collecting the orichalcum, and come back — the third clause is the one the contract stresses.

- Follow the waiting herd when House Ferrant finally lets it walk, into the deep Cradle, toward whatever it has been listening to.

- Stand guard through a Glass Plains measurement season alongside Vehk observers and the Lions’ permanently unimpressed Veteran — and notice who else is watching the watchers.

- Recover stolen blood-book pages — or molt-book skins, which travel worse and tell more — before their contents are read, copied, or believed.

- Carry an Ardenne-sealed instrument to a signing between parties who have tried to kill each other twice this year, and keep every signatory alive until the wax is cool.

- Hunt a true vampire wearing the Corvara name — with a House blood-medic attached to the party who has personal reasons and does not discuss them.

- At the highest tiers: enter the Talvecq trusteeship lands and document what leaves the tokens at the demesne stone; or be present, in whatever capacity the moment requires, when the petition is finally answered.

**Patrons.** *Lady Ilse Ardenne* — work for the notary college is steady, formal, and a reference that opens every door the faction has; her personal commissions are rarer, unexplained, and, the college has noticed, increasingly connected to the petition she guaranteed and the scar she does not register. *Solenne Corvara-Mar* — the reader’s clients need things found, verified, and carried; she pays in Rings or in answers, and the second currency is the dangerous one. *Severin Talvecq* — twenty years of investigation have made him the best-informed private individual in the Dust Lanes and the most careful employer a party will ever have: he has already lost everyone once, and he checks the demesne stone himself, every year, alone.

## Player Toolkit

A House character should name lineage, demesne, household role, visible sign of the gift, and the relative who taught them how to suppress or express it. Decide what the House gives its dependents in exchange for obedience and which family obligation you consider legitimate. Name one sworn family that sees you as a person rather than a title and one relative whose approval still matters against your judgment.

A Dynast should describe how Manifestation changes posture, appetite, voice, senses, and social behavior—not only combat statistics. Decide who has seen the fully expressed form, what calms it, and what rumor outsiders repeat about your blood that is false.

## GM Toolkit

Houses can provide sanctuary, retainers, local law, hereditary expertise, old records, and resources concentrated over generations. They cannot act outside their demesnes as if blood were universal authority, make lineage private once publicly expressed, or keep dependents loyal without fulfilling reciprocal obligations.

Run a demesne through three voices: the House’s account, a sworn family’s account, and an outsider’s account. None should be wholly false. House horror works best when affection and exploitation share a table, not when every noble is secretly a monster.

**GM questions:** What does this household owe that it calls generosity? Who would inherit if the blood chose unexpectedly? Which family secret is actually protecting someone outside the family?
