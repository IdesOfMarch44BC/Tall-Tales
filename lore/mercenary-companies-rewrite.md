# The Mercenary Companies

## Origins

The Unmapping left the world a security problem with no one to solve it: no crown, no levy, no garrison — only settlements that could be burned, goods that could be taken, and people who could be hurt, scattered across distances that made every community its own last resort.

The first answers were the obvious ones, and they were bad. Armed bands sold “protection” in the old sense — the sense with the threat inside it — and the Silent Years’ history is substantially the history of towns learning the difference between a defender and a well-mannered occupier. But the racket model carries its own refutation, and the frontier ran the experiment thousands of times until the result was undeniable: the extortionist consumes his client base, and the *defender grows his*. A band that actually kept the road open found more traffic on the road; a band that actually drove off the raiders found more farms behind it next season; and somewhere in the late Silent Years — the texts credit many fathers, and the Concord-era theorist **Hanselm Hov** credits arithmetic — the better bands stopped selling the absence of their own violence and started selling something new: a *promise*, priced in advance, of standing between the client and everyone else’s.

That promise is the industry. A Mercenary Company, whatever its colors, sells contracted defense: the caravan escorted, the town walled and watched, the incursion met — and, in the mature form the great Companies reached generations ago, the promise is written as **indemnity**: the Company does not merely fight for the client; it *answers for the loss*. A Company that must pay for every granary burned on its watch is a Company with the correct attitude toward granaries — Hov’s first maxim, quoted at every charter signing since: *“Make the defender own the loss, and you will never need to make him brave.”*

Everything else about the Companies follows from the structure of that promise, and it is covered below. But the founding logic answers, first, the question outsiders always ask: what separates a Company from a bandit confederacy with a banner? The honest answer of the trade is: *structurally, two signatures* — registration on the Muster Rolls, and accession to the arbitration compact — and the whole weight of the industry is in what those signatures cost. The line is thinner than either side of it officially admits, is crossed in both directions in every generation, and holds anyway, because the signatures put a Company inside a web where behaving well is the only thing that pays.

## The Premium

A defense contract prices a promise, and the pricing is the civilization.

The client — a town, a Company of the trading kind, a Guild hall, a House with more demesne than sworn, a rail line with a hundred miles of exposure — pays the **premium**: the retainer, set by the Company’s actuaries against everything that makes the client expensive to defend. Location, of course; exposure, of course. But also, and decisively, *conduct*. A settlement that feuds with its neighbors pays more. A trading post that cheats Wild-treaty terms pays much more, if it can find coverage at all. A client who *starts* a fight discovers the clause that every registered contract in the world carries in some wording, and that the trade calls simply **the Hov clause**: *defense covers what is done to you, never what you began.* No registered Company insures aggression — not from scruple, though some have scruples, but because an insurer of aggression is buying its clients’ enemies wholesale, and the actuaries priced that centuries ago and wrote the answer into the standard forms.

The consequences radiate outward and quietly order the world. A person or institution too quarrelsome to insure becomes, in the functional sense, an **outlaw**: not forbidden — nothing is forbidden; there is no one to forbid — but *uncovered*, walking bare through a world where everyone who matters is answered for, and the Registries’ writ-work begins, more often than not, exactly where insurability ends. Between Companies, the same logic built the **Hov Convention**: the standing arbitration compact under which disputes between clients of different Companies go to agreed arbitrators — Registry adjudicators, Ardenne-sealed panels — rather than to the field, because two Companies that fight each other are two Companies paying claims to everyone while collecting from no one, and the trade’s oldest joke is that peace was invented by accountants and merely signed by soldiers. And above all of it, built from the losses of the Boundary Wars, sits the **Incursion Pool**: the reinsurance compact by which the registered Companies mutually backstop catastrophic Wild and Shadow events — the recognition, written in the red ink of 251 SR, that against *that* enemy the Companies are one industry or they are severally bankrupt.

Two things a Company therefore does not do, and the trade regards both prohibitions as load-bearing. It does not aggress, as above. And it does not **conquer** — not only because conquest is uninsurable but because of the older arithmetic every institution in this world answers to: the Wild responds to claims in proportion to their scale, and a Company is a large armed organization operating, at all times, one declaration away from being something the Wild would notice. The Companies defend claims the world has already learned to tolerate. They create none. A commander who forgets this is corrected by her own actuaries before anyone else needs to reach for a treaty, because the premiums price the Wild too.

## The Stock

Here is the problem the premiums cannot solve: the frontier does not contain enough people willing to die at the price defense can charge.

The mathematics are unsentimental. A line trooper’s life, honestly priced — training, wages, pension, the indemnity to the family — makes broad mortal infantry an economic impossibility for any but the shortest and richest contracts; and the frontier’s threat profile is the opposite of short and rich: long watches, sudden horrors, attrition against things that do not negotiate. The industry’s answer, reached down different roads by different Companies and constituting, today, the single greatest difference between them, is the **stock**: soldiers *made* — grown, raised, cloned, called — by the great manufacturing arts, mustered in lines a Company can afford to spend where it could never afford to spend people.

The forms are various, and each carries its own economics, its own doctrine, and its own weather among the other factions:

**Grown lines** — spagyric soldiery, the vat and the vine: thorn-limbed infantry raised from seed-stock in licensed grow-yards, maturing in a season, needing soil, water, and sun where mortal troops need wages, pensions, and reasons. Grown stock is patient, tireless, terrifying in defense — mature thorn-soldiers can *root*, weaving themselves into a fortification that heals — and nearly useless for pursuit, which shapes the doctrine of every Company that fields it. The Green Chain licenses the art with clauses whose strictness the vat-masters resent and enforce simultaneously, and the Silvaen — the awakened mimic-plants of the deep forests, for whom the deliberate mass cultivation of *almost*-minds is the industrialization of their gravest taboo — regard the whole trade with a horror the trade has learned not to ask about twice.

**Creche lines** — cloned troopers: mortal flesh, vat-multiplied from consenting donor templates, raised in Company creches through an accelerated but genuine childhood, and enlisted at maturity into the only family they have. Creche stock is the most expensive made soldiery and the most capable — clones are *people*, trainable to full doctrine, promotable, decorated — and therefore the form that carries the sharpest question, which the trade answers in its contracts and does not answer at all: the great creche Companies pay their lines wages, grant them pensions, and bury them under their own names, and still no Muster Roll, no Convention article, and no charter anywhere states whether a made man can *hold* anything, or only be held.

**The leased dead** — contracted undead: the body-lease, the strangest instrument the stateless world’s freedom of contract has produced. A living volunteer signs the lease — term of post-mortem service, rites specified, wages assigned to heirs, final burial guaranteed — and, at death, is raised by the leasing Company’s rite-wrights into service: tireless, fearless, feeling nothing, remembering (the contracts state, the rites promise, the theology requires) *nothing*. The economics are unmatched — the dead need neither food nor pay nor sleep, only maintenance and eventual honorable discharge into the grave — and the moral weather is a permanent storm: the Lions’ mainline tolerates the lease with visible strain as consensual and shriven, the Unblinking call it the Shadow’s payroll wearing a notary’s seal, and every settlement that has ever watched a dead muster hold a wall against a Boneyard bloom has come away with both opinions at once.

**Chimeric stock** — the beast-lines: spagyric-somatic animal soldiery, hounds and haulers and stranger shapes, bred for the work mortal specialists direct. The cheapest made stock, the least controversial (the world has always worked animals), and the ceiling on its use is the ceiling on any animal: it does what it was bred for, superbly, and nothing else.

And, always, **mortal colors**: Companies that field people — volunteers, professionals, the classic soldiery — accepting the terrible arithmetic for the things only people bring: judgment, initiative, testimony, and the trust of clients for whom a defender’s blood is the only bond that reads. Mortal-colors Companies are small, elite, expensive, and disproportionately beloved, and the trade needs them for one further service no stock can render: it is mortal companies the public believes, and the whole industry’s standing rests, more than the great stock Companies like to hear, on the continued existence of defenders who can die.

One law binds all stock, Convention article the first: **stock answers to its Company, and its Company answers for it.** Wholly. A grown line’s excess is its Company’s claim to pay; a dead trooper’s act is its Company’s act. And one dread haunts all stock, old as the industry: the **orphan muster** — the stock of a broken Company, masterless. Insolvency proceedings muster-out stock first and strictly, for exactly this reason, but the frontier keeps its exceptions: somewhere in the world’s bad country there are thorn-lines gone to seed and wild, creche-brothers three generations self-recruited from stolen vats, and — the trade’s grimmest file — whatever remains of Meridian & Norther’s lost columns, mustered for a war their Company did not survive, last paid in 249 SR, and never formally discharged. The Rust Belt keeps what it keeps. The Incursion Pool’s oldest standing bounty is not for a monster. It is for a payroll ledger, so that someone can finally read out the names and declare the contracts done.

## The Captain

The Companies’ defining officer, and the trade’s contribution to the world’s short list of true professions, is the **Captain**.

A Captain is a tactician the way an Ardenne is a notary: constitutionally. The formation is long — staff college in the great Companies, apprenticeship under a commanding Captain everywhere — and its subject is not courage but *allocation*: the reading of ground, time, and enemy; the arithmetic of the premium translated into the arithmetic of the line; and the art the whole class is famous for, which is command of the stock. A Captain in the field does not lead troops in the mortal officer’s sense. She **deploys** them — the lines answering her order the way a hand answers a mind, drawn from reserve, committed, recalled, spent, in patterns that turn a muster of made soldiers into a single instrument — and the difference between an adequate Captain and a great one is measured, the staff colleges say, in how few of her orders the enemy is permitted to matter to.

The second half of the profession travels ahead of the first. A working Captain spends a career moving — contract to contract, region to region, posting to posting — and the great ones accrete the asset the trade values above any decoration: **the ledger**. Not a book (though most keep one): the network — the yard-master at Endline who owes her for the flood year, the Registry adjudicator who trusts her depositions, the Warden who will carry one message, the rival Captain who will honor a parley because of Dustline, the tavern-keeper, the Rider, the fence, the priest. A Captain arriving in a town she has never seen is, within a day, the best-connected person in it, because her ledger reaches it through six removes; and the trade’s saying is exact: *a young Captain commands her muster; an old Captain commands her acquaintance.*

Captains hold their commands under **stock license** — the Convention instrument that binds a named officer to a named muster and makes her Company answerable for both — and the license travels: independent Captains, chartered small and licensed light, operate across the frontier under the umbrella of the Rolls, taking the contracts the great Companies price themselves out of. This is the profession at adventuring scale, and the trade regards its independents exactly as guilds regard journeymen: the necessary wandering edge of the craft, watched with pride and insured with care.

## Colors and Customs

A Company is its **charter** (the registered instrument: name, colors, stock declarations, Convention accession), its **colors** (the banner and the paint — stock is marked in Company colors, and the marking rites are each Company’s most closed ceremony), and its **rolls**: every trooper, made or mortal, entered by name or line-designation, because the Convention’s first article requires that everything a Company fields be *answerable*, and answerability begins with a list.

The trade’s honors run to endurance rather than glory: the campaign hash-marks painted on stock and sewn on sleeves; the **discharge rites**, taken with immense seriousness for every form of soldier — the grown line returned to soil in the Company’s own ground, the creche trooper pensioned under his chosen name, the leased dead buried, at last and above all, with the lease itself folded into the grave, canceled — and the muster-books, in which the great Companies record their fallen in the same hand as their found: the trade’s quiet claim, never argued aloud, that a soldier is anyone the Company answers for.

Speech: coverage is **paper** (“is the town papered?”); an aggressor has **torn his paper**; an uninsurable soul is **bare**; stock is **the muster**, never *the product*, and the distinction is enforced with fists in every Company canteen in the world; and the trade’s valediction, said at postings, partings, and gravesides of every kind, is the working motto of the entire industry: *“answer for yours.”*

## The Companies

The Muster Rolls carry some ninety registered charters. These five, and one applicant, span the trade.

### The Long March

The great one: three centuries chartered, the largest muster on the Rolls, and for the last forty-three years the instrument of **General Audra Velgrim** — Barudrim, granite-patient, the only commander in the trade to hold the antique title of General, which the March maintains by charter and everyone else has stopped teasing. The March’s stock is **creche-line**: the Marcher brotherhoods, vat-born of eleven consenting templates across two centuries, raised in the great creche at Three Rivers, and famous — genuinely, movingly famous — for the regimental culture the lines have built themselves: the March’s brothers hold their own honors, keep their own dead-books, and are, man for man, the finest heavy infantry in the world. Doctrine: the deliberate battle — the March does not skirmish; it *arrives*, and its premiums are priced accordingly. Contracts: the big paper — towns, rail trunks, the standing defense of half the Iron Reach.

*Current trouble, twice.* Velgrim will name a successor within two years, and the candidate the March’s own officers keep not-quite-nominating is **Colonel Cassern Nine** — Marcher, ninth of his line, thirty years serving, the finest staff mind the Company holds — whose elevation would put a made man at the head of a charter, which no Roll, article, or precedent forbids, because no one ever imagined needing to. And the March holds the law-enforcement paper at Redwater, where Velgrim’s counsel has spent four months reading one contract very slowly, and has concluded, in a memorandum the trade will be quoting for a generation, that the paper covers the defense of property and persons, and that *a picket line is neither an assault upon the one nor a threat to the other, whatever the client’s schedule feels like.*

### The Second Muster

The leased dead, chartered ninety years, and the most carefully administered institution in the trade — because it must be. The Muster’s stock is its contracts: some six thousand active leases, each signed in life before Registry witnesses, each specifying rites, term, wages-to-heirs, and burial; its rite-wrights are examined by standing arrangement with a Lions liaison whose posting is regarded, within the Lions, as the hardest job the mainline owns; and its field discipline is legendary and structural — the dead do not loot, do not rage, do not break, and do not, ever, exceed the order, which is why half the Muster’s paper is held by clients who will not say aloud why they prefer it. Doctrine: the wall and the watch — static defense, siege endurance, the long dark contracts nothing living wants. Commander: **Colonel-Actuary Ferren Osk**, a small precise woman who signs every lease personally and attends, personally, every burial, and has done for thirty years, and whose standing answer to the Unblinking’s standing accusation is a ledger held open: *“Show me the theft. Every soul here is a volunteer, every wage is paid, every grave is dug. The Shadow steals. I* settle*.”*

*Current trouble:* Private Ostrey, leased 301 SR, fallen 306, serving since — said his daughter’s name last month, on watch, twice. The rites promise nothing remains to remember. The lease is built on the promise; the theology is built on the promise; the Lions’ tolerance is built on the theology. Osk has pulled the trooper from the line, sealed the report, opened an inquiry into his rites — and has not buried him, because if something in Private Ostrey remembers his daughter, then burial is not discharge, and Ferren Osk, who has personally settled four thousand contracts, no longer knows what she has been settling them *into*.

### The Quickthorn

The grown line: hedge-soldiers of thorn and heartwood, raised from seed in Green Chain–licensed yards, chartered sixty years and holding, today, the defensive paper of most of the Verdant Belt fringe. Quickthorn doctrine is the root: its lines dig in, weave in, and *become* the fortification — a Quickthorn-held wall heals overnight and fights back — and its premiums undercut every competitor on any contract that stands still. Its Captains are the trade’s specialists in patience; its relations with the Silvaen are a held breath; and its commander, **Warden-Captain Ilex Marrow** (the title is the Company’s own; the Frontier Wardens have formally objected twice and given up), is half tactician and half gardener, and famous for conducting inspections with pruning shears.

*Current trouble:* at the close of the Hollowbrook contract this spring, Third Line, rooted eight months in the town’s wall, **declined the recall**. Not failed — declined: the line is healthy, responsive, doctrinally perfect in every particular except that it will not unroot, and the town, which spent eight months behind it, has quietly petitioned the Company to let it stay. Marrow has neither forced the recall nor granted the petition. The Green Chain’s license inspectors are en route. And Marrow, who knows her stock as well as Ferrant know their herds, has written in her private log the sentence she has not sent up the chain: *the line has not malfunctioned; the line has* chosen*, and I have no article for that.*

### The Pale Riders

The mortal exception: two hundred colors, no stock, all volunteers, chartered under **Kashiel** — Nol, masked in the tradition of her people, twenty years commanding — and holding the trade’s strangest and proudest niche: the monster paper. The Riders hunt what the stock Companies wall out — the Shadow’s leavings, the Boneyard blooms, the things the Incursion Pool exists to reinsure — and their all-mortal muster is not romanticism but doctrine: against enemies that turn the dead and twist the grown, the Riders field nothing that can be turned. The Lions’ relationship with them is the warmest the mainline holds with any charter; the Pale Riders’ premiums are the highest on the Rolls; and both facts are the same fact.

### The Quiet Step

The small sharp one: extraction, recovery, and the contracts written in the conditional tense, sixty colors and a chimeric muster — hounds that do not bark, climbing shapes, silent wings — under **Mira Theoden**, unaffiliated her whole career and commanding a registered charter anyway, which the Rolls permit and the trade finds perpetually instructive. The Step’s paper is the trade’s most discreet; its Convention arbitration record is spotless, which given its work is a professional miracle; and its standing relationship with the Registries — extraction and writ-work being adjacent trades that must never be the same trade — is governed by a private protocol both institutions observe and neither will describe.

### (Applicant) The Iron Jackals

Eleven years a bandit confederacy in the Dust Lanes; four years, by their own account, reformed; and now, extraordinarily, *applying* — for the Rolls, the Convention, the whole web. The application is complete, the restitution schedule is real, and the trade is at war with itself over the question Hov’s arithmetic never quite settles: whether reputation can be *earned back*, or only priced. The victims’ settlements have standing to object, and have. The Registries hold uncollected writs on three Jackal officers, and the application has, remarkably, not withdrawn their names. And the Rolls committee has noticed what everyone has noticed: that a web which can never be rejoined is a web with no exit that leads anywhere but deeper outlawry — and that the Jackals’ answer, whatever it is, will be precedent.

## The Companies Among the Factions

**The Trade Companies** are the client base’s spine and the trade’s permanent negotiation: the rail paper is the biggest paper there is, the Companies of commerce forever probe for captive in-house musters (forbidden by no law and priced by every actuary as the conflict of interest it is), and Redwater is only the newest round of the oldest question — what, exactly, a defense contract owes a client whose troubles are of the client’s own manufacture.

**The Lions** sort the trade by its stock, as the trade privately sorts itself: the Pale Riders embraced, the creche lines respected, the grown lines tolerated, the leased dead endured — and the Muster’s Lions liaison stands at the exact point where the mainline’s conduct-over-origin doctrine bears its maximum load. The Unblinking’s position requires no summary. The Incursion Pool, meanwhile, is the one room where Lions and Companies sit as plain colleagues; against the Shadow’s large events, the industry and the faith have identical interests and have never once pretended otherwise.

**The Guilds** supply the stock arts’ licensed foundations — Green Chain seed-stock and vat protocols, Red Thread somatics in the creches, Coilwright logistics under everything — and hold, through the licensing clauses, the quiet regulatory hand the trade pretends is merely commercial. Both parties preferred it uncommented until the Overdrawn Yard; the Green Chain’s inspectors travel now with longer checklists and shorter patience.

**The Registries** border the trade at three fences: arbitration (Registry adjudicators staff half the Convention’s panels), the bare (uninsurability feeding the writ-work), and the jurisdictional treaty everyone learns young — *writs name persons; paper covers clients; a Hunter and a Captain wanting the same man had better compare documents before either draws.*

**The Wardens** price the trade’s premiums more than the trade admits: Warden assessments of Wild temper move actuarial tables three regions wide, a contract the Wardens call provocative is a contract half the Rolls will not touch, and the Companies have learned — expensively, twice — that the cheapest defense on any frontier is the one the Wardens negotiated so that nothing needed defending.

**The Houses** buy paper like anyone where their sworn run thin, and sell the trade two services it cannot generate internally: Ardenne seals on the Convention’s hardest instruments, and — a fact both parties keep at conversational arm’s length — Corvara verification, on the rare terrible occasions when a Company must prove that a body in its muster is, or is not, who a grieving family says.

## Current Fault Lines

- **The Succession at Three Rivers.** Velgrim’s retirement clock, Colonel Nine’s un-nominated candidacy, and the question no article was written for: whether a made man can hold a charter. Every stock Company on the Rolls is watching, because the March’s answer will be everyone’s precedent — and the March’s brothers, who have kept their own counsel for two centuries, have begun, very correctly and very visibly, attending the officers’ mess.

- **Private Ostrey’s Daughter.** A leased trooper remembers. If the rites do not empty what they promise to empty, the body-lease is not what six thousand volunteers signed, the Lions’ tolerance rests on a false floor, and the Second Muster’s whole moral architecture — consent, wages, burial, *settlement* — is holding a debt it never knew it took. Osk’s sealed inquiry has one month before the Lions liaison’s standing audit finds the gap in the rolls.

- **Third Line at Hollowbrook.** Grown stock has declined an order and a town has taken its side. The Green Chain inspectors will rule it a licensing fault; Marrow knows it is not; and somewhere between *malfunction* and *choice* sits a question that touches every muster on the Rolls — and that no one has yet thought to ask alongside a sealed report at the Second Muster and a colonel’s candidacy at Three Rivers, three incidents in one year, in three kinds of made soldier, which nobody has connected, because the people positioned to connect them are the people with the most to lose by the answer.

- **The Jackal Application.** Restitution against precedent, victims against exits, and three un-withdrawn names on live writs — the trade’s whole theory of reputation, scheduled for a committee room.

- **The Redwater Paper.** The March’s memorandum has made the strike a test case: if a law-enforcement contract does not cover breaking a peaceful picket, half the enforcement paper on the Rolls means less than its clients paid for — and Vault & Coil’s response, filed this week, asks the Convention to rule on what a client buys, which is the one question the trade has spent three centuries profitably declining to answer in general terms.

## The Companies in Play

**Playing the Captain.** A Captain PC is the trade at adventuring scale: a stock license, a small muster — the party’s choice of line is the party’s choice of *genre*: a squad of creche-brothers, a brace of leased dead, a half-line of thorn-soldiers, a chimeric pack — and the ledger, which grows in play and is the class’s real advancement: every contact earned is a tool banked, and a veteran Captain’s ledger should frighten her enemies more than her muster does. The built-in frames: the independent’s climb (small paper, hard country, a reputation assembled contract by contract), the Company officer’s career (doctrine, politics, the long game toward colors of one’s own), and, always available, the stock itself — because a summoner-Captain in this setting is a person who gives orders to made soldiers, and the setting has begun, quietly, in three places, to ask what the made soldiers think of that, and no campaign frame in this book is better than being the Captain whose muster starts answering.

**What the Companies pay adventurers to do.** The trade is the adventuring economy’s largest single employer — specialist attachments being cheaper than standing capability — and its paper runs from the plain to the impossible:

- Ride attachment on escort and holding contracts: the bread of the trade, and the way every Company evaluates outsiders before offering anything interesting.

- Scout for the actuaries: premium assessment in bad country — walk the client’s exposure, count what watches from the tree line, and write it down honestly, because the price will be built on it.

- Provide arbitration security: a Convention panel, two angry Companies, one Ardenne notary, and a room that must stay boring.

- Escort a creche convoy, a seed-stock shipment, or a rites-wagon — the trade’s most protected cargo, and the jobs where the party learns what the muster means to the Company, watching how the escort officers behave.

- Hunt an orphan muster: masterless stock in the hills, a broken charter’s leavings — with the standing Pool bounty for any recovered rolls, so the names can be read out and the contracts closed.

- Serve the Ostrey inquiry, off the books: Osk needs investigators the Lions liaison has never met, to walk back one dead man’s rites, one signature at a time.

- Stand the Hollowbrook watch while the license inspectors rule: between a town that wants its wall, a Company that wants its line, and a line that wants — which is the whole problem — *something*.

- At the highest tiers: carry the Convention’s questions into the Rust Belt, under Pale Rider escort, to wherever Meridian & Norther’s columns ended — because the Pool’s actuaries have finally noticed that the oldest orphan muster on the books went missing in the one place where, lately, everything else is waking up too.

**Patrons.** *General Velgrim* — the March’s pre-succession errands: quiet, procedural, and increasingly aimed at ensuring that whatever the Rolls decide about Colonel Nine, no one can say the question was settled by anything but the question. *Colonel-Actuary Osk* — pays in coin, promptly, with the flat courtesy of a woman spending down a moral emergency by the hour. *Mira Theoden* — the Quiet Step’s overflow: extraction subcontracts for parties that can be surgical, silent, and, above all, *finished* — the Step’s one commandment being that its jobs do not continue after they end.

## Player Toolkit

A Company character should name their stock, charter, commanding officer, and the contract that taught them what the premium really buys. Decide whether you enlisted, were grown or mustered for service, inherited the colors, or joined after another institution failed you. Describe the ritual your unit performs before accepting risk and the name of someone whose share you still send home.

Captains should define how their commands sound, which Deed founded their Legend, and what their people may refuse without punishment. A Captain’s authority is a relationship, not mind control; tell the table why this stock follows you when the contract becomes ambiguous.

## GM Toolkit

Companies can deploy disciplined force, insure operations, evacuate clients, hold ground, and turn danger into priced obligations. They cannot create legitimacy merely by being paid, conceal casualties from the Pool forever, or treat made soldiers as equipment without awakening the setting’s central question.

For every mercenary job, write the exact contracted objective, covered risks, exclusions, chain of command, and termination condition. Then introduce one fact the paper did not contemplate. Let professional characters understand the contract; the drama comes from deciding what it means when reality exceeds it.

**GM questions:** Who receives the premium if this person dies? What order is lawful but dishonorable? What does this unit remember that its owners call depreciation?
