# The Monastic Orders

## Origins

The Orders did not begin with an answer. They began with a question, and they have had the great institutional discipline never to finish answering it.

The question, in its oldest recorded form: *if the mind can move the hand, and the hand can move the world, what is the mind waiting for?* Every tradition of practice descends from some version of it. The body is slow, breakable, mortal, and — this is the observation at the root of everything — *apparently optional*: the mind reaches through it to touch the world, and a reach can be trained, and a trained reach can be extended, and the far end of that extension, pursued across a lifetime of cultivation, is a mind that touches the world *directly*. The body transcended. The form left behind.

Transcended *to what* — ascension, dissolution, perfection, power — is the argument, and the argument is the tradition: the monasteries are best understood as a family of institutions that share a practice completely and a purpose not at all.

The practice is old. Older than Standard Reckoning, older by its own texts than the nations whose fall the Reckoning counts from, and it survived the Unmapping for a reason every monastery understands in its bones and teaches on the first day: **the Orders claim nothing.** No demesne, no territory, no wealth beyond the walls and the garden, no flag. A monastery is the smallest possible assertion a community of humans can make against a landscape — we are here, we want nothing, we are trying to leave in the only direction that takes nothing with it — and the Wild, which answers claims in proportion to their scale, appears to hear that assertion as very nearly silence. There are monasteries deep in country no Warden will guide a Company surveyor into, standing untouched for centuries, their bells audible to things that level rail camps. Why the Wild’s sovereigns tolerate them is debated by everyone except the monks, who consider the question answered by the practice itself, and by the Wardens, who have noted which institutions the Wild tolerates and drawn their own quiet conclusions about what the land actually objects to.

The tradition’s outward form is a fusion so old the seams have healed. From one inheritance: the cloister and the cell, the vows, the liturgical hours rung on bells, the scriptoria where the texts are copied by hand, the abbot’s crook and the gate that is never locked. From the other: the training hall, the forms practiced at dawn, the master and the student, the koan-terse teaching stories, the belief that the body must be perfected before it can be honestly renounced. A visitor sees robed figures illuminating manuscripts in the morning and running blade-forms in the yard at dusk, and asks which tradition the monastery follows, and is told — with the small smile this question has earned for centuries — *the practice*.

And the practice begins, for every postulant in every Order, at the same place. It begins with the weapon.

## The First Word

The first attainment of the cultivated mind is a weapon, and the tradition has never pretended to be entirely comfortable with this.

After the postulant years — stillness, breath, the emptying disciplines; two years for the quick, ten for the thorough, the tradition prizing the thorough — the novice’s will learns to stand outside the body, and it stands as an **armament**: a manifested form of force and light, drawn from nothing, dismissed to nothing, unique to its wielder as a voice. The monk does not choose the form. The form is *discovered* — it is simply what that mind’s reach looks like when it finally becomes visible — and this is why the tradition, which renounces violence in nine circumstances out of ten, has never abandoned the weapon as its first teaching. The weapon cannot lie. Everything a mind is — how it meets a problem, where it grips, what it does with resistance — arrives in the form, legible to a master’s eye, and the whole pedagogy of the Orders stands on the moment called **the Reading**: the new-manifested novice stands in the hall, the weapon burning quietly in their hands, and the masters read it, and what they read is the curriculum.

*The weapon is the syllabus.* A straight blade is a mind that meets problems directly and whole, and is taught in lines and proofs. A curved blade flanks — it will always find the approach, never the collision — and is taught in questions. The hammer-minds break problems into pieces and are given big things to break; the needle-minds find the one true point and are nearly ruined by broad instruction, so they are not given it; the hook-minds pull problems out of shape to see what they are attached to; the chain-minds bind, control, and think in systems, and make the tradition’s great administrators, to their lifelong mild disappointment. There are rare forms every generation — the shield-minds, who solve nothing and protect everything; the key-minds, who open, and who supply most of the tradition’s talent for the space-cutting arts; and, a handful a century, the **formless**, whose will manifests as no weapon at all, only a pressure in the room, and about whom every Order’s private instructions say the same two things: honor them, and watch them.

The Reading is also why the Orders pursue the self-taught. Manifestation is learnable outside the walls — rarely, roughly, by accident of temperament or desperation — and a wild blade is an unread one: a mind armed with its own reach and taught by nothing but its circumstances. The Orders seek such people out, with an interest the unaffiliated do not always experience as flattering; the walkers carry standing instructions on the subject; and the tradition’s honesty about its motives is characteristic. *We are not afraid of what they are*, the instruction reads, in the version copied at Stillwater. *We are afraid of what they were never shown they are.*

One more thing about the weapon, and it is the thing the whole tradition orbits. The form is innate — and sometimes it **changes**. A monk of decades’ standing manifests one morning and the blade is *different*: the stylus become an axe, the chain become a straight sword, the shield become, in one recorded and much-discussed case, a bell. Mainstream doctrine holds the changed blade as the gravest of warnings — the mind’s foundation shifted, the self in flux, the practitioner to be rested, watched, and re-Read — because the alternative reading is unbearable: that the first Reading was not the truth of the person but only the truth of the person *so far*, and that a mind might legitimately become someone else. The minority who hold that alternative reading have a name, and a founder, and a section of this chapter, further down, that every other Order wishes were shorter.

## The Way

The cultivation past the first attainment runs up two limbs of one body, and the tradition’s shorthand for them is **the Edge and the Eye**.

**The Edge** is the outward way: the will against the world. Its ladder is what the blade can cut, and the rungs are doctrine, climbed across a working lifetime. Flesh first, which any manifested blade cuts from the day of the Reading. Then *materials* — timber, stone, worked steel — which is where most honest practitioners spend their middle decades. Then *magic*: wards, workings, standing enchantment, the point at which the Enclaves began keeping files. Then *space* — the art the world calls Slash Space and the tradition calls **the opened way**: a cut through distance itself, stepped through, sealed behind. And past that, attested in every era by a handful and described adequately by none of them, the blade that cuts *things ordinary language does not hold* — connections, inevitabilities, the masters’ texts say; one late manuscript says *debts*, and the scriptoria have argued about that line for two hundred years.

On the opened way, one point of doctrine matters enough to state plainly, because the Enclaves dispute it and the difference is not academic. The Enclave theorists hold that any passage through distance must transit *somewhere* — the Wyrd, presumably, the medium of every other supernatural travel — and that the monks are therefore doing an unexamined and unprotected thing. The Orders’ teaching is that the cut does not go through anywhere: *it declines to go through.* The will does not borrow a road from the dreaming; it briefly refuses the premise that a road is required. No monk has ever been lost in transit, met anything in transit, or experienced transit at all — there is no *in* — and the Order of the Standing World, for whom this fact is nearly scripture, invites the theorists annually to produce one contrary case, and annually the theorists produce instead a paper, and relations continue warm.

**The Eye** is the inward way: the will upon minds, beginning with the practitioner’s own. Its foundations are the still mind — a discipline that makes senior monks nearly impossible to deceive, terrify, or magically sway — and its ascent runs through perception (the reading of intent and feeling in a face, a stride, a room, refined past anything that should be called intuition), *speech without voice* between consenting practitioners, the memory arts, and, at its height, the disciplines of **going out**: the mind loosed from the body deliberately, briefly, and returned — which the tradition regards not as a power but as a *rehearsal*, and speaks of exactly that plainly.

The Eye is where the tradition’s second inheritance lives, and where its heaviest vow binds. The old schools of the mind — the perceivers, the mind-speakers, the memory-workers whom earlier generations trained apart and the world called Mentalists — were folded into the Orders across a long century of convergence, on the shared recognition that theirs was the same cultivation begun from the other limb; and they brought with them the ethical architecture the Orders now enforce for the whole world’s peace of mind: **the Vow of the Closed Door**. No entry into an unwilling mind. Not for mercy, not for justice, not for the client, not for the abbot. The vow is trained as reflex rather than held as rule — a senior of the Eye does not *refrain* from intrusion; the intrusion has been made, by decades of practice, into something their will no longer has a shape for — and it is enforced, where training fails, by the only body all Orders answer to on any subject: the examiners of Still Water, whose visits are rare, courteous, and remembered. It is this architecture that the Noble Houses borrowed against when House Corvara sought endorsement of its blood-reading protocols: the mind is the Orders’ province, the Corvara were at pains to prove their palate never crosses into it, and the decennial renewal of that endorsement is conducted by both parties with perfect gravity and something adjacent to friendship.

Around both limbs, the life: **the Hours**, eight bells from dark to dark, each with its exercise — forms at first light, copy-work in the morning, labor at midday, because the tradition holds that a will cultivated past a body that no longer sweeps floors has been aimed wrong; the vows of small property and open gates; and the deaths. A monk’s death is attended like anyone’s — and then, a few times a century, it is not a death. A master in the last season of a long cultivation is found absent: cell ordered, robe folded, body nowhere, blade gone with the mind that made it. The tradition calls these **the Departures**, records them with scrupulous unexcitement, and refuses — institutionally, doctrinally, permanently — to say what they are. Every Order reads the Departures as evidence for its own answer. That the evidence is real, and that it proves nothing in particular, is perhaps the only description of the tradition’s whole subject that all five Orders would sign.

## Structure

A monastery is small — thirty souls is common, two hundred is a great house — and flat: **postulants** at the gate-work and the emptying disciplines; **novices** from the Reading until the master’s sash; **brothers and sisters**, the working tradition, who teach, copy, till, and walk; **masters**, who have climbed some real distance up one limb or both; and one **abbot or abbess**, chosen by the masters, holding the house’s judgment and its seal. Orders are families of monasteries sharing a doctrine of the end; houses of different Orders treat one another as cousins — hospitality absolute, theology at dinner.

Between the houses move the **walkers**: masters who have taken the road as their cell, carrying techniques, letters, Readings for houses too small to keep a full bench of masters, and the tradition’s quiet errands. A walker’s arrival is half festival, half inspection, and wholly welcome; a walker is also, when a house has gone strange — doctrine curdling, an abbot’s blade changed and unreported, a novice’s manifestation botched — the way the tradition finds out. The walkers answer to no Order. This is understood by everyone to be the point.

The gate custom binds every house of every Order: **anyone may sit at the gate.** They are fed from the first evening, sheltered from the third, and asked nothing until they ask something — and if what they ask is entry, the answer is the postulant’s mat and the emptying years, regardless of who they were. The tradition’s power is the only one in the world that arrives by neither blood nor lightning nor bargain: it is *learned*, and the gate is where the learning starts, and the Orders hold the openness of the gate as their oldest vow precisely because everything else about the way is so long and so hard. The gate is open. The way is the wall.

And the tradition breaks people. It says so, at the gate, in the plain manner it says everything: the emptying years unhouse some minds rather than steadying them; the Reading occasionally reveals a form its bearer cannot live with; and, rarest and worst, a cultivated will can **sunder** — the discipline failing at depth, the blade manifesting unbidden, changed, or *independent*: acting on the wielder’s unacknowledged mind rather than their governed one. A Sundered monk is the tradition’s whole pastoral machinery mobilized at once — Still Water’s healers, the walkers’ watch, the home house’s patient years — and, when all of that fails, a Sundered master loose in the world is among the most dangerous beings the setting contains: a will that cuts what its owner will not admit to wanting cut. The Orders hunt their own lost with a tenderness and a totality that outsiders find difficult to watch. They do not post writs. They send the person’s teacher.

## The Orders

Five Orders hold most of the tradition’s houses; dozens of independent monasteries orbit them. What divides the five is the only question that matters: *transcend to what?*

### The Order of the Threshold

*The end is ascent.* The Threshold holds that a perfected mind crosses *upward* — that the Heavens are not a birthplace but a destination, that the celestial hosts include those who climbed, and that the cultivation is a stair whose top is union with the divine order. Threshold houses are the most Western in furniture — their Hours are sung, their scriptoria gild, their relationship with the church network is warm and their relationship with the Lions warmer than any other Order manages — and their reading of the Departures is serene: the departed arrived. Their burden is the wound they share with the Heavens they aim for: a tradition of ascent must explain why the ascended do not come back and say so, and the Threshold’s honest answer — *perhaps the crossing takes the part that would want to* — is spoken only at senior tables, late.

### The Order of the Unstruck Bell

*The end is release.* The Bell holds that the spinning top — the whole armature of planes and wars, Heavens and Shadow, Dream and Nightmare — is a structure one can decline; that the perfected will’s last act is to leave the entire cosmology, completely, taking nothing, owing nothing; and that this exit is not death, not ascent, and not describable, being precisely the thing the structure contains no words for. The name is the doctrine: the sound a bell holds before it is struck — whole, real, and not yet part of the world’s noise. Bell houses are the sparest and most Eastern in temper, keep the longest silences, produce the tradition’s deepest Eye-work — and are watched, politely, by everyone, because a doctrine of total exit is separated by one impatient reading from a doctrine that the structure *deserves* to be left, and the Bell’s own masters police that reading with more vigilance than any outsider knows to ask for.

### The Order of the Standing World

*The end is here.* The Standing World holds the tradition’s strangest and, its masters say, most obvious doctrine: that the Material World is the one plane that does not dream — the one place the Wyrd does not reach, the ground everything else fights over — and that the trained will is not a borrowing from any other plane but the Material World’s *own* power: the one force in existence native to the ground floor. Arcane craft leans on the question of its origin; the divine descends from above; the blood is bargained or forged or marked — and the will is grown *here*, from nothing but a mind and its years, and it is therefore, the Order teaches, what transcendence is *for*: not leaving the world but becoming what the world can raise in its own defense, unborrowed and unowed. Standing World houses run the tradition’s hardest Edge-work, sit closest to the frontiers, take the Slash Space dispute personally, and are, alone among the Orders, on comfortable terms with the question of the Machine — their texts speak of the Material’s mute preference to be left alone by Heavens and Shadow alike, and of the will as *that preference, finally armed*. The Lions do not know what to do with them. The Standing World finds this restful.

### The Order of Still Water

*The end is clarity.* Still Water descends most directly from the old schools of the mind, and holds that transcendence is not a crossing but a *finishing*: the self clarified until nothing in it is unexamined, unowned, or capable of surprising its owner — and that what such a mind does afterward (ascend, exit, remain) is its own business and probably various. The Order keeps the tradition’s inward arts, trains its healers of minds — the only practitioners in the world who treat madness as a craft with a literature, consulted quietly by Houses, Enclaves, and once, in a case no one names, by the trusteeship of the Talvecq lands — and staffs the examiners of the Closed Door, the nearest thing the whole tradition has to a shared authority. Still Water’s houses are the ones that feel least like fortresses and most like wells. Its burden is professional: the healers of minds carry what they hear, the vow forbids the shortcuts, and the Order’s own seniors are Read more often than any others, at their own standing request.

### The Order of the Second Reading

*The end can wait.* The heresy, eighteen years old and growing.

Its founder, **Vasha Orin**, was a chain-mind of the Standing World — a systems thinker, a binder, forty years cultivated, a master whose blade had administered three houses — and in 294 SR she was on the road near the Hollow Marches when the Broken Track took six hundred souls in a night, and she spent that winter with the families, and in the spring, at forms, her blade came out *changed*: the chain become a plain woodsman’s axe. She stood for the mandated re-Reading and then declined the mandated rest, and the sentence she said instead is the founding text of her Order and the splinter in the tradition’s palm: *“The first Reading told you how I thought. This one tells you what I learned. If the blade may change, so may the answer — and my answer is that a will cultivated in sight of the world’s suffering, and spent only on its own perfection, has misread itself.”*

The Second Reading holds that the changed blade is not a warning but a *revelation* — the mind legitimately become someone new — and that the tradition’s abstentions (no sides, no contracts, no interventions) are not humility but hoarding: the one learnable power in the world, kept behind walls by the people who learned it. Its houses are few, young, and mobile; its members walk more than they sit; and its works are already famous, none more so than the **Night of Cut Ledgers**, four years ago, when Second Reading monks opened the debtors’ stockade at a Vault & Coil works town and cut — physically, irreversibly, with blades that by then could cut *records* — every debt-ledger in the Company office. The Company demanded the monks. The mainline Orders refused to surrender them and refused to shelter them, in the same breath, and have been living inside that sentence ever since. The Second Reading’s numbers grow after every intervention. Its founder’s axe, the walkers report, has begun to change again — and Orin has not stood for a third Reading, and has not said why.

## The Orders Among the Factions

**The Lions** circle the tradition uneasily. The power is not divine, not arcane, not blood — it fits no doctrine, threatens no doctrine, and answers to a discipline even the Unblinking privately admire; the mainline’s relationship with the Threshold is nearly warm, and Karsh’s own tract devotes a famously inconclusive chapter to the question of whether a power grown entirely from the self can owe anything to anyone. The Second Reading has ended the comfort of the abstraction: monks intervening in the world are monks the Lions must have a position on, and the position does not exist yet, and both institutions know who is being tested by that.

**The Enclaves** and the Orders are opposite answers to the same terror — power that must be carried — and their mutual regard is real and permanently edged: the Slash Space dispute, the Enclaves’ unrequited hunger to study the will as they study the mass, and the Orders’ standing observation, delivered once a generation with the gentlest possible cruelty, that the Enclaves teach children to carry what erupted into them, while the gate teaches anyone to carry what they chose. Northwatch and the Standing World’s northern houses cooperate in the field regardless, in the way of frontier professionals everywhere.

**The Houses** touch the tradition at two points: the Corvara endorsement, renewed each decade by Still Water; and the gate, which does not check blood-books — there are Dynasts behind monastery walls, unexpressed House children who chose the one power that asks nothing of the body, and at least one abbess whom a certain blood-book lists, in careful ink, as *released to the practice*.

**The Companies** since the Cut Ledgers regard the tradition as a solved problem that has stopped being solved. The mainline Orders’ neutrality was the world’s most reliable; Vault & Coil’s standing demand sits at every abbot’s gate; and the Companies’ quiet countermeasure — ledgers copied in triplicate and stored in three towns — is noted by the Second Reading with open satisfaction, as the first tribute the powerless have ever collected from an institution by *implication alone*.

**The Wardens** and the monks share the deep country and a temperament — two traditions of paying attention that want nothing — and the Wardens’ private canon includes the fact recorded at this chapter’s start: the Wild tolerates the monasteries. Wardens escort walkers without being asked. Certain monasteries leave a bell-rope where a Warden can reach it. Neither tradition has ever formalized any of this, on the shared understanding that formalizing it would be the first claim either had made.

**The unaffiliated** include the tradition’s whole recruiting ground and its wild edge: the self-taught blades, the gate-sitters, the released. A former monk keeps the still mind and the manifested will for life — the cultivation does not un-happen — and the tradition’s relationship with its released is the gentlest of any faction’s: the gate that let them in does not close behind them, and a released brother who returns at sixty is fed from the first evening, like anyone.

## Current Fault Lines

- **The Standing Blade.** Four months ago, at a small independent house in the High Cradle, the master Ioan Vesh — Bell-trained, sixty years cultivated, a needle-mind — was found Departed: cell ordered, robe folded, body gone. And the blade *remained*: manifest, upright in the center of the cell, sustained by no mind anyone can find, unchanged since. Every doctrine of every Order breaks on it somewhere — a blade cannot outlive its will; a Departure takes everything; a needle does not stand. The house has sealed the cell, the walkers have converged, pilgrims of five Orders are on the roads, and the Standing World and the Unstruck Bell, whose readings of the event are precise opposites, have both requested custody, in letters of escalating beauty.

- **Orin’s Third Reading.** The founder’s axe is changing again, and she will not stand for the Reading her own doctrine invented. If the Second Reading’s revelation-of-change applies to everyone but its founder, the heresy dies of its own text; if she stands and the blade shows something her movement cannot carry, it dies faster. Her seniors know this. Her seniors are, quietly, in schism about whether to compel their own founder, which the rest of the tradition watches with grief and exactly no satisfaction.

- **The Vault & Coil Demand.** The Company has moved from demanding the Cut Ledgers monks to pricing them: a standing private bounty, unregistered, uncertified, of the kind the Registries exist to prevent — and the hunters it attracts are exactly that kind. The mainline Orders will not shelter the Second Reading and will not see monks sold, and abbots who spent lifetimes on the Eye are discovering the limits of a neutrality that both sides have stopped believing.

- **A Sundered Master.** A hammer-mind of the Standing World, Sundered two years ago and lost from care last spring, is somewhere in the eastern Verdant Belt. Structures fail along her line of travel — clean cuts, load-bearing points, no one yet harmed, the precision itself the frightening part: her governed mind is still protecting people from her ungoverned one, and losing. Her teacher has gone after her, alone, as the tradition does. He is eighty-one. The house that let him go is hiring, very quietly, people to follow at a distance he must not notice.

- **The Gate Question.** The Endline Charter’s success has reached the Cradle: a petition circulates among younger masters to found teaching houses in the rail towns — the gate carried to where the people are, the postulant years compressed, the way made survivable for those who cannot leave their livelihoods for a mountain. The elders hear the compassion and see the arithmetic: the emptying years are the wall that keeps the Sundered rare, and a tradition that shortens the wall to widen the gate is running an experiment whose failures will manifest armed.

## The Orders in Play

**Playing the practice.** A monk PC is the setting’s one chosen power: no eruption, no blood, no bargain — a gate, a mat, and the years. The character hooks are built into the chapter: the blade whose form is a fact about you that a stranger with the right training can *read across a room*; the Order whose answer to the great question you carry or quarrel with; the vow architecture that other factions will test the moment they learn what your Eye can do; and, always available, the changed blade — the mechanically simple, dramatically bottomless event by which the campaign tells your character that they are no longer who they were Read to be. A wild blade — self-taught, unread, sought by walkers whose interest is genuine and not entirely optional — is the unaffiliated variant, and one of the strongest solo backstories the setting offers.

**What the Orders pay adventurers to do.** They don’t — and that is the shape of the work. The Orders have no treasury and hire no one; what they have is the gate-debt: hospitality, healing, teaching, and Readings extended to anyone, for decades, until half the frontier owes the tradition something no coin denominates — and when a house asks the party for help, it is calling that kind of debt, and everyone within a hundred miles knows what answering it is worth. Typical asks, by tier:

- Escort a walker through country where the roads have gone bad — and learn, over three weeks of fireside instruction you did not ask for, why walkers rarely need the escort and always accept it.

- Carry a sealed Reading from a small house to a distant master whose eyes it needs; the seal matters; the party will be told exactly why only if it holds.

- Sit the outer watch at a monastery hosting a re-Reading that has drawn the wrong kind of pilgrim.

- Find a self-taught blade before their circumstances finish teaching them — the tradition’s version of the Enclave rider, gentler in method and no less urgent.

- Follow the teacher who went after the Sundered master, at the distance he must not notice, with instructions for the day his tenderness fails.

- Stand between a Second Reading cell and the unregistered hunters holding Vault & Coil paper — without joining either side, if the party can manage what the abbots could not.

- Guard the sealed cell of the Standing Blade through a season of pilgrims, doctrine, and at least one visitor who does not cast a shadow correctly.

- At the highest tiers: be in the hall, by some chain of debts and invitations, on the day Vasha Orin finally stands for her Third Reading — because the tradition’s texts are unanimous that *someone* always witnesses the moment a way divides, and unanimous about nothing else.

**Patrons.** *An abbess of Still Water* — the mind-healers’ work generates the tradition’s most delicate errands: patients to be found, families to be told, and the rare client whose troubles turn out to be someone else’s craft. *A walker* — the closest the tradition has to a quest-giver: itinerant, unhurried, alarmingly well informed, and in the habit of repaying favors with teaching, which parties discover is the most valuable currency in the setting that cannot be stolen. *Vasha Orin* — for parties whose consciences run ahead of their caution: the founder’s asks are real, righteous, escalating, and the axe is changing, and no one who works for her is confident they are working for the same woman the first Reading described.

## Player Toolkit

A Mentalist should name their Order, first Reading, teacher, psychic discipline, and the thought or emotion that makes Overload tempting. Describe a mind blade, mind gun, psicrystal, or other focus as an expression of self: what does another trained Mentalist learn merely by seeing it? Decide which boundary around consent your character treats as absolute and which circumstance has made that boundary difficult.

Choose one lesson you quote too often, one ordinary pleasure monastery life taught you to value, and one conclusion your Order reached about you that you intend to disprove. A released or self-taught Mentalist should also name the walker who continues to check on them.

## GM Toolkit

Orders can teach psychic control, heal certain injuries of mind and identity, recognize disciplined mental intrusion, shelter seekers, and preserve knowledge outside commercial webs. They cannot compel a member’s enlightenment, guarantee that a Reading remains true, or use neutrality to erase the consequences of what they understand.

Psychic effects require boundaries at the table. State what is sensed, what remains private, and when consent matters. Offer impressions and structures rather than reading a character sheet aloud. Overload should buy a meaningful stronger effect while creating strain or exposure tied to the discipline used.

**GM questions:** What did the Reading perceive correctly but interpret wrongly? Which thought is this NPC protecting? What would cause the monastery’s fixed point to move?
