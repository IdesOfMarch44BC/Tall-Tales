# Running the Frontier

This chapter turns the setting material into decisions at the table. It distinguishes common knowledge from restricted knowledge, gives players a shared basis for making characters, and gives the GM a procedure for building settlements, jobs, journeys, and faction pressure without needing to invent a nation-state the setting does not have.

## What Everyone Knows

The frontier is stateless, not lawless. No crown claims the whole map. Town customs, Company contracts, Guild marks, Registry writs, House demesnes, church obligations, and old treaties overlap. Most disputes are not about whether a rule exists, but which promise governs here and who is willing to enforce it.

The old nations ended during the Unmapping. Everyone knows that large territorial claims draw the Wild’s attention; few agree whether the danger is a law of nature, an old punishment, or a story institutions tell to preserve the present order. Complete maps are treated with the unease reserved for loaded weapons. Local maps, route books, survey fragments, and remembered paths are ordinary.

The five planes are real enough to affect daily language and practical enough to appear on a character sheet. The Heavens answer obligation and mercy; the Machine answers logic and construction; the Shadow answers ambition and concealment; the Wild answers vigor and growth; the Wyrd answers curiosity and feeling. Ordinary people know the associations. Scholars, priests, and practitioners disagree about almost everything beyond them.

Most people belong to a town, family, trade, congregation, crew, or route rather than a great faction. Factions matter because they make distant promises enforceable. A mark, sigil, writ, book, or known name lets a stranger call on help three regions away—and lets obligations follow them just as far.

## What Is Not Common Knowledge

Material labeled here or elsewhere as a **GM truth** should not be assumed by player characters. A character may know it through a suitable background, discovery, or Reputation.

- The connections collected under the Deep Currents in *Inter-Faction Relations* are patterns no institution presently possesses whole.

- The exact motives of sovereigns, celestials, Fey, and other planar beings are never common knowledge, even when their public acts are.

- An institution’s hidden crimes, sealed records, compromised leaders, and private succession disputes belong first to the GM. Members may know rumors or one side of the argument.

- A named current event is public only where its chapter says it is. News travels by rail, rider, relay, and gossip. Distance and interested editors matter.

When uncertain, give a player the public fact, one credible interpretation favored by their community, and one question that remains open. Lore should create choices, not test whether the player memorized the book.

## Character Creation in the Setting

Every player should answer the following before play. Short answers are enough.

1. **Where are you from?** Name a settlement, demesne, circuit, Company line, monastery, enclave, hall, or stretch of road. Decide what people there eat, fear, and recognize as respectable.

2. **Who taught you?** Classes describe capability; teachers explain its habits. Name a person, institution, creature, book, accident, or hard season.

3. **Who are your people?** This can be a faction, but need not be. Name the people whose trouble can interrupt your plans without asking permission.

4. **What paper or sign do you carry?** A Guild mark, Registry seal, Lions sigil, Company letter, House token, Warden glyph, monastery cord, unpaid invoice, family recipe, or nothing. Decide who respects it and who resents it.

5. **What do you owe?** Choose a person or institution and describe the obligation in a sentence. Debt may be money, secrecy, rescue, apprenticeship, blood, hospitality, or a promise.

6. **What crossed the horizon?** Ordinary people can remain home. Something made this character’s capabilities, obligations, or enemies larger than one community. Say what happened.

7. **Which faction gets you wrong?** Choose one institution and state the mistaken assumption its members commonly make about you.

8. **What would bring you back?** Name the news that would make you abandon a profitable expedition and return home immediately.

### Connecting the Party

Choose at least two bonds per character: one to the character on the left and one to the character on the right.

- They witnessed one of your Deeds and tell the story differently.
- You carry paper that makes you responsible for them.
- Their people sheltered yours during a bad season.
- You know what their institution asked them to conceal.
- You were rivals for the same teacher, writ, post, or contract.
- They found you after your first uncontrolled use of power.
- You share a creditor, enemy, route, dead friend, or unfinished promise.
- One of you can return home only if the other comes too.

The party should also choose a shared answer to three questions: who first paid you together, which community presently trusts you, and what failure people still attach to your collective name.

## Building a Frontier Settlement

A useful settlement needs six facts. The GM can prepare them in a few sentences.

1. **Reason:** Why does this place exist here? Water, a station, a crossing, a mine, a hall, defensible ground, pilgrimage, a sovereign treaty, or stubborn inheritance.

2. **Promise:** What arrangement keeps it alive? A Company service contract, House protection, Lions circuit, Warden understanding, Guild industry, mercenary premium, or a compact among local families.

3. **Price:** What does that arrangement cost ordinary people? Scrip dependence, tithes, secrecy, labor, conscription, restricted travel, dangerous production, or an obligation inherited from founders.

4. **Face:** Who can speak for the place, and who actually decides? These should be different people whenever possible.

5. **Stranger test:** What must outsiders do to be treated as guests rather than weather? Surrender longarms, buy a round, state their business at the chapel, show papers, exchange route news, or wait to be invited across a marker.

6. **Change:** What stopped working recently? This is the adventure. The train is late, the dead are dreaming, the well tastes of iron, children know an unfamiliar song, a contract has two copies, or the Warden signs point inward.

### A Settlement’s Three Layers

Prepare one detail at each layer.

- **Daily life:** food, work rhythm, clothing, courtship, worship, entertainment, and what closes after dark.

- **Local power:** who owns stores, controls transport, keeps records, mediates violence, and can call help from elsewhere.

- **Deep ground:** what was here before the settlement and what remains attentive beneath its ordinary life.

## Law Without a State

When characters ask whether something is legal, determine four things instead of consulting a universal code.

1. **Jurisdiction:** Which relationship governs—local custom, contract, writ, demesne law, Guild procedure, church discipline, treaty, or the rules of a sovereign court?

2. **Standing:** Who is allowed to complain, investigate, judge, and enforce?

3. **Proof:** What counts here—witness, seal, ledger, oath, relic, psychic Read, blood-book, or demonstrated fact?

4. **Remedy:** What makes the injured party whole—Rings, service, surrender, exile, voiding paper, a duel, public acknowledgment, or violence?

Conflicts become adventures when two valid jurisdictions produce incompatible remedies. The Registries may hold a lawful writ that stops at a demesne stone; a Company contract may authorize work a Warden assessment calls catastrophic; a Guild mark may certify a procedure a town refuses to permit.

## Faction NPCs

Build a faction NPC from five choices.

- **Office:** What are they authorized to do?
- **Doctrine:** Which principle do they sincerely believe?
- **Need:** What must happen before the next report, train, Gather, council, or season?
- **Limit:** What can they not spend, reveal, admit, cross, or order?
- **Human tell:** What ordinary habit survives the office?

Do not make every faction representative the faction’s average opinion. Give them one orthodox belief, one local adaptation, and one private disagreement. Institutions become credible when members can disagree without ceasing to belong.

### Reaction to the Party

Track faction reaction in four broad states.

| State | What it means | Typical response |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Unknown | The party is unproven or known only by rumor | Papers checked, payment held in escrow, close supervision |
| Useful | They completed clean work or possess needed capability | Ordinary contracts, introductions, limited shelter |
| Trusted | They kept faith when breaking it would have paid | Sensitive work, institutional backing, access to restricted people or places |
| Entangled | The faction now considers the party part of its problem | Protection and demands arrive together; neutrality becomes difficult |

A faction can respect the party while opposing its current goal. Trust measures confidence in behavior, not agreement.

## A Job That Belongs in Tall Tales

Every strong job combines at least three pressures.

- **The stated work:** escort, recover, investigate, hold, negotiate, survey, hunt, repair, or deliver.
- **The human claim:** someone will bear the cost if the party succeeds exactly as contracted.
- **The institutional edge:** a faction’s authority ends, overlaps, or contradicts another’s.
- **The strange fact:** the world is acting as though a story, promise, machine, bloodline, or boundary were literally load-bearing.
- **The clock:** a train, hearing, winter, wound, maturity, market run, celestial arrival, or approaching sovereign.

Before the session, write what the employer believes, what they omitted, and what they are genuinely wrong about. These are different categories. An employer who knows everything is a briefing document, not a source of play.

## Travel and Information

The frontier is connected unevenly. Rail is fast where maintained and politically expensive where contested. Roads belong to seasons. Warden relays carry exact messages but not casual traffic. Lions circuits carry news through relationships. Company factors know commercial facts quickly and everything else through a commercial lens. Registry boards know names and jurisdictions. Guild halls know materials and procedures. Monasteries remember slowly and for a very long time.

For each journey, choose one pressure rather than rolling for empty distance:

- the route is physically failing;
- passage requires a promise;
- another traveler carries consequential news;
- the map and the remembered path disagree;
- the party can arrive quickly only by becoming visible;
- weather forces hospitality with someone inconvenient;
- a local custom reveals who has traveled here before;
- the destination changes while the party is en route.

News should arrive with a source. Tell the players who claims the fact and what that source wants. Accurate information can still be partial, late, or framed.

## Using the Wild and the Other Planes

The planes are not encounter tables. They are pressures with recognizable grammar.

- **Heavens:** introduce an obligation that makes power morally specific.
- **Machine:** make the stated solution work literally and expose the omitted requirement.
- **Shadow:** offer an effective shortcut that changes who can trust whom.
- **Wild:** let life, appetite, weather, or territory exceed the intended boundary.
- **Wyrd:** connect emotion and curiosity to an unexpected second consequence.

When a plane complicates an action, connect the complication to what the character asked it to do. Avoid random punishment. The cost should make the success more meaningful and create a later choice.

Sovereigns and great planar beings should enter play first through effects: changed migration, impossible weather, repeated dreams, altered language, new taboos, or lesser beings behaving with shared purpose. A direct appearance is a campaign event, not a wandering monster.

## Safety and Tone

The setting includes body transformation, institutional abuse, religious conflict, medical horror, coercive psychic practices, slavery-adjacent created troops, and lethal injury. Establish lines and veils before play, especially around loss of identity, involuntary transformation, surgery, mind-reading, and harm to communities.

The intended tone is not misery without leverage. Institutions are compromised because they are made by people, but they also feed towns, keep promises, rescue children, preserve roads, and restrain worse powers. Give players people worth protecting inside every structure they oppose and credible reasons someone decent might remain within it.

## Campaign Frames

### The Standing Crew

A factor, chapter, board, hall, or independent town keeps the party on retainer. Each job exposes another part of the patron’s web until the crew must decide whether it serves the institution, reforms it, leaves it, or becomes a rival center of obligation.

### The Long Route

The party is responsible for a circuit, rail branch, relay chain, or pilgrimage road. Repeated settlements change between visits. Their earlier choices become local history, and the campaign’s map grows from relationships rather than comprehensive survey.

### The Convergence

Choose one flashpoint from *Inter-Faction Relations*. Every character has a different tie to it. The campaign begins with small evidence and rises toward a hearing, treaty, war, collapse, or revelation that no faction can manage alone.

### The Unpapered Company

The party begins unaffiliated and takes work from everyone. Their shared Reputation becomes a mark more reliable than institutional paper. The long question is when the world begins treating them as a faction and what promise they make when it does.

### The Bad Map

The party inherits a route book whose fragments imply someone completed part of a forbidden comprehensive survey. Every institution wants a different use made of it; the Wild appears to be editing it; and one place on the map insists, through several independent witnesses, that it does not exist.

## Preparing the First Session

Bring five things:

1. A settlement built from the six facts above.
2. An employer with an office, need, and limit.
3. A job with a human claim and institutional edge.
4. One sign that the deep ground is active.
5. A question addressed to a specific player character’s origin, debt, or paper.

End the first session by making the party choose which promise to keep, not merely which enemy to defeat. That choice is the frontier in miniature.
