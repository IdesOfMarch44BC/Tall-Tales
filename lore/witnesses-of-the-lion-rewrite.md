# Witnesses of the Lion

## Origins

The Lions did not begin with Leo.

They began with the people who survived something and needed to understand what had survived them.

In or around 151 SR, a demonic incursion overran the frontier town of Redoubt. The town was held — barely, at terrible cost — by a group of fighters and traveling priests who had arrived there separately, for separate reasons, and discovered in the course of the Long Night that they shared something more specific than circumstance. Not just the willingness to fight. Not just the capacity for it. Something in the quality of attention they brought to the work: a refusal to look away from what they were fighting, to reduce it to abstraction, to pretend that the souls behind them were anything less than fully real people whose full reality was exactly what was at stake.

They stayed together afterward. Not because anyone organized them into staying — because none of them could explain to anyone who hadn’t been there what the experience had meant, and they had found, briefly, people who didn’t require the explanation.

Word traveled, as it does in frontier communities where survival depends on information moving faster than threats. Fighters and priests who recognized the description of that quality of attention found their way to the group. The group became a company. The company became an institution. And in 168 SR the institution became large enough that Leo noticed it, and Leo, being Leo, went to see it in person rather than sending anyone else.

What he found was an organization that had independently arrived at the central commitment of his own tradition: that the people being protected are not abstractions, that the threats being fought are real in ways that require full acknowledgment rather than comfortable distance, and that the work is worth doing because the alternative is unthinkable, not because the reward is sufficient. He did not found the Lions. He *found* them. The distinction is the first thing every Page and Novice is taught, and the organization has never allowed it to blur: they are not Leo’s creation. They are what his tradition recognized when it saw it.

The name carries the founding logic in compressed form. *Witness* is the verb. *Lion* is the tradition. A Lion who forgets that their primary function is a specific quality of attention — who reduces the work to fighting and the protected to populations — has misunderstood what they joined. This is not a devotional nicety. The Lions have learned across a century and a half that the quality of attention brought to the work determines the quality of the work itself: the Zealot who fights for fully real people makes better decisions under pressure, keeps better faith with the communities they hold, and recovers from terrible engagements with less of the particular spiritual damage that accumulates in those who saw terrible things and pretended they were abstract. Witness is not the Lions’ ornament. It is their engineering.

## Leo

Leo is the first and greatest of the leonine guardinals — the celestial lions of the Heavens’ host, of whom every other is, in some sense that the celestials themselves decline to make precise, his descendant or his echo. He is older than Standard Reckoning, older than the nations that fell before it, and present in the historical record across all of it: never as a ruler, never as a general of the Heavens’ campaigns, always in the same role, arriving where the line between the Shadow and some huddled fraction of the living was about to fail, and holding it.

The churches venerate him as a demigod. His own position on this is documented, consistent, and ignored: he is not a god, he does not want worship, and prayer addressed to him as a deity is prayer addressed incorrectly. *“Pray to the Heavens,”* runs the most-quoted of his recorded remarks, *“and if I am the answer they send, I will come as the answer — not as the one who heard.”* The churches have absorbed this instruction into their liturgy and continued more or less as before, on the theory — never stated quite this baldly — that a being who arrives when the desperate call his name is a god in every way that matters to the desperate, whatever he prefers to be called.

This unresolved argument is not trivia. It is the theological seam that runs through the entire organization. If Leo is a god, then the Lions are his church-militant and their mandate is whatever he wills. If Leo is a celestial — immensely powerful, immensely good, and *bounded*, a soldier of the Heavens rather than a sovereign of them — then the Lions are something more uncomfortable and more interesting: an alliance between mortals and a being who is himself under authority, fighting a war whose rules of engagement neither he nor they get to set. Leo has told them which it is. The difficulty is that some of them believe the answer is his humility rather than the truth.

The Heavens fight under self-imposed limits: there are things they will not do, lines they will not cross, methods they refuse regardless of outcome, and the knowledge that evil is willing to do what good is not sits in the Heavens like a wound that will not close. Leo carries the wound like the rest of the host. The Lions inherit it through him. What a given Lion does with that inheritance — accepts it as the price of being what they are, or concludes that mortals are not bound by the Heavens’ restraint and someone must do what the angels will not — is, more than anything else, what determines which side of the organization’s internal argument they end up on.

## Structure

The Lions are organized around a rank hierarchy shared between the two tracks, with different titles reflecting different functions within the same authority structure.

The **Zealot track** uses the language of martial service: Page, Squire, Knight, Veteran, Banneret, Commander, Champion.

The **Circuit Rider track** uses the language of traveling ministry: Novice, Acolyte, Rider, Veteran Rider — and then the same senior titles as the Zealot track, since no mortal has achieved Banneret standing on the Rider track and the Commander and Champion positions are held by celestial beings whose track is somewhat beside the point.

Authority within the shared hierarchy is genuine rather than ceremonial: a Veteran Rider commands the same deference from junior Zealots as a Veteran Knight does from junior Riders. In practice this produces friction between senior members of the two tracks, who have different instincts about most situations — friction the leadership considers healthy and the people experiencing it consider corrosive, which is probably the correct assessment of both.

**Roles are not ranks.** A *Sergeant* is a Zealot of any senior standing holding command of a formation; a *Chaplain-Quartermaster* manages a chapter’s church relationships and stores; a *Warden-Liaison* (the title predates the Frontier Wardens and annoys everyone) manages a chapter’s intelligence relationships. A Veteran serving as a formation Sergeant is addressed as Veteran-Sergeant; the hyphen carries a fine distinction that Lions notice and outsiders don’t.

**Joining.** Pages and Novices come from three sources in roughly equal measure: children of frontier communities sponsored by their Circuit Rider (the Rider’s sponsorship is a serious act; a Rider answers for those they send); adult converts, usually survivors of an incursion, whose applications are handled with a specific gentleness because the organization knows exactly what kind of night produces them; and church-raised candidates from the core regions, who arrive with the best doctrine and the longest adjustment. There is no birthright track. The child of a Veteran starts as a Page like anyone else, and the organization is quietly vigilant about keeping it that way.

**Discipline and leaving.** The Lions distinguish three exits, and the distinction is doctrine. *Release* is honorable — injury, age, or the simple truthful statement that the member can no longer carry the work; the released keep their standing, their welcome at any chapter, and a pension drawn from the church funds. *Censure* is corrective — suspension of duties, retraining, penance-circuit service under a senior Rider; most censured members return. *Severance* is the rarest thing the organization does, reserved for members who have violated the witness itself — who abandoned people they were bound to protect, falsified what they saw, or used the Lions’ name to do what the Lions exist to prevent. The severed are named in every chapter-house record, not for vengeance but because the organization’s credibility is the promise that its members are what the sigil says they are, and a promise requires an accounting of its exceptions. An unaffiliated former Lion is, almost always, one of the released. The severed are something communities learn to ask about very carefully.

**The mortal Banneret** — currently Cassien Vairo, a Knight by track, seated at Thirstgate since 301 SR — coordinates the operational deployment of both tracks across the material plane. The Banneret does not command the celestial Bannerets, who answer upward to the Commanders and ultimately to Leo, but works alongside them on situations requiring coordination between the mortal and celestial elements of the organization. Formally the celestials outrank the mortal; practically the mortal holds operational authority over the humanoid force that constitutes the Lions’ daily presence in the world; the gap between formal and practical authority is managed through relationship rather than protocol, which is to say it is managed exactly as well as the individuals involved.

## The Two Tracks

**Zealots** are the organization’s martial presence. Their training is intensive, physically demanding, and spiritually rigorous in ways that distinguish it from purely martial formation. A Zealot is not a soldier. A soldier fights for an employer. A Zealot fights because they have made a specific assessment of what the world requires of them and found that the assessment leaves no comfortable alternative.

The martial tradition draws from Leo’s own fighting style, filtered through six generations of practitioners adapting it to the threat environment the Lions actually face. Its core is stated in three clauses that every Page learns before they learn anything else: *close the distance, hold the line, and do not stop seeing.* The first two are tactics. The third is the tradition — the discipline of continuing to witness what you are fighting rather than retreating into the useful abstraction of combat, and of remembering, in the middle of a serious engagement, that the reason you are there is standing behind you, fully real and fully at stake.

The prayer tradition is what most distinguishes Zealot formations from secular fighting companies. Zealots pray — not as ritual obligation or morale practice but as genuine communication with the celestial beings whose aid they may need and whose nature they are, in some sense, trying to embody. Doctrine holds that prayer travels: that it rises through the Realm of Vapor, the boundary through which divine will enters the world as vision and premonition, and that the quality of the prayer — its honesty, its specificity, its acknowledgment of what the Zealot is actually experiencing rather than what they believe they should be experiencing — determines whether aid arrives, and in what form. Whether the doctrine is mechanically true is a question the Lions’ theologians argue and the Lions’ Veterans do not, because the Veterans have stood in the moments where it was tested.

This produces a culture of emotional honesty inside Zealot formations that outsiders consistently find surprising. A formation in which nobody admits to fear, doubt, or spiritual uncertainty is understood by senior members to be a formation with a problem — one whose prayer has gone performative, and whose access to aid has correspondingly degraded. Senior Zealots cultivate the conditions for honest acknowledgment of difficulty, not because they find weakness acceptable but because they have buried people who died of pretense.

**Circuit Riders** are the organization’s ministerial presence, its intelligence network, its community-relationship infrastructure, and the primary human face of the Lions for most people in most places. They travel — constantly, deliberately, on routes covering every community within the Lions’ mandate, which is effectively every community the church network can support.

A Rider on circuit knows their route the way a good shepherd knows a pasture: every community, every family, every pattern that constitutes normal, and every deviation from normal that might constitute warning. The intelligence this provides is the organization’s most valuable strategic asset and the one its enemies work hardest to disrupt. A community that has lost its Rider has lost something it rarely valued fully until it was gone — and has stopped providing the Lions with the early warning the Rider’s visits quietly generated.

The healing and support a Rider provides are partly practical and partly something rarer. The practical part is medicine, doctrine, news, and the settling of the small disputes that curdle into large ones. The rarer part is this: the Rider who comes back, who remembers what they found last time, who asks after the situation they heard about three visits ago, is providing the experience of being *seen and remembered* — which is, for many frontier communities, the scarcest commodity there is. This is not a technique. It is the witness tradition in its most ordinary clothes.

## Signs and Rites

The Lions’ sigil is **the Open Eye in the Mane** — a lion’s head, face-on, with a single unlidded eye at the center of the mane where the brow should be. Rendered in gold thread on the Zealot’s shoulder-cloak, stamped in the wax of a Rider’s letters, burned shallowly into the lintel of every funded church. Communities that cannot read know the sign. The sign is the promise.

**Dress** follows function and declares track. Zealots in the field wear grey riding leathers under a half-cloak of undyed wool — the cloak’s left shoulder carries the sigil, and its hem carries a strip of red only after the wearer’s first holding action, added by the formation’s Sergeant, never by the wearer. Riders wear no uniform at all — doctrine holds that a Rider should look like someone who belongs on the road, not like an institution arriving — but every Rider carries the *iron lantern*: small, battered by custom even when new, and lit at dusk in whatever community they’ve reached. A lit lantern outside a door means the Rider is within and may be approached at any hour for any reason. Frontier children learn the lantern before they learn the sigil.

**The Vigil** is the tradition’s central rite, held on the anniversary of the Long Night of Redoubt and after every serious engagement: the formation or chapter sits together through a full night, unarmed, and speaks aloud — one at a time, without interruption or response — what each of them actually saw. Not the tactical account, which is recorded separately and matters less. What it *was*. What it felt like. What they cannot stop seeing. The Vigil is where the emotional honesty of the formations is made and remade, and Lions who have sat one describe the difference between a formation that keeps the Vigil honestly and one that performs it as the difference between armor and paint.

**The dead** are witnessed, not eulogized. A fallen Lion’s name, and one true thing about them — chosen by whoever knew them best, and the choosing is itself a duty — is entered in the chapter’s Book of the Seen and read aloud at the next Vigil. The graves are plain. The Book is not.

**Speech.** Lions say *“I saw it”* where others say *I swear*; the phrase is oath-weight and is not used loosely. *“Holding”* is the all-purpose answer to *how are you* inside the organization, and means exactly what it says. A situation, or a person, that has passed beyond help is *“past the line”* — the phrase is quiet, final, and never said carelessly, because a Lion who says it has just told you what they intend to stop trying to save.

## The Funding Structure

The Lions are funded by churches, which means their operational capacity is inseparable from the health of the network that supports them.

The church network is not a single institution. It is a distributed collection of local congregations and regional associations, connected by shared veneration of the Heavens — of the Lantern-Keeper who guards the hearth, the Ledger who witnesses oaths, the Grey Shepherd who walks the dead home, and the rest of the host, in combinations that vary by region and by which powers a community has had reason to call on — and by Leo’s tradition riding across all of it. Most funding churches share the Lions’ commitment against the Shadow’s specific threats. Some fund the Lions because the Lions’ presence is worth the tithe on purely practical grounds. The distinction matters less to operations than the consistency of the coin.

What the churches receive is the Lions’ presence and attention: the Riders on circuit, the Zealots who answer, and the institutional depth that lets a single congregation’s warning summon a response no single congregation could mount. This is an exchange of value, not a charity, and both parties treat it as one — which gives both a standing to grieve when it fails.

The complications arise where church interests and Lions requirements diverge. A church whose funding flows from a Trade Company in conflict with another Company the Lions also serve communicates its discomfort in the ways institutions do. A regional association aligned with the harder doctrine funds with expectations attached — expectations about how the local chapter engages Arcanists, Houses, and Wardens — and the administrative officers who manage church relations navigate these pressures continuously and imperfectly, holding the funding that makes the organization viable while resisting the capture that would unmake its mandate. It is the least celebrated work in the Lions and, several Bannerets have observed, the work on which everything else stands.

## The Internal Argument

The Lions are not a monolith. They are an organization with a shared founding commitment and a genuine, generations-old disagreement about what that commitment requires — and the disagreement now has names.

The hard faction calls itself **the Unblinking**. The name is doctrine: witness means seeing *fully*, and the Unblinking hold that the organization’s mainline has trained itself to look at arcane magic, at the Houses’ pacts and altered lineages, at the Wardens’ friendships with the Wild’s sovereigns — and to blink. Their founding text is *On the Whole Sight*, by the Veteran Rider **Solomon Karsh**, still living, still writing, still received at any chapter with the wary courtesy owed to a brilliant man whose conclusions the leadership has spent thirty years declining to adopt. Karsh’s argument deserves stating fairly, because it is not stupid: arcane magic comes from somewhere inhuman and may come from a bargain whose price is unpaid; Contractor pacts bind mortals to entities including, documentably, the Shadow’s own architects of bargains; the Heavens fight under limits because they are the Heavens — *and mortals are not*. Someone, the Unblinking conclude, must be willing to see the whole threat and do what the angels will not. The wound in the Heavens, in their reading, is a mandate.

The mainline has no name for itself, which is itself a position — *the organization*, its members say, with a small stress that does the work of a manifesto. The Unblinking call them **the Half-Lidded**, and mean it as gently as it sounds. Outsiders increasingly say **the Vairine**, after the Banneret whose eleven years have defined the moderate position in practice: sustained working relationships with the Enclaves and the Guilds, formal correctness with the Houses, cautious and personal case-by-case judgment of the Wardens. The mainline’s answer to Karsh is also worth stating fairly: the Heavens’ limits are not squeamishness, they are the entire difference between the Heavens and the Shadow; a Lions that does what the angels will not is not a harder instrument of the good but a new recruit of the enemy, wearing the sigil; and the century of demonstrated coexistence — the Enclave graduates who have served frontier towns for lifetimes, the House lineages that have kept faith for nine generations — is evidence that whole sight, honestly practiced, sees *that* too.

The argument extends into everything. Into technology: if vim is truly a domesticated echo of the Realm of Electricity — the Heavens’ own boundary — then the mainline reads every Constructive Heart as a small standing grace, and the Unblinking read it as presumption, divinity milked into batteries by Tinkerers who never asked. Into the mandate itself: the Lions were founded against the Shadow, but the Frozen Coast chapters spend their winters against the *Nightmare*, a different enemy in a parallel war, and whether Nightmare-leakage falls within the mandate or beyond it is a live doctrinal dispute with real deployment consequences, currently decided chapter by chapter, which satisfies no one. And into history: the Kindling. The Unblinking’s institutional ancestors are the faction most plausibly behind the burning of the Candlevault a century ago, the organization has never resolved the question, and the Burning Library’s silent centennial observance in Thirstgate last year was received by the mainline as grief, by the Unblinking as accusation, and by Banneret Vairo — who ordered the Library’s people housed and provisioned for the night at the chapter’s expense — as both.

The practical result is regional. Where the mainline holds, working relationships with the other institutions function. Where the Unblinking are ascendant — parts of the Dust Lanes, several eastern church associations — Enclaves operate at distance, House envoys travel with guarantees, and Wardens simply route around. Individual Lions and individual members of every other institution maintain relationships that exist somewhat outside all of this; a Zealot and an Arcanist who have held a line together know something about each other that no doctrine fully captures, and the organization, wisely, has never tried to legislate that knowledge away.

## The Celestial Dimension

The Lions are a mortal organization with celestial leadership, and the join between the two is the most unusual structural fact about them.

The three Knight Commanders are celestials who chose this organization and its mission: **Uldrathax**, the Dragon Ascendant, called the Long Vigil, who has watched the Shadow’s frontiers since before Standard Reckoning and speaks perhaps twice a year, to effect; **Emeriel**, the Solar, called the Drawn Blade, the Commander who takes the field, whose arrivals are the events by which frontier communities date their histories; and **Sarvahl**, the Throne Archon, called the Standing Wall, who holds the Lions’ relationship to the Heavens’ wider order and whose fortress-logic shapes the organization’s doctrine of the held line. They have existed longer than the Lions’ recorded history. They have seen the war’s shape across spans no mortal archive reaches. And they cannot fully grasp the current operational texture of the material plane — the price of coal at Endline, the mood of a church association, which Company can be trusted this decade — which the mortal Banneret grasps completely and cannot see past. The relationship works when each party remembers that the other’s knowledge is genuine and bounded. It works poorly whenever either forgets.

Between them stand the celestial Bannerets — senior enough to coordinate major operations, present enough to understand current conditions, celestial enough to speak upward in ways no mortal can. **Knight Commander Selene Dayn** is, by long-standing local usage, misranked in her own honorific — she is a celestial Banneret, an Avoral Guardinal, and the frontier communities that began calling her Commander two generations ago have never been corrected because she has never cared. She maintains the most consistent visible material-plane presence of any celestial in the regions, her judgment is regarded as severe and fair in equal measure, and her working relationship with Vairo is generally considered the load-bearing joint of the entire mortal–celestial structure.

The prayer-answered aid that Veteran-level Lions can call in serious engagements is the celestial dimension at its most direct. The Lions’ archive preserves the accounts of these moments — not as records of celestial power but as records of what it *was like*, what the Veteran understood afterward that no preparation had supplied, and what the experience demanded of them in the engagement that followed. One such account, studied at every Acolyte’s elevation, is reproduced here.

> *From the account of Veteran Rider Ozanne Pell, recorded at Thirstgate three days after the Night at Cold Hollow, 271 SR. Entered in the open archive at her insistence.*
>
> They ask me to record the tactical circumstances, so: the Boneyard to the northeast bloomed early, the dead came with the dusk, and I had the chapel at Cold Hollow with forty-one souls inside it and the door as my line. I prayed. I want it written down that I prayed badly — no composure, no form, just the truth, which was that I was afraid, and that the door was oak and I was less than oak, and that forty-one is a number but the miller’s girl was pressed against my back and breathing and that was not a number.
>
> I expected a Hollyphant, if I expected anything. The lantern light changed instead. It did not brighten. It *steadied* — as if the light had been afraid too, all along, and had stopped.
>
> He did not arrive the way power arrives. He arrived the way attention arrives. He looked at every soul in that chapel, one at a time, the miller’s girl longest of all, and I understood — I am trying to be precise — that he was not counting them. I count them. It is my discipline to see them as fully real and I work at it the way you work at anything. He had never needed the work. He does not look at the dead the way I look at the dead, either. I flinch first, inside, and then make myself see. He has never needed the flinch.
>
> They tell me I should record the fighting. I cannot. It was not fighting as I know the word. It was very brief, and it was outside, and when it was done the night was only a night.
>
> He came back in and I knelt, and he said — and this is exact, I will swear it as exact — *“Get up. You held. I only ended it. You do not kneel to the one who arrives last.”*
>
> I have been a Rider for nineteen years and I have taught the witness to novices as a discipline, a practice, a thing mortals do on purpose because it does not come to us by nature. I still teach it that way. But I understand now what the discipline is *for*. It is not a virtue we invented. It is an imitation. There is a way of seeing that exists in the world whole and unlabored, and I stood next to it for the better part of an hour, and everything I have practiced for nineteen years is the shadow a candle throws of it.
>
> They ask me what he is. I do not know. I know what he is not: he is not far away. That is the thing I cannot make the young ones understand until it is their night.

There is one appearance the archive does not circulate. Leo came to the ruin of House Talvecq in 259 SR, and the account of what he found there is sealed by his own instruction — the only seal in the archive, and it has held for fifty-three years. Every senior Lion knows it exists. What it would mean for the organization if it were opened is a question senior Lions have learned not to ask each other, because the possible answers sort them into factions faster than any doctrine does.

## The Lions Among the Peoples

The Lions’ presence is felt differently depending on who is present and what has come before.

A Circuit Rider on their regular route is a familiar figure — expected, welcomed, part of the community’s rhythm, and a relationship with a *person*, not with the organization; a community whose Rider of twenty years is finally replaced experiences the replacement as bereavement, not administration. A Zealot patrol is a different register: respected, reassuring, and informative — Zealots asking questions mean something is being investigated; Zealots passing through fast mean the trouble is elsewhere, which is a comfort that never entirely feels like one. A major response — formations converging, a Veteran taking personal command — empties roads and fills fortified towns. A celestial arriving in visible form is a story a community tells for a generation, and the Lions manage those stories with care: the appearance is evidence that the situation warranted it, not evidence that the Lions command such things routinely, and the difference matters for what communities believe they can rely on.

Among the setting’s peoples, three relationships are distinct enough to name.

**The Korr** and the Lions are the two cultures in the world organized around witness, and they have been arguing about what the word means for a hundred years. The Korr elders hold that true witness requires height — the whole seen from above, uninvolved and therefore undeceived. The Lions hold that witness without the willingness to close the distance is spectation wearing a serious expression. The argument is conducted with complete mutual respect and no progress whatsoever, and meanwhile Korr are overrepresented on the Rider track beyond any other people — the young ones who leave the cliffs finding, in the circuit, a way to spend their short lives seeing *closely* that their elders cannot quite condemn. The most famous of them, the Rider **Sekka of the Fallen Ledge**, served thirty-one years — most of a Korr life — and her home cliff-city entered her name in its watch-records as *one who descended, and saw*. Among the Korr, this is either the highest compliment or a controversy, depending on the elder asked.

**The Dóm** courier tradition and the Rider tradition meet on the Frozen Coast, where they have negotiated, over generations and without ever writing it down, a working division: Dóm carry the words, Riders carry the witness. The Iceharbor chapter’s standing compact with the Long Slope courier lines means Lions intelligence crosses the north faster than anywhere else in the world, and a Dóm courier bearing the iron-lantern token of the Iceharbor chapter is owed shelter at any funded church — an obligation the churches honor and the Dóm, characteristically, have never once needed to invoke twice.

**The Nol** present the Lions with a fact the doctrine is still catching up to: the mask tradition works. Nol masks were made against the predators of the unseen world — against, in the terms the Lions now possess, the Nightmare’s hunters, which stalk by familiarity — and they demonstrably function. The Frozen Coast and Dust Lanes chapters have quietly recruited Nol for two generations; the Pale Riders under Kashiel are the visible edge of a broader pattern. The Unblinking are suspicious of protective traditions whose mechanism no theology explains. The chapters that winter against the Nightmare have stopped asking the theology to hurry.

## Notable Lions

**Banneret Cassien Vairo.** Human, Knight track, seated at Thirstgate since 301 SR — eleven years, the longest tenure in a century. The moderate position made flesh: working relationships with the Enclaves and Guilds, formal correctness with the Houses, case-by-case judgment of Wardens. Courteous, unhurried, and increasingly grey; the speculation about his health is now open enough that he has begun making jokes about it, which the people who know him best find less reassuring than he intends.

**Veteran Marcus Fadrick, called the Champion of the East.** The senior mortal Lion of the Dust Lanes operations, formation Sergeant of the Dustline chapters, and the Unblinking’s evident preferred successor to Vairo. The sobriquet is a provocation and everyone knows it: Champion is a rank, the rank is celestial, and the Unblinking bestowed the title on a mortal deliberately — a compact way of saying that mortals must rise to what the celestials hold in reserve. Fadrick has never used the title himself and never once disclaimed it, and the precision of that balance is the most informative fact about him. His record in the Boneyards is genuinely without stain. This is what makes him dangerous, and he knows that too.

**Veteran Rider Solomon Karsh.** Author of *On the Whole Sight*; the Unblinking’s mind, as Fadrick is its sword-arm. Old now, circuit-retired, resident at an eastern church association that funds accordingly. Receives visitors, answers letters, and has spent three decades being more persuasive in a sitting room than the mainline finds comfortable to admit.

**Knight Commander Selene Dayn.** Avoral Guardinal; celestial Banneret in fact, Commander by unstoppable local usage. The Lions’ most visible celestial and the working joint between Vairo’s operational authority and the Commanders’ war. Severe, fair, and possessed of the specific patience of a being who has watched mortal institutions rise and fall and has decided this one is worth her attention anyway.

**The Wandering Witness.** A Circuit Rider — Sister Thane, Sister Idris, or simply the Witness, depending on the community speaking — twenty-three years on the same circuit across the southern Iron Reach and the Hollow Marches, a route no other Rider has been willing to take before or since. She operates well outside normal protocol, and the hierarchy extends to her the careful attention reserved for members whose work is too valuable to constrain and too unconventional to endorse. The communities on her circuit do not debate her standing. They set a place for her.

**Brother Anselm of Endline.** An Acolyte with no formal seniority who has become the most consulted figure in the Salt Reach — the Company station chief, the Registry administrator, and the Gorûn route-elders all bring him questions of judgment, and the senior Lions of the region have noted his standing and chosen, so far, neither to formalize nor disturb it. The organization produces perhaps one Anselm a generation. What it does with them is usually decided too late.

## Current Fault Lines

- **The Succession.** Vairo is failing — slowly, and with dignity, and unmistakably. The Unblinking have their candidate in Fadrick, whose operational record makes him difficult to oppose on merit. The mainline has no candidate of comparable stature, and the names in circulation — a Verdant Belt Veteran of quiet reputation; Dayn herself, if the celestials would permit an unprecedented arrangement; even, in whispers nobody owns, the Wandering Witness — reveal the depth of the problem. Whoever is elevated inherits an organization whose factions have been rehearsing this argument for thirty years.

- **The Talvecq Seal.** Severin Talvecq has spent two decades investigating his family’s destruction, and his investigation has finally produced something: a formal petition, backed by House Ardenne’s guarantee, requesting the opening of the sealed account. The petition sits with Vairo. The seal is Leo’s. No mortal Banneret has ever asked Leo to reverse an instruction, and the organization does not know what it fears more — the asking, or the answer.

- **The Eastern Funding Ultimatum.** The church association funding the Dust Lanes reconstruction line — Karsh’s hosts — has attached conditions to next year’s tithe: harder doctrine on Enclave cooperation, formal review of the Nol recruitment, and a named voice on the regional deployment council. Refusing the conditions defunds Dustline. Accepting them arms the Unblinking with precedent. The administrative officers have bought one year of delay and know exactly how they bought it.

- **The Kindling, Reopened.** The Library’s centennial vigil in Thirstgate has been followed by something unprecedented: a quiet approach from Library seniors offering, for the first time in a hundred years, to share what their survivors recorded of the night the Candlevault burned — in exchange for reciprocal access to the Lions’ internal records of the same decade. The mainline sees the offer as the chance to close the organization’s oldest wound. The Unblinking see it as an archive fishing expedition by an institution with a grievance. Both are aware the records may vindicate no one.

- **The Nightmare Question.** The Frozen Coast chapters have formally requested a doctrinal ruling: is the Nightmare within the mandate? A ruling of yes commits the organization to a second war it is not funded to fight. A ruling of no abandons Iceharbor’s winters to local improvisation and Nol masks. The Commanders, who could settle it, have referred the question back to the mortal leadership — which the mortal leadership has correctly understood as a test, without being told of what.

- **The Rust Belt, Tabled Again.** The reclamation of the Rust Belt is on the Thirstgate agenda for the first time in a decade, tabled by Fadrick, whose argument is characteristically clean: the Lions have spent twenty years witnessing the Rust Belt and declining to act on what they saw. If witness does not eventually issue in action, what is it for? The mainline has not yet found the flaw in the question. There may not be one, which is a separate problem.

## The Lions in Play

**Joining and rising.** A Page or Novice character begins inside the organization’s slowest machinery: training circuits, chapel duties, the unglamorous escort work by which the Lions evaluate temperament before they evaluate anything else. Knight or Rider standing brings independent assignment — a circuit of one’s own, a formation posting — and the first real experience of the Lions’ defining condition, which is being the only institutional presence for fifty miles and having to decide what the sigil requires. Veteran standing brings the prayer-aid threshold, a voice in chapter councils, and the attention of both factions, who recruit at exactly this tier. The obligations are heavy at every level — the Lions hold the nearest thing to a total claim on their members of any faction — and the protections are correspondingly deep: any funded church is shelter, any chapter is backing, and the sigil is credit, witness, and warrant across every region the network reaches.

**What the Lions pay adventurers to do.** The organization is chronically over-mandated and under-staffed, and contracts work to capable outsiders constantly — through Chaplain-Quartermasters at chapter houses, through Riders who need more sword than their circuit carries, and through the Registry, with which the Lions maintain a standing account. Typical work, by tier:

- Escort a Circuit Rider through a stretch of route that has started eating couriers — and notice, as the Rider notices everything, what has changed along it.

- Hold a chapel, a rail station, or a town’s line through a Boneyard bloom until the nearest formation arrives; the Lions pay promptly and attend the Vigil of anyone who held.

- Investigate a community that has gone quiet between Rider visits — the assignments the Lions hate most and outsource most carefully.

- Carry the iron-lantern token across the Long Slope or the Gorûn routes when the intelligence cannot wait for the circuit.

- Retrieve what a severed former Lion has been doing with the sigil — quietly, with documentation, and alive if possible, because the Book of the Seen requires an accounting.

- Guard the Glass Plains monitoring post through a measurement season, in the company of House Vehk observers and one permanently unimpressed Veteran.

- Enter the Rust Belt with a Warden guide and answer, in writing, the only question Thirstgate is currently asking: *what is in there now?*

- At the highest tiers: carry a message to a Beast Lord under Winter Concession protocols; stand with a formation when Emeriel takes the field; or be in the room, in whatever capacity, when the Talvecq seal is finally addressed.

**Patrons.** *The Chaplain-Quartermaster* — every chapter has one; theirs is the ledger from which adventurers are paid and the judgment by which they are invited back. *A Rider on a hard circuit* — the Wandering Witness is the extreme case, but every region has a Rider whose route needs more protection than doctrine allots, and a party that earns a Rider’s trust has earned forty communities’ trust with it. *Knight Commander Dayn* — for parties that have proven themselves twice, the summons that means the situation is real; she pays in standing rather than Rings, and her standing is worth more.

A party need hold no Lions member at all to spend a campaign inside the Lions’ weather. In most regions, they *are* the weather.

## Player Toolkit

A Lions character should name their home chapter or circuit, sponsor, rank, and the community whose faces prevent “the mission” from becoming an abstraction. Decide which track trained you even if your current work crosses both: martial formation, traveling ministry, intelligence, logistics, or pastoral care. Describe how you wear the Open Eye in the Mane and the honest fear or doubt your formation has already heard you name aloud.

A Witness should define their Spark of Faith through practice rather than creed alone: what does prayer look like under pressure, what form does grace take, and what sacrifice are they too ready to offer? Name a celestial teaching you accept, one doctrinal interpretation you question, and a person outside the Lions whose moral judgment you trust.

The organization’s roles remain part of the fiction even though **Witness** is the player class: Zealot and Circuit Rider describe service, posting, and training, not separate mechanical classes. A Witness may have served either role or moved between them.

## GM Toolkit

The Lions can provide sanctuary, trusted couriers, trained formations, community intelligence, moral authority, and access to celestial counsel. They cannot answer every call, command celestials as assets, remain neutral about visible suffering, or intervene without changing the political weather.

Make prayer specific. Aid should answer the person and situation addressed, sometimes through an obligation rather than spectacle. A Sacrifice must be chosen and meaningful; do not use it to remove player agency or demand harm unrelated to the prayer. Show the Lions at their best through attention, logistics, and return visits, not only battle.

**GM questions:** Who has the Lions witnessed but not helped? Which mandate exceeds this chapter’s resources? What honest admission would change the formation’s next decision?
